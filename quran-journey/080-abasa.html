The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Abasa was revealed in Makkah and carries one of the most deeply personal corrections in the entire Quran. The name "Abasa" means "He Frowned," referring to an incident where the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) unintentionally frowned and turned away from a blind companion who came seeking guidance.

The companion was Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum, a blind man who came eagerly to learn about Islam. At that moment, the Prophet (peace be upon him) was engaged in a conversation with some of the leaders of Quraysh, hoping to win them over to Islam. When Abdullah interrupted, the Prophet frowned and turned away, not out of arrogance, but because he was focused on what he thought was a more important opportunity for da'wah.

Ibn Kathir narrates that Allah revealed these verses to gently correct the Prophet (peace be upon him), teaching a universal lesson: in Allah's sight, the sincere seeker of truth is more valuable than a hundred arrogant elite. The blind man who came with a thirsty heart deserved more attention than wealthy leaders who had no intention of accepting the truth. Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum later became so honored that the Prophet appointed him as the mu'adhin (caller to prayer) of Madinah and left him in charge of the city multiple times when he went on military expeditions.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Introduction to Surah Abasa; At-Tirmidhi; Sahih Muslim

After the opening correction, the surah transitions to describe the noble nature of the Quran as a reminder for anyone who wishes to take heed. It then shifts to a powerful reflection on Allah's blessings in creation: how He brings food from the earth through rain, growing grains, grapes, vegetables, olives, palms, gardens, fruits, and grass, all as provision for humans and their livestock.

The surah concludes with a terrifying description of the Day of Judgment: the Deafening Blast, when a person will flee from their own brother, mother, father, spouse, and children. On that Day, every person will be consumed by their own fate. The faces of the believers will be bright and laughing, while the faces of the disbelievers will be covered with dust and darkness.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
عَبَسَ وَتَوَلَّىٰ
Abasa wa tawalla
He frowned and turned away.

Word by Word

عَبَسَabasaHe frowned
وَتَوَلَّىٰwa tawallaAnd turned away
Simple Explanation

The surah opens in the third person, not addressing the Prophet (peace be upon him) directly as "you," but saying "he." Ibn Kathir explains that this is a form of gentle reproof, using indirect speech to soften the correction. "Abasa" means to frown or show displeasure on the face, and "tawalla" means to turn away physically. The Prophet (peace be upon him) was speaking with the chiefs of Quraysh when a blind man came seeking knowledge of Islam. As-Sa'di notes that Allah used the third person out of respect for His Prophet, even while correcting him, showing us how to give advice with dignity and gentleness.

2

Because there came to him the blind man.

Word by Word

جَاءَهُja'ahuCame to him
الْأَعْمَىٰal-a'maThe blind man
Simple Explanation

The reason for the frown: a blind man came to him. This was Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum, a cousin of Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her). Ibn Kathir explains that Abdullah could not see the Prophet's facial expression, so the frown did not hurt him directly, but Allah still corrected it because the inner attitude matters. As-Sa'di points out something profound: Allah describes him as "al-a'ma" (the blind one) not to belittle him, but to make the point stronger. This man was blind, he could not see, he had a genuine excuse for interrupting, and his blindness made his eagerness to learn even more admirable.

Now Allah shifts to direct address: "What would make you know?" This is a rhetorical question meant to awaken reflection. "Yazzakka" means to purify oneself, to grow spiritually, to become a better person. Ibn Kathir explains that the blind man might have been on the verge of a spiritual breakthrough. Perhaps this one teaching session was what he needed to purify his soul. As-Sa'di adds that we can never judge a person's spiritual potential by their outward appearance. The blind beggar might be closer to Allah than the richest chief of Quraysh. Only Allah knows the hearts.

Simple Explanation

Two possibilities are presented: either the blind man would be spiritually purified, or he would receive a reminder that benefits him. "Dhikra" means a reminder that stays in the heart and changes behavior. Ibn Kathir explains that both outcomes are immensely valuable. Even if a person does not reach full purification, being reminded of truth is itself a great benefit. As-Sa'di notes that Allah is highlighting the two greatest outcomes of teaching: purification of the soul (tazkiyah) and beneficial remembrance (dhikra). Both were possible for the blind man, and turning away from him meant potentially losing both.

Now Allah contrasts the two types of people. "Istaghna" means to consider oneself free of need, self-sufficient, not requiring guidance or anyone's help. Ibn Kathir explains this refers to the wealthy leaders of Quraysh whom the Prophet was addressing. They were rich, powerful, and arrogant, thinking they had no need for anyone, not even Allah. As-Sa'di notes that "istighnaa" (feeling self-sufficient) is one of the most dangerous spiritual diseases. When a person thinks they don't need guidance, they close the door to all growth and salvation.

"Tasadda" means to turn toward someone, to give them your full attention and effort. Ibn Kathir explains that the Prophet (peace be upon him) was devoting his attention to the Quraysh leaders because he hoped they would accept Islam, and their acceptance could influence entire tribes. His intention was noble, but his prioritization was being corrected. As-Sa'di points out that the Prophet was not doing anything sinful; he was doing da'wah. But Allah was teaching him, and through him all of us, that the sincere seeker deserves more priority than the arrogant elite, regardless of worldly status.

7
وَمَا عَلَيْكَ أَلَّا يَزَّكَّىٰ

Simple Explanation

A powerful relief for the Prophet (peace be upon him): you are not responsible if the arrogant person refuses to be purified. Ibn Kathir explains that this verse lifts the burden of results from the Prophet. His job is to deliver the message, not to force people to accept it. If the Quraysh leaders choose to reject, that is their loss, not his failure. As-Sa'di notes that this is a fundamental principle of da'wah: the caller to truth is responsible for effort, not outcomes. You do your best to convey the message, and the rest is in Allah's hands. Do not exhaust yourself chasing those who show no interest.

"Yas'a" means to rush, to strive, to come with eagerness and effort. This blind man did not casually drop by; he came striving, making effort despite his disability. Ibn Kathir explains that Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum walked through the streets of Makkah, blind and without a guide, driven purely by his thirst for knowledge and guidance. As-Sa'di highlights the beauty of this contrast: the wealthy leaders had everything but no desire for truth, while the blind man had nothing yet was desperate for it. Allah valued the striving heart over the comfortable body.

9
وَهُوَ يَخْشَىٰ
Wa huwa yakhsha
And he fears [Allah].

Word by Word

وَهُوَwa huwaAnd he
يَخْشَىٰyakhshaFears (Allah)
Simple Explanation

"Yakhsha" is a deeper form of fear, a reverential awe mixed with love and knowledge. It is the fear that comes from knowing Allah's greatness. Ibn Kathir explains that Abdullah came not just seeking knowledge, but seeking it out of fear of Allah, wanting to know the right path so he could follow it. As-Sa'di distinguishes between two types of fear: "khawf" (general fear) and "khashyah" (fear born from knowledge and reverence). Abdullah had khashyah. He feared Allah and wanted to learn more so he could worship properly. This is the highest motivation for seeking knowledge.

From him you are distracted.

Word by Word

فَأَنتَfa-antaThen you

Simple Explanation

"Talahha" means to be distracted, to occupy yourself with something else, to be diverted from what matters more. Ibn Kathir explains that this verse completes the contrast: while the sincere seeker came striving with fear of Allah, you turned away from him to give attention to those who had no interest. As-Sa'di notes that this is the culmination of the correction. Allah is saying: this man came with eagerness and God-consciousness, but you were distracted from him by those who think themselves self-sufficient. After this, the Prophet (peace be upon him) never repeated this mistake, and he would honor Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum greatly, saying: "Welcome to the one on whose account my Lord corrected me."

"Kalla" is a strong word of rebuke and emphasis meaning "No!" or "Never let this happen again!" Then comes a shift in topic. "Innaha tadhkirah" means "indeed this is a reminder," referring to the Quran itself. Ibn Kathir explains that the Quran is a reminder, a source of guidance and light for anyone who wants it. As-Sa'di notes that the Quran is described as a "tadhkirah" (reminder) because it reminds people of what their fitrah (natural disposition) already knows. Deep down, every human soul recognizes truth. The Quran simply awakens what is already there.

The Quran is available for anyone who wishes to benefit from it. "Man sha'a" means whoever wills, whoever chooses. This verse establishes free will in accepting guidance. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah does not force anyone to accept the reminder. The Quran is there, open and available, but the choice to accept it lies with each individual. As-Sa'di adds that this also connects to the earlier correction: since the Quran is for whoever wants it, the eager blind man who wanted it deserved attention more than the arrogant chiefs who did not.

This Quran is recorded in honored sheets. "Suhuf" are pages or scrolls, and "mukarramah" means honored, dignified, elevated in status. As-Sa'di notes that describing the sheets as "honored" is to show the immense value of what they contain. When the container is honored, it tells you how precious the content is. The Quran is so sacred that even the pages it is written on are dignified.

Two more attributes of the Quran's pages: "marfu'ah" means raised high in status and rank, and "mutahharah" means purified from any imperfection, falsehood, or corruption. Ibn Kathir explains that these sheets are elevated above all other scriptures because they contain the final, uncorrupted Word of Allah. "Mutahharah" means no impurity touches them, neither physical impurity nor the impurity of falsehood. As-Sa'di adds that the Quran is purified from any error, contradiction, or alteration. It remains exactly as Allah intended it, protected in the heavens and protected on earth.

"Safarah" comes from "safir" meaning ambassador, messenger, or scribe. These are the noble angels who carry and transmit the Quran. Ibn Kathir explains that these are the angels who serve as intermediaries, carrying the revelation from the Preserved Tablet to the Prophet through Jibreel (Gabriel), peace be upon him. As-Sa'di identifies them as the angels responsible for writing and delivering the divine scriptures, noble beings who are entrusted with the most precious cargo in existence: the Word of Allah.

16
كِرَامٍ بَرَرَةٍ

Word by Word

Two qualities of these angel-scribes: "kiram" (noble, generous, honorable) and "bararah" (righteous, dutiful, obedient). As-Sa'di notes that Allah describes the carriers of His word to emphasize the chain of honor: the message is from Allah (the Most Honored), written on honored pages, carried by honored angels. Every link in this chain is pure and noble. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "The one who recites the Quran skillfully will be with these noble, dutiful angels" (Sahih Bukhari and Muslim).

A powerful exclamation of astonishment. "Qutila" literally means "may he be destroyed" or "cursed be he" and is an Arabic expression of severe rebuke. Ibn Kathir explains that this is an expression of wonder at human ingratitude: despite all the blessings Allah has given, the human being still denies and disbelieves. As-Sa'di notes that "kufr" here carries both meanings: disbelief in Allah AND ingratitude for His favors. The human being was created from nothing, given everything, yet turns away from his Creator. What astonishing ingratitude!

As-Sa'di notes that this is a method of argumentation: first express astonishment at ingratitude, then remind the ungrateful person of their humble beginning to break their arrogance. How can you be arrogant when you know what you were made from?

From a worthless drop, Allah created a being of immense complexity and then wrote out his entire life. Yet this being has the audacity to be ungrateful?

From the moment of birth to the moment of death, Allah has made the path accessible. The human has no excuse for going astray when the road has been lit so clearly.

After life comes death, and after death comes burial. "Amatahu" means He caused him to die, and "aqbarahu" means He made a grave for him, He arranged for him to be buried with dignity. This is a blessing most people never think about: the dignity of burial is a gift from Allah.

The variety shows Allah's generosity: He did not give us just one type of food, but endless variety.

29
وَزَيْتُونًا وَنَخْلًا
Wa zaytuman wa nakhla
And olive and palm trees.

Word by Word

وَزَيْتُونًاwa zaytunaAnd olive (trees)
وَنَخْلًاwa nakhlaAnd palm trees
Simple Explanation

Olives and date palms, two of the most blessed trees in Islamic tradition. Ibn Kathir explains that the olive tree is uniquely beneficial: its fruit is eaten, its oil is used for cooking, lighting, and medicine, and it grows in rocky, difficult terrain where other trees cannot survive. The date palm (nakhla) is called the "sister of the believer" because every part of it is useful. As-Sa'di notes that both of these trees provide sustenance that lasts: olive oil and dates can be stored for long periods, providing reliable nutrition. Allah is showing the range of His provision: grains for daily meals, fruits for enjoyment, and oil and dates for lasting sustenance.

30
وَحَدَائِقَ غُلْبًا
Wa hada'iqa ghulba
And lush gardens.

Word by Word

وَحَدَائِقَwa hada'iqaAnd gardens
غُلْبًاghulbaLush / dense / thick
Simple Explanation

"Hada'iq" are enclosed gardens, orchards with walls, and "ghulba" means dense, thick, lush, with tall and strong trees. Ibn Kathir explains that these are magnificent gardens with towering trees and abundant fruit, the kind of gardens that fill a person with awe when they enter them. As-Sa'di notes that "ghulb" specifically refers to trees that are tall, thick-trunked, and full of foliage. These are not small shrubs but massive, established gardens that only Allah could produce. The word evokes images of thick forests and dense orchards overflowing with life and provision.

31
وَفَاكِهَةً وَأَبًّا
Wa fakihatan wa abba
And fruit and grass.

Word by Word

وَفَاكِهَةًwa fakihatanAnd fruit
وَأَبًّاwa abbaAnd grass / pasture
Simple Explanation

"Fakihah" means fruits in general, all kinds of fruits that humans enjoy. "Abb" refers to pasture, grass, and herbage that livestock graze on. Ibn Kathir explains that "fakihah" covers every type of fruit people eat for pleasure and nutrition, while "abb" is the provision Allah makes for animals. As-Sa'di notes that this pairing is deliberate: fruit for humans and grass for their animals. Allah provides not just for you but for the creatures that serve you. The entire food chain, from the rain that falls to the fruit you eat and the grass your livestock feeds on, is orchestrated by Allah.

32
مَّتَاعًا لَّكُمْ وَلِأَنْعَامِكُمْ
Mata'an lakum wa li-an'amikum
[As] enjoyment for you and your grazing livestock.

Word by Word

مَّتَاعًاmata'anAs enjoyment / provision
لَّكُمْlakumFor you
وَلِأَنْعَامِكُمْwa li-an'amikumAnd for your livestock
Simple Explanation

All of this, every rain drop, every split in the earth, every grain, grape, olive, date, garden, fruit, and blade of grass, is "mata'" (provision and enjoyment) for you and your livestock. Ibn Kathir explains that this verse summarizes the entire passage: everything mentioned from verse 24 to here is Allah's gift to creation. As-Sa'di notes that "mata'" implies both necessity and enjoyment. Food is not just sustenance; it is also pleasure. Allah could have made nutrition tasteless, but He made it delicious. He could have made everything one color, but He made it beautiful. This generosity demands gratitude, not ingratitude.

33
فَإِذَا جَاءَتِ الصَّاخَّةُ
Fa-idha ja'atis-sakhkhah
But when there comes the Deafening Blast.

Word by Word

فَإِذَاfa-idhaBut when
جَاءَتِja'atThere comes
الصَّاخَّةُas-sakhkhahThe Deafening Blast
Simple Explanation

A sudden, dramatic shift from blessings to terror. "As-Sakhkhah" is one of the names of the Day of Judgment, meaning the Deafening Blast, a sound so loud and terrifying that it overwhelms the ears. Ibn Kathir explains that "sakhkhah" comes from "sakhkha" meaning to deafen, to pierce the ears with an unbearable sound. This is the trumpet blast that announces the Day of Judgment. As-Sa'di notes the powerful contrast: one moment we were talking about peaceful gardens and delicious food, and now suddenly comes the Deafening Blast that ends everything. This is exactly how the Day of Judgment will come: suddenly, without warning, while people are enjoying their worldly blessings.

34
يَوْمَ يَفِرُّ الْمَرْءُ مِنْ أَخِيهِ
Yawma yafirrul-mar'u min akhih
On the Day a man will flee from his brother.

Word by Word

يَوْمَyawmaOn the Day
يَفِرُّyafirruWill flee
الْمَرْءُal-mar'uThe person / man
مِنْminFrom
أَخِيهِakhihiHis brother
Simple Explanation

One of the most heart-wrenching verses in the Quran. On that Day, a person will flee from his own brother. "Yafirru" means to run away in terror, to flee. Ibn Kathir explains that the terror of the Day of Judgment will be so overwhelming that the closest bonds of blood and love will mean nothing. A person will not even think about their sibling; they will be entirely consumed by their own fate. As-Sa'di notes that the surah lists family members in ascending order of closeness, starting with the brother, to show that even the deepest bonds will be severed by the sheer terror of that Day.

35
وَأُمِّهِ وَأَبِيهِ
Wa ummihi wa abih
And his mother and his father.

Word by Word

وَأُمِّهِwa ummihiAnd his mother
وَأَبِيهِwa abihiAnd his father
Simple Explanation

The escalation continues: he will flee from his mother and father, the two people who brought him into the world, who raised him, who sacrificed everything for him. Ibn Kathir explains that the mother is mentioned before the father to emphasize the intensity. The mother's bond with her child is the strongest natural bond in creation, yet even this bond will be severed on that Day. As-Sa'di notes that a person flees from their parents not out of hatred, but out of pure, overwhelming self-concern. Every person will be thinking: "Nafsi, nafsi!" (Myself, myself!). The terror is so great that love itself is forgotten.

36
وَصَاحِبَتِهِ وَبَنِيهِ
Wa sahibatihi wa banih
And his wife and his children.

Word by Word

وَصَاحِبَتِهِwa sahibatihiAnd his wife / companion
وَبَنِيهِwa banihiAnd his children
Simple Explanation

The climax of the list: his wife (sahiba, literally his life companion) and his children (banihi). These are the people a person would normally die for, the people closest to their heart. Ibn Kathir explains that the word "sahiba" means companion, the one who shares your life. And "banihi" are his own children, the ones he worked his entire life to provide for. Yet on this Day, he will run from all of them. As-Sa'di concludes that if a person flees from their own children, it means their fear has reached the absolute maximum. There is nothing in this world stronger than a parent's love for their child, yet even this is erased by the terror of the Day of Judgment.

37
لِكُلِّ امْرِئٍ مِّنْهُمْ يَوْمَئِذٍ شَأْنٌ يُغْنِيهِ
Likulli-mri'in minhum yawma'idhin sha'nun yughnih
For every man, that Day, will be a matter adequate for him.

Word by Word

لِكُلِّlikulliFor every
امْرِئٍimri'inPerson
مِّنْهُمْminhumAmong them
يَوْمَئِذٍyawma'idhinOn that Day
شَأْنٌsha'nunA matter / concern
يُغْنِيهِyughnihiSufficient for him / consuming him
Simple Explanation

The explanation for why people flee from their families: every person will have enough to worry about on their own. "Sha'n" means a matter, a concern, and "yughnihi" means it will be more than enough for him, it will completely consume him. Ibn Kathir explains that each person's own sins, fears, and accountability will be so overwhelming that they have zero capacity to think about anyone else. As-Sa'di notes that this is the ultimate loneliness. In this world, we depend on family, friends, and community. But on that Day, every person stands alone before Allah. No one can help anyone else. Your deeds are your only companion.

38
وُجُوهٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ مُّسْفِرَةٌ
Wujuhun yawma'idhin musfirah
[Some] faces, that Day, will be bright.

Word by Word

وُجُوهٌwujuhunFaces
يَوْمَئِذٍyawma'idhinOn that Day
مُّسْفِرَةٌmusfirahBright / radiant
Simple Explanation

After the terrifying scene, a ray of hope. "Musfirah" means bright, luminous, radiant, like the face of dawn breaking through darkness. Ibn Kathir explains that these are the faces of the believers, shining with the light of their faith and good deeds. Their faces reflect the joy and relief of knowing they are saved. As-Sa'di notes that "isfar" is the word used for dawn breaking, when light overcomes darkness. The believers' faces on that Day will literally glow with light and happiness, a physical manifestation of the spiritual light they carried in their hearts during their worldly life.

39
ضَاحِكَةٌ مُّسْتَبْشِرَةٌ
Dahikatun mustabshirah
Laughing, rejoicing at good news.

Word by Word

ضَاحِكَةٌdahikatunLaughing
مُّسْتَبْشِرَةٌmustabshirahRejoicing at good news
Simple Explanation

Two beautiful descriptions: laughing and rejoicing. "Dahikah" means genuinely laughing with joy, and "mustabshirah" means receiving and celebrating good news. Ibn Kathir explains that these believers are laughing because they see the reward that awaits them and they are overjoyed at the news of Paradise. After years of patience, sacrifice, and worship in the world, they finally see the payoff. As-Sa'di notes the contrast with the previous verses: while others are fleeing in terror, these believers are laughing and celebrating. The same Day that is the worst for some is the best for others. Your experience of that Day depends entirely on what you did in this world.

40
وَوُجُوهٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ عَلَيْهَا غَبَرَةٌ
Wa wujuhun yawma'idhin 'alayha gharabah
And [other] faces, that Day, will have upon them dust.

Word by Word

وَوُجُوهٌwa wujuhunAnd faces
يَوْمَئِذٍyawma'idhinOn that Day
عَلَيْهَا'alayhaUpon them
غَبَرَةٌgharabahDust
Simple Explanation

The second group: faces covered with dust. "Gharabah" means dust, dirt, a layer of grime. Ibn Kathir explains that these faces are covered with the dust of humiliation and shame. While the believers' faces are radiant, these faces are darkened and dirtied. As-Sa'di notes that "gharabah" is the dust that settles on something neglected or destroyed. These faces look like the faces of someone who has suffered a catastrophic defeat. And indeed, they have: they lost the greatest gamble of all, they bet their eternal life on this temporary world and lost everything.

41
تَرْهَقُهَا قَتَرَةٌ
Tarhaquha qatarah
Covered by darkness.

Word by Word

تَرْهَقُهَاtarhaquhaCovered / overwhelmed by
قَتَرَةٌqatarahDarkness / gloom
Simple Explanation

On top of the dust, darkness. "Tarhaquha" means to overwhelm, to cover completely, and "qatarah" means darkness, blackness, gloom. Ibn Kathir explains that these faces have a double covering: dust (gharabah) from humiliation, and darkness (qatarah) from despair. The dust represents shame, and the darkness represents hopelessness. Together they paint a picture of utter devastation. As-Sa'di notes that while the believers' faces are bright (light upon light), the disbelievers' faces are dust upon darkness. The outward appearance reflects the inward reality: their hearts were dark in this life, so their faces will be dark on that Day.

42
أُولَـٰئِكَ هُمُ الْكَفَرَةُ الْفَجَرَةُ
Ula'ika humul-kafaratul-fajarah
Those are the disbelievers, the wicked ones.

Word by Word

أُولَـٰئِكَula'ikaThose
هُمُhumuThey are
الْكَفَرَةُal-kafarahThe disbelievers
الْفَجَرَةُal-fajarahThe wicked ones
Simple Explanation

The final verse identifies them with two devastating labels: "al-kafarah" (the disbelievers) and "al-fajarah" (the wicked, immoral ones). Ibn Kathir explains that "kufr" is disbelief in the heart and "fujur" is wickedness in action. These two go together: when a person rejects truth internally, their external behavior becomes corrupt. As-Sa'di notes that the surah ends by naming the disease: disbelief combined with wickedness. They disbelieved in Allah's signs and lived wicked lives as a consequence. The bright faces belong to the believers who believed and did good; the dark faces belong to those who disbelieved and did evil. The surah began with a gentle correction about valuing sincere seekers and ends with the ultimate consequence of rejecting truth. May Allah make us among those with bright, laughing faces on that Day.

Key Lessons
  • 01
    Sincerity outweighs status. Allah corrected His own Prophet for prioritizing wealthy leaders over a sincere, humble seeker. In Allah's sight, the person who comes with a thirsty heart is more valuable than a hundred arrogant elite, regardless of wealth, power, or social standing.
  • 02
    Never underestimate anyone's spiritual potential. The blind man might have been the one whose heart was ready for purification. We cannot judge people's spiritual worth by their outward appearance, disability, or social status. Only Allah knows the hearts.
  • 03
    The Quran is a reminder for whoever wants it. Allah did not make the Quran exclusive to any tribe, race, or class. It is open to anyone who sincerely seeks it. The only requirement is a willing heart.
  • 04
    Reflect on the blessings in your food. The detailed description of the food chain (rain, soil splitting, grains, fruits, gardens) is an invitation to see Allah's hand in every meal. Gratitude begins with reflection.
  • 05
    The Day of Judgment will sever all worldly bonds. On that Day, a person will flee from their own brother, parents, spouse, and children. The only thing that will matter is your relationship with Allah and your deeds. Invest in what lasts.
  • 06
    Your face on Judgment Day reflects your heart in this life. Bright faces for the believers, dark faces for the disbelievers. What you cultivate inside today will be displayed on your face tomorrow. Fill your heart with faith, and your face will shine with light.
