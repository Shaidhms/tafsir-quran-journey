The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Buruj was revealed in Makkah during a period when the early Muslims were facing severe persecution from the Quraysh. The surah tells the story of the People of the Ditch (Ashab al-Ukhdud), believers from a previous nation who were burned alive in trenches of fire simply because they believed in Allah. This story served as a powerful source of comfort and strength for the persecuted Muslims in Makkah.

The name "Al-Buruj" means "the great stars" or "the constellations." Allah opens the surah by swearing by the sky full of constellations, the promised Day of Judgment, and the witness and the witnessed. These grand oaths set the stage for a story of ultimate sacrifice and divine justice.

The Prophet (peace be upon him) narrated the full story in a famous hadith recorded in Sahih Muslim. A king had a sorcerer who, when he grew old, asked the king for a boy to train. The boy instead learned from a monk and became a believer in Allah. When the king discovered this, he tried to kill the boy multiple times but failed. Finally, the boy told the king: "You will not be able to kill me until you gather the people, tie me to a trunk, take an arrow from my quiver, and say 'In the name of Allah, the Lord of the boy.' Then shoot me." The king did exactly that, and the boy died. But the people all declared: "We believe in the Lord of the boy!" The furious king then ordered trenches to be dug, filled with fire, and threw the believers in. A woman with her baby hesitated at the edge, and the baby spoke: "O mother, be patient, for you are upon the truth."

Source: Sahih Muslim, Hadith 3005 (Kitab az-Zuhd)

The surah's message is clear: persecution of believers is a recurring pattern throughout history, but Allah's justice is certain. Those who torture believers and do not repent will face the punishment of the Burning Fire, while the believers who remained steadfast will have Gardens beneath which rivers flow. The surah then shifts to describe Allah's magnificent attributes, His absolute power, and ends by affirming the Quran's place in the Preserved Tablet (Lawh al-Mahfuz).

For the early Muslims being tortured in Makkah, hearing about believers who chose death over disbelief was both a consolation and an inspiration. It taught them that their suffering was not meaningless, that Allah was watching, and that ultimate victory belongs to the believers.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَالسَّمَاءِ ذَاتِ الْبُرُوجِ
Was-sama'i dhatil-buruj
By the sky containing great stars.

Word by Word

وَwaBy (oath)
السَّمَاءِas-sama'iThe sky
ذَاتِdhatiPossessor of
الْبُرُوجِal-burujThe great stars / constellations
Simple Explanation

Allah opens with a grand oath by the sky that contains "al-buruj." Ibn Kathir explains that "buruj" refers to the great stars and constellations that adorn the night sky. The word comes from "baraja" meaning to appear prominently or tower above. These constellations are like grand towers in the sky. As-Sa'di adds that this oath draws attention to Allah's magnificent creation, the vastness of the universe, and the precision with which the stars are placed. The grandeur of this oath sets the tone for the serious subject that follows.

2
وَالْيَوْمِ الْمَوْعُودِ
Wal-yawmil-maw'ud
And [by] the promised Day.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd (oath)
الْيَوْمِal-yawmiThe Day
الْمَوْعُودِal-maw'udThe promised
Simple Explanation

The second oath is by "the promised Day," which is the Day of Judgment. Ibn Kathir states that this is the Day that Allah has promised all of creation, when every soul will be brought to account. As-Sa'di explains that calling it "the promised Day" emphasizes its certainty. It is not a possibility or a maybe. It is a promise from Allah, and Allah never breaks His promise. This oath reminds both the oppressors and the oppressed that a Day of perfect justice is coming.

3
وَشَاهِدٍ وَمَشْهُودٍ
Wa shahidin wa mashhud
And [by] the witness and what is witnessed.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd (oath)
شَاهِدٍshahidinA witness
وَwaAnd
مَشْهُودٍmashhudWhat is witnessed
Simple Explanation

Scholars have offered several interpretations. Ibn Kathir narrates from Abu Hurayrah that the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "The witnessed day is the Day of Arafah, and the witness day is Friday" (reported by At-Tirmidhi and Ahmad). Other scholars say the "witness" refers to the Day of Judgment itself (when all of creation gathers to witness the reckoning), or that Allah is the witness over all things, or that the Prophet (peace be upon him) is the witness over his Ummah. The beauty of the Quranic language is that it can encompass all these meanings simultaneously.

4
قُتِلَ أَصْحَابُ الْأُخْدُودِ

Cursed were the companions of the trench.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

Now comes the answer to the oaths. "Qutila" here is used as a curse or condemnation, meaning "may they be destroyed" or "cursed were they." The "Ashab al-Ukhdud" are the people of the trench or ditch. Ibn Kathir explains that these were tyrants who dug deep trenches, filled them with fire, and threw believers in. The word "ukhdud" means a long trench dug in the ground. This refers to the historical event narrated by the Prophet in Sahih Muslim about a tyrannical king who persecuted believers for their faith.

5

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

The trench was filled with "an-nar dhat al-waqud," a fire full of fuel, meaning it was a massive, intensely burning blaze. The tyrants did not just light a small fire. They fueled it until it was an inferno, specifically designed to torture and kill the believers. As-Sa'di notes that mentioning the abundance of fuel emphasizes the horrific nature of this crime and the extremity of the persecution. The scene described is one of pure evil: a burning pit prepared for innocent people whose only "crime" was belief in Allah.

6
إِذْ هُمْ عَلَيْهَا قُعُودٌ

Simple Explanation

The tyrants were sitting around the edge of the trench, watching. They sat comfortably while believers burned. Ibn Kathir points out the chilling detail: they were not forced to be there, they were not in a hurry. They sat and watched, as if this were entertainment. This describes a level of cruelty and dehumanization that is hard to fathom. They had so thoroughly dehumanized the believers that watching them burn alive was something they could sit through calmly. This detail amplifies the horror of their crime and the certainty of their punishment.

7

They deliberately, knowingly, and willingly tortured believers. As-Sa'di explains that their own witnessing of their crime will be used as evidence against them on the Day of Judgment. They cannot deny it because they themselves watched it happen. The word "shuhud" (witnesses) connects back to verse 3, where Allah swears by "the witness and the witnessed." They will be witnesses against themselves.

8

Wa ma naqamu minhum illa an yu'minu billahil-'azizil-hamid
And they resented them not except because they believed in Allah, the Exalted in Might, the Praiseworthy.

This verse reveals the "crime" of the believers: they believed in Allah. That was it. They committed no crime, no offense, no injustice. Their only "fault" was faith. Ibn Kathir notes how Allah describes Himself here as "Al-Aziz" (the Mighty, who will avenge His servants) and "Al-Hamid" (the Praiseworthy, who deserves to be worshipped). This is a powerful rhetorical point: believing in the Mighty, Praiseworthy God is the most noble thing a person can do, yet the tyrants treated it as the worst crime. This pattern repeats throughout history whenever truth confronts power.

9
الَّذِي لَهُ مُلْكُ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضِ ۚ وَاللَّهُ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ شَهِيدٌ

Allah owns everything in the heavens and the earth. The tyrant king thought he had power, but his "kingdom" was nothing compared to the dominion of Allah. And Allah is "Shahid" over all things. As-Sa'di explains that this verse is both a consolation to the oppressed (Allah saw everything and will bring justice) and a warning to the oppressors (you cannot escape the One who witnesses all things and owns all things). The word "shahid" beautifully connects back to the oath in verse 3.

10

Indeed, those who have tortured the believing men and believing women and then have not repented will have the punishment of Hell, and they will have the punishment of the Burning Fire.

Simple Explanation

The divine verdict. Those who tortured believers and did not repent will face two punishments: Hell and the Burning Fire. Ibn Kathir notes a profound subtlety: Allah says "thumma lam yatubu" (then did not repent), leaving the door of repentance open even for those who committed this horrific crime. Al-Hasan al-Basri remarked: "Look at the generosity and mercy of Allah! They killed His close friends and allies, yet He still offers them the chance to repent." The word "fatanu" means to put through a trial by fire, the same fire they used against the believers will be used against them, but infinitely worse.

11

After describing the punishment of the oppressors, Allah immediately describes the reward of the believers. They were thrown into trenches of fire in this world, but in the next world they will have Gardens with rivers flowing beneath them. As-Sa'di explains that "al-fawz al-kabir" (the great attainment) is the ultimate success. The believers who were burned alive "lost" in the eyes of the world, but in reality they won the greatest victory. This is a complete reversal of worldly perception. The ones who appeared defeated were actually the victors, and the ones who appeared powerful were actually the losers.

Now Allah shifts to describing His own power. "Al-batsh" means to seize with overwhelming force. When Allah seizes a tyrant or a wrongdoer, His grip is devastating and inescapable. Ibn Kathir cites the hadith in Sahih Bukhari where the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Indeed, Allah gives respite to the wrongdoer, but when He seizes him, He does not let him escape." Then he recited this verse. The "la" in "lashadid" is for emphasis: certainly, absolutely, undeniably severe. This is a warning to every oppressor throughout history.

13

As-Sa'di adds that this verse establishes Allah's absolute power. The One who created everything from nothing can certainly recreate it. This also connects to the story of the believers who were killed. Their death is not the end. Allah will bring them back and reward them for their sacrifice.

14

After mentioning His severe punishment, Allah immediately mentions His forgiveness and love. He is "Al-Ghafur" (the One who covers sins and forgives abundantly) and "Al-Wadud" (the Loving, the Affectionate). As-Sa'di notes the beautiful pairing: even after describing the horrific crime of the trench, Allah mentions that He forgives those who repent. And "Al-Wadud" means He does not just tolerate the repentant person, He actually loves them. This is extraordinary mercy.

True kingship belongs to Allah alone.

"Fa''al" is an intensified form. He does not merely do things. He does whatever He wills, whenever He wills, however He wills, with absolute power and no resistance. This is a source of both awe and comfort: awe because His will is unstoppable, and comfort because He has willed good for the believers and justice for the oppressed. The tyrant king tried to prevent the boy from spreading faith, but Allah's will prevailed.

Ibn Kathir explains that these are presented as warnings: other powerful armies defied Allah and were wiped out. What happened to them will happen to anyone who follows their path. The Quraysh who were persecuting the Muslims should take heed.

Ibn Kathir explains that both represent the ultimate fate of those who oppose Allah. Their power, wealth, and armies could not save them. The Quran is inscribed in "Al-Lawh al-Mahfuz," the Preserved Tablet. The believers' sacrifice was not in vain, the oppressors' crime is recorded, and the Quran that tells their story is preserved forever.

Key Lessons
  • 01
    Faith may cost everything, but it is worth everything. The believers of the trench gave their lives rather than deny Allah. Their sacrifice was not a loss but "the great attainment" (al-fawz al-kabir). True victory is measured by where you stand with Allah, not by worldly outcomes.
  • 02
    Persecution of believers is a pattern throughout history. From the People of the Ditch to Pharaoh to Thamud, tyrants have always tried to crush faith. But every single one of them was destroyed while faith endured. The Quraysh's persecution of the early Muslims was nothing new, and their fate would be no different.
  • 03
    Allah's door of repentance remains open even for the worst criminals. Even those who burned believers alive are offered the chance to repent (verse 10). This shows the immensity of Allah's mercy. No sin is too great for His forgiveness, as long as repentance is sincere.
  • 04
    Allah is both Al-Ghafur and Al-Wadud. He does not just forgive; He loves. His relationship with His servants is not cold justice but warm affection. This combination of forgiveness and love is unique to Allah's description of Himself.
  • 05
    No power can resist Allah's will. He is "Fa''al lima yurid" (Effecter of what He intends). The king tried to kill the boy multiple times but failed until Allah willed it. When Allah intends something, no force in the universe can prevent it.
  • 06
    The Quran is eternally preserved. Written in the Preserved Tablet, it cannot be altered, corrupted, or destroyed. This is Allah's guarantee that the truth will always be accessible to humanity.
