The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Fajr is one of the most powerful early Makkan surahs. It was revealed at a time when the Quraysh were deeply attached to their wealth, power, and worldly status, and they saw these as signs of Allah's favor. If someone was rich, they assumed Allah loved them. If someone was poor, they assumed Allah had humiliated them.

Allah begins with a series of majestic oaths: by the dawn, the ten sacred nights, the even and the odd, and the night as it passes. These oaths set the stage for a powerful message about the rise and fall of nations and the true nature of Allah's tests.

The "ten nights" referenced in verse 2 are understood by Ibn Abbas, Ibn Zubayr, Mujahid, and others to be the first ten nights of Dhul Hijjah. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "There are no days in which righteous deeds are more beloved to Allah than these ten days." They said: "Not even jihad for the sake of Allah?" He said: "Not even jihad for the sake of Allah, except for a man who goes out with his self and his wealth and does not return with anything."

Source: Sahih Bukhari 969

The surah then recounts the destruction of three mighty civilizations: the people of Ad (who built Iram of the pillars), the people of Thamud (who carved homes into mountains), and Pharaoh (who ruled with tyranny). Despite their immense power and resources, all were destroyed when they transgressed beyond limits.

The surah then addresses a fundamental flaw in human thinking: mistaking worldly blessings for divine approval and worldly hardship for divine punishment. Allah corrects this: wealth is a test, not a reward. Poverty is a test, not a punishment. The surah ends with one of the most beautiful passages in the Quran, addressing the "nafs al-mutma'innah" (the peaceful, content soul) and inviting it to return to its Lord, pleased and pleasing.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَالْفَجْرِ
Wal-fajr
By the dawn.

Word by Word

وَwaBy (oath)
الْفَجْرِal-fajrThe dawn
Simple Explanation

Allah begins by swearing an oath by the dawn (fajr). The dawn is a powerful symbol: it is that moment when darkness gives way to light, when a new day begins. Ibn Kathir explains this refers to the dawn in general, representing hope and renewal. Some scholars say it specifically refers to the dawn of Dhul Hijjah (the 10th day, Eid al-Adha). Allah swears by His creation to draw our attention to its significance.

2
وَلَيَالٍ عَشْرٍ
Wa layalin 'ashr
And [by] ten nights.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd (oath)
لَيَالٍlayalinNights
عَشْرٍ'ashrTen
Simple Explanation

The majority of scholars, including Ibn Abbas, say these are the first ten nights of Dhul Hijjah, the most sacred days in the Islamic calendar. These are the days leading up to Eid al-Adha, days of immense worship, sacrifice, and remembrance of Allah. The Prophet (peace be upon him) emphasized the virtue of good deeds in these ten days above all other days (Sahih Bukhari 969). Allah swears by them to highlight their weight.

3
وَالشَّفْعِ وَالْوَتْرِ
Wash-shaf'i wal-watr
And [by] the even and the odd.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd (oath)
الشَّفْعِash-shaf'iThe even
وَwaAnd
الْوَتْرِal-watrThe odd
Simple Explanation

Scholars have different views on what "the even and the odd" refer to. Some say the even refers to the Day of Sacrifice (10th Dhul Hijjah) and the odd refers to the Day of Arafah (9th Dhul Hijjah). Others say it refers to all of Allah's creation which exists in pairs (even) while Allah Himself is One (odd/witr). As-Sa'di suggests it points to the prayers: some are even in their units, some are odd (like Witr prayer). The depth of meaning in these two words shows the richness of the Quran.

4

The word "yasri" means to travel or move on. The night passes, it doesn't stay forever. This is a profound reminder: no matter how long and dark the night feels, it is always moving toward its end. Ibn Kathir notes this could also mean the night as it comes and covers everything, emphasizing Allah's control over the cycle of time. Both meanings carry comfort: darkness is temporary by design.

This is a rhetorical question challenging the listener: if you have any sense at all, these oaths should be enough to wake you up. The signs of Allah are everywhere for those who think.

6

Allah now begins recounting the stories of destroyed nations, starting with Ad. The people of Ad were an ancient Arabian tribe known for their enormous physical strength and advanced civilization. The question "have you not seen?" is asked to make the listener reflect on historical evidence that power alone cannot save you from Allah's punishment.

7

Iram was the legendary city of the people of Ad, described as having towering pillars and magnificent architecture. Some scholars say "Iram" is a reference to their ancestor. Either way, the point is clear: they built something extraordinary, yet it could not protect them from Allah. Their civilization became a lesson for all of humanity.

8

Allah Himself testifies that nothing like Ad's civilization had ever been created on earth. They were unmatched in their strength, their buildings, and their achievements. This is not just praise; it is a warning. If the most powerful civilization ever to exist could be completely erased by Allah, what chance does anyone else have who defies Him? Power, wealth, and innovation mean nothing without obedience to Allah.

9
وَثَمُودَ الَّذِينَ جَابُوا الصَّخْرَ بِالْوَادِ

The people of Thamud lived in the area of Al-Hijr (modern-day Mada'in Salih in Saudi Arabia). Their remarkable skill was carving massive homes and structures directly from solid rock in the mountainous valleys. The word "jabu" means to cut through or carve. Prophet Salih was sent to them. They demanded a she-camel as a miracle, and when it appeared, they killed it in arrogance. Their rock-carved homes could not save them from Allah's punishment.

10
وَفِرْعَوْنَ ذِي الْأَوْتَادِ
Wa Fir'awna dhil-awtad
And [with] Pharaoh, owner of the stakes.

Word by Word

الْأَوْتَادِal-awtadThe stakes / pegs
Simple Explanation

Pharaoh is described as "dhi al-awtad" (owner of stakes/pegs). Scholars give different explanations: Ibn Kathir mentions that Pharaoh would torture people by staking them to the ground with pegs. Others say it refers to the pyramids, which are like pegs driven into the earth. Some say it refers to the vastness of his armies, which were as firmly established as stakes. Regardless of interpretation, Pharaoh represented the height of political tyranny, yet even he could not escape Allah's grasp.

11
الَّذِينَ طَغَوْا فِي الْبِلَادِ
Alladhina taghaw fil-bilad
Those who transgressed within the lands.

Word by Word

الَّذِينَalladhinaThose who

فِيfiIn

Simple Explanation

The word "taghaw" comes from "tughyan" meaning to cross all limits, to rebel against Allah's boundaries. All three nations shared this common trait: they went beyond bounds in the land. Their power made them arrogant, and their arrogance made them oppressors. This is a universal pattern: when people feel invincible, they start acting as if there are no rules. But Allah's rules always apply, no matter how powerful you think you are.

12
فَأَكْثَرُوا فِيهَا الْفَسَادَ

And increased therein the corruption.

Word by Word

الْفَسَادَal-fasadThe corruption
Simple Explanation

"Fasad" means corruption, mischief, and spreading evil. They didn't just transgress once; they kept increasing ("aktharu") the corruption in the land. This is the spiral of sin: it starts small and grows if unchecked. They corrupted morality, justice, and social order. As-Sa'di points out that their corruption included oppressing the weak, denying Allah's messengers, and destroying the fabric of righteous society.

13
فَصَبَّ عَلَيْهِمْ رَبُّكَ سَوْطَ عَذَابٍ
Fasabba 'alayhim rabbuka sawta 'adhab
So your Lord poured upon them a scourge of punishment.

Simple Explanation

The word "sabba" means to pour down heavily, and "sawt" literally means a whip or lash. The image is of punishment being poured down upon them like a torrential downpour of pain. Each nation received its own form of destruction: Ad was destroyed by a furious wind, Thamud by a terrible scream, and Pharaoh was drowned in the sea. Despite their different forms, the punishment was unified in its source: it came from Allah, the Lord of all worlds.

14
إِنَّ رَبَّكَ لَبِالْمِرْصَادِ

Indeed, your Lord is in observation.

Word by Word

إِنَّinnaIndeed

Simple Explanation

"Al-mirsad" comes from "rasada" meaning to watch and observe. The word evokes the image of someone stationed at a watchtower, letting nothing pass unnoticed. Allah is watching every deed, every oppression, every act of transgression. No one escapes His observation. Ibn Kathir says: "All creatures will pass before Him, and He will recompense each person according to their deeds." This verse is both a warning to tyrants and a comfort to the oppressed: Allah sees everything.

15
فَأَمَّا الْإِنسَانُ إِذَا مَا ابْتَلَاهُ رَبُّهُ فَأَكْرَمَهُ وَنَعَّمَهُ فَيَقُولُ رَبِّي أَكْرَمَنِ
Fa'ammal-insanu idha mab-talahu rabbuhu fa'akramahu wa na''amahu fayaqulu rabbi akraman
And as for man, when his Lord tries him and [thus] is generous to him and favors him, he says, "My Lord has honored me."

Word by Word

فَأَمَّاfa'ammaSo as for
الْإِنسَانُal-insanuThe human
إِذَا مَاidha maWhen

Simple Explanation

This verse exposes a fundamental flaw in human thinking. When Allah gives someone wealth, health, and comfort, that person assumes: "Allah must love me! I'm special! I deserve this!" But notice the key word here: "ibtala" (tested). Allah says the wealth IS a test. The honor is not a sign of divine approval; it is a trial to see what you will do with it. Will you be grateful or arrogant? Will you share or hoard? The Quraysh made this exact mistake, equating wealth with divine favor.

16

The correct response to hardship is patience and trust. Neither situation is an indicator of your worth in Allah's eyes.

17
كَلَّا ۖ بَل لَّا تُكْرِمُونَ الْيَتِيمَ

"Kalla!" is a strong rebuke meaning "Absolutely not! You have it all wrong!" Allah rejects their entire framework of thinking about wealth and poverty. Notice the shift from singular "insan" (one person's thinking) to plural "tukrimu-na" (you all). This is a societal failure, not just an individual one.

18

The word "tahadduna" is powerful. It doesn't just say "you don't feed the poor." It says "you don't even encourage EACH OTHER to feed the poor." It's not enough to give charity yourself; a righteous society actively promotes and encourages generosity. When people stop reminding each other to care for the needy, the entire community fails. This verse holds society accountable as a collective, not just as individuals.

19
وَتَأْكُلُونَ التُّرَاثَ أَكْلًا لَّمًّا
Wa ta'kulunat-turatha aklan lamma
And you consume inheritance, devouring [it] altogether.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

In pre-Islamic Arabia, women and children were often denied their rightful inheritance. The powerful men would consume everything, taking shares that didn't belong to them. "Lamma" means to gather everything together and devour it all, leaving nothing for the rightful heirs. This was a form of institutionalized injustice. Islam later corrected this by establishing clear inheritance laws that protect the rights of women, children, and all relatives.

20
وَتُحِبُّونَ الْمَالَ حُبًّا جَمًّا

"Jamma" means overwhelming, excessive, all-consuming. The problem isn't having wealth; it's loving it excessively. When the love of money becomes the driving force of your life, it corrupts everything: your relationships, your ethics, your worship, your humanity. As-Sa'di explains that this excessive love of wealth is what leads to all the social evils mentioned: neglecting orphans, ignoring the poor, and consuming others' inheritance. Wealth should be a tool, not an idol.

21

The Iram of the pillars, the rock-carved homes of Thamud, the pyramids of Pharaoh, all turned to dust. This is the ultimate equalizer.

22

The image is awe-inspiring and terrifying: all of creation gathered, the earth flattened, the angels in perfect formation, and Allah presiding over the judgment. Nothing will be hidden on that day.

23

24

This is the ultimate regret: realizing too late that you spent all your effort on the temporary and neglected the permanent.

25

For the oppressed, this is a source of comfort: true justice will come.

This pair of verses (25-26) creates a powerful contrast with the previous descriptions of Ad, Thamud, and Pharaoh, who thought they were beyond anyone's reach.

النَّفْسُan-nafsuThe soul
الْمُطْمَئِنَّةُal-mutma'innahThe reassured / at peace
Simple Explanation

After the terrifying scenes of judgment, the tone shifts dramatically to one of the most tender and beautiful addresses in the entire Quran. "Al-mutma'innah" comes from "itmi'nan" meaning tranquility, peace, and contentment. This is the soul that found peace through remembering Allah, that remained firm during trials, that was content with Allah's decree in both ease and hardship. Ibn Kathir says it is the soul that is certain in faith, peaceful in worship, and undisturbed by worldly fluctuations. It is the highest state of the human soul.

ارْجِعِي إِلَىٰ رَبِّكِ رَاضِيَةً مَّرْضِيَّةً
Irji'i ila rabbiki radiyatan mardiyyah
Return to your Lord, well-pleased and pleasing [to Him].

Word by Word

ارْجِعِيirji'iReturn
إِلَىٰilaTo
رَبِّكِrabbikiYour Lord
رَاضِيَةًradiyatanWell-pleased
مَّرْضِيَّةًmardiyyahPleasing (to Him)
Simple Explanation

Two beautiful words define this return: "radiyah" (you are pleased with Allah) and "mardiyyah" (Allah is pleased with you). It's a mutual pleasure. You spent your life content with whatever Allah decreed, and now Allah is content with you. "Irji'i" (return) is in the feminine form because "nafs" (soul) is grammatically feminine in Arabic. This is an invitation, not a command of fear, but one of love and honor. It is the homecoming every believer hopes for.

فَادْخُلِي فِي عِبَادِي
Fadkhuli fi 'ibadi
And enter among My [righteous] servants.

Word by Word

فَادْخُلِيfadkhuliSo enter
فِيfiAmong
عِبَادِي'ibadiMy servants
Simple Explanation

Allah says "'ibadi" (MY servants) with the possessive "my," showing honor and closeness. Being called a servant of Allah is the highest title a human can carry. You will join the ranks of the prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous. After a lifetime of striving, you are welcomed into this noble company. This is the ultimate sense of belonging: being among those whom Allah Himself claims as His own.

30
وَادْخُلِي جَنَّتِي
Wadkhuli jannati
And enter My Paradise.

Word by Word

وَادْخُلِيwadkhuliAnd enter
جَنَّتِيjannatiMy Paradise
Simple Explanation

The surah ends with the most beautiful invitation in existence: "Enter MY Paradise." Again, "jannati" (MY Paradise) carries the possessive. Allah doesn't say "enter Paradise" but "enter MY Paradise," as if it was prepared personally for you. After all the warnings about destroyed nations, the rebuke of materialism, and the terrifying Day of Judgment, the surah concludes with pure mercy, warmth, and welcome. This is where the journey of the mutma'innah soul ends: in eternal bliss, in the company of the righteous, in the garden of the Most Merciful.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Fajr
  • ◆
    Wealth is a test, not a reward. Poverty is a test, not a punishment.

    Allah corrects the most common human misconception: equating worldly blessings with divine approval. Both wealth and hardship are tests. What matters is how you respond to each.

  • ◆
    No civilization is too powerful for Allah's punishment.

    Ad built the unmatched Iram, Thamud carved mountains, Pharaoh ruled empires. All were destroyed when they transgressed. Power without submission to Allah is a path to ruin.

  • ◆
    Allah is always watching (bil-mirsad).

    No deed goes unnoticed. This is a warning for oppressors and a comfort for the oppressed. Justice may be delayed, but it is never denied.

  • ◆
    Social responsibility is a collective duty.

    Honoring orphans, feeding the poor, and protecting inheritance rights are not optional acts of kindness. They are obligations. A society that fails in these fails entirely.

  • ◆
    The "real life" is the Hereafter.

    On the Day of Judgment, people will wish they had "sent ahead" good deeds for their real life. Every moment of this world is an opportunity to invest in eternity.

  • ◆
    The ultimate goal: becoming a "nafs al-mutma'innah."

    The peaceful soul that is content with Allah in every situation. This is the soul that will hear the most beautiful invitation: "Enter My Paradise." This state comes through remembrance, patience, gratitude, and trust in Allah's plan.
