The Story Behind This Surah

Surah An-Naba opens the 30th Juz (Amma) of the Quran and sets the tone for the entire final portion. It was revealed in Makkah when the disbelievers of Quraysh were arguing and questioning each other about the reality of the Day of Judgment. Some denied it outright, others were uncertain, and they treated it as a joke. Allah responds to their skepticism with overwhelming evidence from His creation.

The surah follows a powerful three-part structure. First, it presents signs of Allah's creative power: the earth spread as a bed, mountains as pegs, humans created in pairs, sleep as rest, night as a covering, day for livelihood, seven strong heavens, a blazing lamp (the sun), rain from clouds producing gardens and grain. The logic is clear: the One who created all of this with such precision can certainly resurrect you.

Ibn Kathir explains that the "Great News" (An-Naba al-Adheem) refers to the Day of Resurrection, about which they disagree. Some scholars also say it refers to the Quran itself, but the stronger opinion is that it refers to the Day of Judgment, as confirmed by the context of the surah. The Quraysh were divided: some denied resurrection completely, while others were doubtful. Allah silences them all by pointing to His creation as proof of His power to resurrect.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Introduction to Surah An-Naba; Tafsir As-Sa'di

The second part describes the Day of Judgment itself: the Trumpet is blown, people come in crowds, the sky is opened as gates, and the mountains are moved like a mirage. Then comes the terrifying description of Hell, lying in wait for the transgressors, where they will remain for ages tasting nothing cool except scalding water and purulence.

The third part contrasts this with the reward of the righteous: gardens, grapevines, full cups, no vain talk or lying. The surah closes with a warning that sends chills: "Indeed, We have warned you of a near punishment, on the Day when a man will observe what his hands have put forth, and the disbeliever will say, 'Oh, I wish that I were dust!'"

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
عَمَّ يَتَسَاءَلُونَ
'Amma yatasa'aloon
About what are they asking one another?

Word by Word

عَمَّ'ammaAbout what
يَتَسَاءَلُونَyatasa'aloonAre they asking one another
Simple Explanation

The surah opens with a striking rhetorical question. "'Amma" is a combination of "'an" (about) and "ma" (what), creating a powerful interrogative. The people of Quraysh were constantly questioning and disputing about the resurrection. Ibn Kathir explains that this question is meant to express amazement and rebuke: what could possibly be so confusing to them? As-Sa'di notes that the question itself implies the answer should be obvious. The tone is one of disapproval, as if to say: "Why are you even debating this?"

2
عَنِ النَّبَإِ الْعَظِيمِ
'Anin-naba'il-'adheem
About the great news.

Word by Word

عَنِ'aniAbout
النَّبَإِan-naba'iThe news
الْعَظِيمِal-'adheemThe great / mighty
Simple Explanation

Here Allah answers the question: they are asking about "An-Naba al-Adheem," the Great News. Ibn Kathir states that the majority of scholars say this refers to the Day of Resurrection. It is called "great" because of its magnitude and the enormity of what will happen on that Day. As-Sa'di explains that calling it "great" is itself a proof against the deniers. This is not some trivial matter they can dismiss. It is the single most important event in all of existence, the Day when every soul will be held accountable.

3
الَّذِي هُمْ فِيهِ مُخْتَلِفُونَ
Alladhee hum feehi mukhtalifoon
That over which they are in disagreement.

Word by Word

الَّذِيalladheeThat which
هُمْhumThey
فِيهِfeehiAbout it / in it
مُخْتَلِفُونَmukhtalifoonAre in disagreement
Simple Explanation

The people were not united in their denial. They were "mukhtalifoon," disagreeing even among themselves. Ibn Kathir explains that some completely denied the resurrection, others were uncertain, and some mocked it. As-Sa'di points out that their disagreement itself is a sign of their confusion. Truth is clear and unites people, while falsehood scatters them. The fact that they could not even agree on what to believe about the afterlife shows they were wandering in darkness without guidance.

4
كَلَّا سَيَعْلَمُونَ
Kalla sa-ya'lamoon
No! They are going to know.

Word by Word

كَلَّاkallaNo! / Nay!
سَيَعْلَمُونَsa-ya'lamoonThey will come to know
Simple Explanation

"Kalla" is one of the strongest words of negation and rebuke in Arabic. It shuts down the conversation. Allah is saying: enough of your debates and mockery. "Sa-ya'lamoon" uses the future tense particle "sa" (soon), meaning they will definitely come to know the truth. Ibn Kathir explains this as a severe warning and threat. As-Sa'di says the word "kalla" is used to deter them from their heedlessness and to warn them that the reality they deny will soon confront them with certainty.

5
ثُمَّ كَلَّا سَيَعْلَمُونَ
Thumma kalla sa-ya'lamoon
Then, no! They are going to know.

Word by Word

ثُمَّthummaThen
كَلَّاkallaNo! / Nay!
سَيَعْلَمُونَsa-ya'lamoonThey will come to know
Simple Explanation

The repetition with "thumma" (then) intensifies the warning. When Allah repeats a threat, it amplifies the severity. Ibn Kathir explains that this repetition serves as emphasis and intensification of the warning. As-Sa'di notes that "thumma" adds a layer of escalation, as if to say: "They will know, and then they will REALLY know." Some scholars say the first refers to knowing at the moment of death, and the second to knowing on the Day of Judgment itself. Either way, the truth will become undeniable.

6
أَلَمْ نَجْعَلِ الْأَرْضَ مِهَادًا
Alam naj'alil-arda mihada
Have We not made the earth a resting place?

Word by Word

أَلَمْalamHave We not
نَجْعَلِnaj'aliMade
الْأَرْضَal-ardaThe earth
مِهَادًاmihadaA resting place / bed
Simple Explanation

Now begins the section of proofs from creation. "Mihad" means a bed or cradle, something comfortable to rest on. The question "alam" (have We not?) demands acknowledgment. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah made the earth stable, settled, and comfortable for human habitation. It is neither too hard nor too soft, neither too hot nor too cold in its overall design. As-Sa'di notes that the One who made the earth this way from nothing certainly has the power to resurrect the dead. This is the core argument of the surah: creation proves resurrection.

7
وَالْجِبَالَ أَوْتَادًا
Wal-jibala awtada
And the mountains as stakes?

Word by Word

وَالْجِبَالَwal-jibalaAnd the mountains
أَوْتَادًاawtadaAs stakes / pegs
Simple Explanation

"Awtad" is the plural of "watad," which means a peg or stake driven into the ground. Just as tent pegs hold a tent in place, mountains stabilize the earth. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah placed mountains on the earth to keep it firm and prevent it from shaking with its inhabitants. As-Sa'di adds that this metaphor of pegs is remarkably precise. Modern geology has confirmed that mountains have deep roots extending far beneath the surface, just like pegs. This was stated in the Quran over 1400 years ago.

8
وَخَلَقْنَاكُمْ أَزْوَاجًا
Wa khalaqnakum azwaja
And We created you in pairs.

Word by Word

وَخَلَقْنَاكُمْwa khalaqnakumAnd We created you
أَزْوَاجًاazwajaIn pairs
Simple Explanation

"Azwaj" means pairs: male and female. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah created mankind as males and females so they may find comfort and companionship in one another, and so the human race may continue through procreation. As-Sa'di points out that the creation of pairs is itself a sign of Allah's wisdom and power. From a single soul, He created its mate, and from the two of them, He scattered countless men and women. The complementary design of male and female points to a purposeful Creator, not random chance.

9
وَجَعَلْنَا نَوْمَكُمْ سُبَاتًا
Wa ja'alna nawmakum subata
And made your sleep [a means for] rest.

Word by Word

وَجَعَلْنَاwa ja'alnaAnd We made
نَوْمَكُمْnawmakumYour sleep
سُبَاتًاsubataA rest / cessation
Simple Explanation

"Subat" comes from "sabt" meaning a cutting off or cessation. Sleep cuts off your activity so your body and mind can recover. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah made sleep as a means of rest for the body, a pause from the exhaustion of daily work. As-Sa'di draws a powerful parallel: sleep is a "mini-death" where consciousness is suspended and then restored. If Allah can take your soul every night in sleep and return it to you every morning, can He not do the same after actual death? Sleep itself is a daily proof of resurrection.

10
وَجَعَلْنَا اللَّيْلَ لِبَاسًا
Wa ja'alnal-layla libasa
And made the night as clothing.

Word by Word

وَجَعَلْنَاwa ja'alnaAnd We made
اللَّيْلَal-laylaThe night
لِبَاسًاlibasaAs clothing / covering
Simple Explanation

"Libas" means a garment or clothing. The night covers the world in darkness just as clothing covers the body. Ibn Kathir explains that the darkness of the night wraps around everything, providing cover and concealment. As-Sa'di notes the beauty of this metaphor: just as clothing protects the body, provides warmth, and gives privacy, the night provides a covering of darkness for rest, coolness after the heat of the day, and a peaceful atmosphere for sleep. Everything in creation serves a purpose.

11
وَجَعَلْنَا النَّهَارَ مَعَاشًا
Wa ja'alnan-nahara ma'asha
And made the day for livelihood.

Word by Word

وَجَعَلْنَاwa ja'alnaAnd We made
النَّهَارَan-naharaThe day
مَعَاشًاma'ashaFor livelihood
Simple Explanation

"Ma'ash" means livelihood, the means of living and earning. Ibn Kathir explains that the daytime is bright so people can go about their work, seek their provision, travel, and conduct their affairs. As-Sa'di highlights the perfect design: night for rest and day for work. This alternating cycle is not accidental. It is a deliberate design by the All-Wise Creator. Together, verses 9-11 show how Allah designed the human experience: sleep for recovery, night for rest, and day for productivity. Every part of the cycle points to a purposeful Creator.

12
وَبَنَيْنَا فَوْقَكُمْ سَبْعًا شِدَادًا
Wa banayna fawqakum sab'an shidada
And constructed above you seven strong [heavens].

Word by Word

وَبَنَيْنَاwa banaynaAnd We constructed
فَوْقَكُمْfawqakumAbove you
سَبْعًاsab'anSeven
شِدَادًاshidadaStrong / firm
Simple Explanation

"Shidad" means strong, firm, and well-built. The seven heavens are described as powerful structures above us. Ibn Kathir explains that these are the seven heavens, firmly built, vast in size, with enormous power and perfect construction. They have stood for eons without any pillars, cracks, or defects. As-Sa'di adds that "banayna" (We constructed) emphasizes that the heavens are a built structure, pointing to a Builder. The word "shidad" indicates they are reinforced and powerful, yet they will be torn apart on the Day of Judgment, showing that Allah's power surpasses even these mighty creations.

13
وَجَعَلْنَا سِرَاجًا وَهَّاجًا
Wa ja'alna sirajan wahhaja
And made [therein] a burning lamp.

Word by Word

وَجَعَلْنَاwa ja'alnaAnd We made
سِرَاجًاsirajanA lamp
وَهَّاجًاwahhajaBurning / blazing
Simple Explanation

"Siraj" means a lamp or torch, and "wahhaj" means intensely burning, blazing with both light and heat. This refers to the sun. Ibn Kathir explains that the sun is described as a "siraj" (lamp) because it provides both light and heat, unlike the moon which only reflects light. The word "wahhaj" conveys the immense heat and radiance of the sun. As-Sa'di notes the precision of Quranic language: the sun is never called "muneer" (illuminating) in the Quran, that word is reserved for the moon. The sun is "siraj" or "diya'" because it generates its own light and heat.

14
وَأَنزَلْنَا مِنَ الْمُعْصِرَاتِ مَاءً ثَجَّاجًا
Wa anzalna minal-mu'sirati ma'an thajjaja
And sent down, from the rain clouds, pouring water.

Word by Word

"Mu'sirat" refers to clouds that are heavy with rain, ready to squeeze out water. "Thajjaj" means pouring down heavily and abundantly. Ibn Kathir explains that these are the rain-laden clouds that Allah sends, and from them comes water pouring down in abundance to revive the earth. As-Sa'di notes that rain is one of the clearest signs of Allah's power and mercy. The entire water cycle, from evaporation to clouds to rain, is a marvel of design. And just as rain brings dead earth back to life, Allah will bring the dead back to life on the Day of Judgment.

15
لِّنُخْرِجَ بِهِ حَبًّا وَنَبَاتًا

"Habb" means grain (wheat, barley, rice, etc.) and "nabat" means vegetation and plants in general. Ibn Kathir explains that rain is sent down so that grain for humans and vegetation for animals may grow. As-Sa'di emphasizes the purposefulness: rain is not random. It serves a function. It produces the food that sustains all life on earth. The connection to resurrection is clear: just as Allah brings living plants out of dead soil using rain, He will bring the dead out of their graves on the Day of Judgment. The mechanism is the same: Allah's command.

16
وَجَنَّاتٍ أَلْفَافًا
Wa jannatin alfafa
And lush gardens.

Word by Word

وَجَنَّاتٍwa jannatinAnd gardens
أَلْفَافًاalfafaLush / dense / intertwined
Simple Explanation

"Alfaf" means dense, lush, and intertwined. These are gardens where trees and plants grow so thick that their branches intertwine. Ibn Kathir explains that these are beautiful, dense gardens with various types of fruits and plants growing closely together. As-Sa'di notes that this is the culmination of the creation sequence: rain leads to grain, grain leads to vegetation, and vegetation leads to lush gardens. From a single drop of rain, Allah produces an entire ecosystem. This progression also foreshadows the gardens of Paradise mentioned later in the surah.

17
إِنَّ يَوْمَ الْفَصْلِ كَانَ مِيقَاتًا

After listing the proofs of Allah's creative power, the surah now shifts to the Day itself. "Yawm al-Fasl" means the Day of Separation or Judgment, when truth will be separated from falsehood, and the righteous from the wicked. "Meeqat" means an appointed time, fixed and determined. Ibn Kathir explains that this Day has a set time with Allah that will not be delayed or brought forward. As-Sa'di emphasizes that "kana" (was) is used in the past tense to indicate absolute certainty, as if it has already happened. It is as certain as yesterday.

18
يَوْمَ يُنفَخُ فِي الصُّورِ فَتَأْتُونَ أَفْوَاجًا

"As-Soor" is the Trumpet or Horn that the angel Israfil will blow. "Afwaj" means groups, multitudes, or crowds. Ibn Kathir explains that this is the second blowing of the Trumpet, which causes all of creation to be resurrected from their graves and brought forth in massive crowds for judgment. As-Sa'di describes the scene: nation after nation, generation after generation, all emerging from the earth simultaneously, heading toward the place of gathering. The sheer scale is unimaginable: every human being who ever lived, from Adam to the last person, all gathered in one place.

19

The sky, which was described as "seven strong heavens" just verses ago, will be opened up and become full of gateways. Ibn Kathir explains that the sky will be split open with so many openings that it becomes like doors and pathways. This is for the descent of the angels. As-Sa'di notes the contrast: the same sky that was described as "shidad" (strong, firm) in verse 12 is now being torn apart. This shows that no matter how strong something is, it is nothing before Allah's power. The mighty heavens that stood for billions of years will be opened like gates on that Day.

20

"Sarab" is a mirage, the optical illusion seen in deserts where travelers think they see water but find nothing. Ibn Kathir explains the stages: the mountains will first be set in motion, then crumbled to dust, then blown away, becoming like a mirage before vanishing completely. As-Sa'di notes the terrifying progression: if the mountains, the most massive and stable things on earth, become nothing but an illusion, what chance does a human being have of escaping Allah's judgment?

21

Ibn Kathir explains that Hell is prepared and waiting, an ambush point that the transgressors cannot escape. It has been made ready since it was created, waiting for the appointed time. As-Sa'di adds that "mirsad" implies that Hell is on the path, meaning everyone must pass by it (as confirmed in Surah Maryam 19:71, "There is none of you except he will come to it"). The believers will be saved, but the transgressors will fall into it.

22
لِّلطَّاغِينَ مَآبًا

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

"Taghin" comes from "tughyan," meaning to transgress, exceed limits, or rebel. "Ma'ab" means a place of return or final destination. Ibn Kathir explains that Hell is the final abode for those who transgressed the limits set by Allah, who disobeyed Him and rejected His messengers. As-Sa'di emphasizes that "ma'ab" (place of return) implies this is where they were always headed. Their sins were taking them toward this destination all along, even if they did not realize it. The word "taghin" covers all forms of transgression: disbelief, arrogance, oppression, and rebellion against Allah.

23
لَّابِثِينَ فِيهَا أَحْقَابًا

Word by Word

"Ahqab" is the plural of "huqb," which means a very long period of time (some scholars say 80 years, others say even longer). The plural "ahqab" means ages upon ages. Ibn Kathir explains that this means they will remain in Hell for successive periods, one after another, with no end. Every time one "huqb" ends, another begins. As-Sa'di clarifies that this does not mean a limited time. Rather, it means endless consecutive periods. The scholars of tafsir are unanimous that the punishment of the disbelievers in Hell is eternal, as confirmed by other verses in the Quran.

24

As-Sa'di notes that this verse establishes the principle: Hell is the opposite of every comfort. Everything a person takes for granted in this world, a cool drink on a hot day, a breeze, shade, will be completely absent. This is the consequence of taking Allah's blessings for granted.

As-Sa'di explains that the exception here makes the punishment worse, not better. When they are told "except" this, they find what is offered is even more torturous than having nothing at all. This is the ultimate deception of their expectations.

26

"Wifaq" means fitting, appropriate, or matching. The punishment fits the crime perfectly. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah's punishment is never unjust. It is exactly proportional to their sins. The transgressors chose to deny Allah's signs, reject His messengers, and live in rebellion. The consequence they face is the fitting, just result of their own choices. This is not cruelty; it is justice.

This verse teaches that belief in the afterlife is not just a theological concept; it is the foundation of moral behavior.

Simple Explanation

"Kidhdhab" is an emphatic form of "kadhib" (lying/denial), meaning they denied vehemently, repeatedly, and stubbornly. Ibn Kathir explains that they not only rejected Allah's signs but did so with extreme and persistent denial. They rejected the Quran, rejected the messengers, and rejected the evidence from creation that surrounded them. As-Sa'di notes that "ayatina" (Our signs) includes both the revealed verses of the Quran and the observable signs in creation. They denied everything: the Book, the Prophet, and the evidence of their own existence. This made their denial complete and inexcusable.

29
وَكُلَّ شَيْءٍ أَحْصَيْنَاهُ كِتَابًا
Wa kulla shay'in ahsaynahu kitaba
But all things We have enumerated in writing.

Word by Word

أَحْصَيْنَاهُahsaynahuWe have enumerated it
كِتَابًاkitabaIn writing / a record
Simple Explanation

"Ahsaynahu" means We have counted, recorded, and preserved it completely. "Kitab" means in a written record. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah has recorded every single deed, whether small or large, good or evil. Nothing escapes His record. The angels write down everything, and it is all preserved in the Preserved Tablet (Al-Lawh Al-Mahfuz). As-Sa'di explains that this verse comes right after the mention of their denial to emphasize: they may deny, but their denial itself is recorded. They cannot escape the record. On the Day of Judgment, they will find every action, every word, and every intention documented with perfect accuracy.

30
فَذُوقُوا فَلَن نَّزِيدَكُمْ إِلَّا عَذَابًا
Fadhooqu falan nazeedakum illa 'adhaba
So taste [the penalty], and never will We increase you except in torment.

Word by Word

فَذُوقُواfadhooquSo taste

Simple Explanation

This is one of the most terrifying verses in the Quran. "Lan" is the strongest form of negation for the future in Arabic, meaning "absolutely never." Abdullah ibn Amr reported that the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "The most severe verse in the Quran upon the people of Hell is this verse." Ibn Kathir cites this narration and explains that not only will the punishment not decrease, it will only increase. There is no hope of relief, no light at the end of the tunnel. As-Sa'di notes that this verse closes the door of hope for the transgressors. In this world, even the worst suffering eventually ends. In Hell, it only gets worse.

31
إِنَّ لِلْمُتَّقِينَ مَفَازًا
Inna lil-muttaqeena mafaza
Indeed, for the righteous is attainment.

Word by Word

إِنَّinnaIndeed
لِلْمُتَّقِينَlil-muttaqeenaFor the righteous / God-fearing
مَفَازًاmafazaAttainment / success / triumph
Simple Explanation

After the terrifying description of Hell, the surah shifts to the beautiful reward of the believers. "Muttaqeen" are those who have taqwa, consciousness of Allah that leads them to obey Him and avoid sin. "Mafaz" means a place of success, triumph, and safety. Ibn Kathir explains that after warning about the punishment, Allah gives glad tidings to the righteous. They will achieve ultimate success: safety from Hell and entry into Paradise. As-Sa'di notes the beautiful contrast: while the transgressors face increasing torment, the righteous are promised triumph. The two outcomes could not be more different.

32
حَدَائِقَ وَأَعْنَابًا
Hada'iqa wa a'naba
Gardens and grapevines.

Word by Word

حَدَائِقَhada'iqaGardens / enclosed gardens
وَأَعْنَابًاwa a'nabaAnd grapevines
Simple Explanation

"Hada'iq" is the plural of "hadeeqah," meaning an enclosed, beautiful garden with trees, fruits, and flowing water. "A'nab" means grapes or grapevines. Ibn Kathir explains that these gardens are specifically enclosed, meaning they are private and exclusive for the righteous. Grapes are singled out because they were among the most prized fruits for the Arabs. As-Sa'di notes that the gardens of Paradise are beyond comparison to anything in this world. The Quran uses worldly terms so we can understand, but the reality of Paradise is far greater than anything we can imagine, as the hadith states: "What no eye has seen, no ear has heard, and no heart has conceived."

33
وَكَوَاعِبَ أَتْرَابًا
Wa kawa'iba atraba
And full-breasted [companions] of equal age.

Word by Word

وَكَوَاعِبَwa kawa'ibaAnd full-breasted companions
أَتْرَابًاatrabaOf equal age
Simple Explanation

"Kawa'ib" refers to young women in the prime of youth, and "atrab" means of equal, matching age. Ibn Kathir explains that this describes the companions of Paradise who are in the peak of their youth and beauty, all of the same age (some scholars say 33 years old, the age of the people of Paradise). As-Sa'di explains that among the rewards of Paradise are pure companions, and this is mentioned as one of the blessings that Allah has prepared for the righteous. The descriptions of Paradise are meant to motivate believers to strive for it through obedience and good deeds.

34
وَكَأْسًا دِهَاقًا
Wa ka'san dihaqa
And a full cup.

Word by Word

وَكَأْسًاwa ka'sanAnd a cup
دِهَاقًاdihaqaFull / overflowing
Simple Explanation

"Ka's" means a cup or goblet, and "dihaq" means full to the brim, overflowing, and continuously replenished. Ibn Kathir explains that their cups will always be full, never empty, filled with the pure drink of Paradise that brings joy without intoxication or hangover. As-Sa'di contrasts this with the drink of Hell: the people of Hell get scalding water and pus, while the people of Paradise get cups overflowing with pure, delightful drink. The "dihaq" (fullness) also symbolizes the completeness of Paradise's blessings: nothing is lacking, nothing is half-measured, everything is abundant.

35
لَّا يَسْمَعُونَ فِيهَا لَغْوًا وَلَا كِذَّابًا
La yasma'oona fiha laghwan wa la kidhdhaba
No ill speech will they hear therein or any falsehood.

Simple Explanation

"Laghw" is vain, empty, useless talk, gossip, backbiting, and all forms of harmful speech. "Kidhdhab" is lying and falsehood. In Paradise, neither exists. Ibn Kathir explains that the atmosphere of Paradise is pure. There is no sinful speech, no hurtful words, no rumors, and no deception. Everything spoken is true and beautiful. As-Sa'di makes a beautiful observation: much of the suffering in this world comes from harmful speech. Paradise removes this entirely. The people of Paradise will hear only words of peace, praise, and joy. This is a reminder for us in this world: guarding our tongues is a key to Paradise.

36
جَزَاءً مِّن رَّبِّكَ عَطَاءً حِسَابًا
Jaza'an mir-rabbika 'ata'an hisaba
[As] reward from your Lord, [a generous] gift [made due by] account.

Word by Word

جَزَاءًjaza'anA reward
مِّن رَّبِّكَmir-rabbikaFrom your Lord

حِسَابًاhisabaSufficient / calculated / abundant
Simple Explanation

"Ata'" means a gift or grant, and "hisab" here means sufficient or abundant, enough to satisfy completely. Ibn Kathir explains that the blessings of Paradise are both a reward for good deeds AND a generous gift from Allah's mercy. "Hisab" here means it is measured and calculated to be more than enough, abundant and satisfying. As-Sa'di beautifully explains that Allah calls it both "jaza'" (earned reward) and "'ata'" (a gift). This means that while the believers earned it through their faith and deeds, the reality is that no human's deeds truly "earn" Paradise. It is ultimately Allah's generous gift and mercy. This keeps the believer humble and grateful.

37
رَّبِّ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضِ وَمَا بَيْنَهُمَا الرَّحْمَـٰنِ ۖ لَا يَمْلِكُونَ مِنْهُ خِطَابًا
Rabbis-samawati wal-ardi wa ma baynahumar-Rahmani la yamlikoona minhu khitaba

Simple Explanation

This verse establishes Allah's absolute authority. He is the Lord of the heavens, the earth, and everything in between. He is "Ar-Rahman," the Most Merciful. Yet despite His mercy, no one has the authority to speak before Him on that Day without His permission. Ibn Kathir explains that on the Day of Judgment, no one, not even the angels, prophets, or any creation, will dare to speak without Allah's permission. As-Sa'di notes the profound combination of mercy and authority: He is the Most Merciful, yet He is also the absolute Sovereign. His mercy does not diminish His authority, and His authority does not diminish His mercy.

38
يَوْمَ يَقُومُ الرُّوحُ وَالْمَلَائِكَةُ صَفًّا ۖ لَّا يَتَكَلَّمُونَ إِلَّا مَنْ أَذِنَ لَهُ الرَّحْمَـٰنُ وَقَالَ صَوَابًا

"Ar-Rooh" (the Spirit) is Jibreel (Gabriel), peace be upon him, mentioned separately from the other angels to honor his elevated status. On that Day, even the mightiest angels stand in rows in complete silence. Ibn Kathir explains that Jibreel and all the angels will stand in rows before Allah, and none will dare speak except those whom Allah gives permission, and they will only say what is correct and true. As-Sa'di highlights the awe of this scene: if the angels, who have never disobeyed Allah for a single moment, stand in silent submission, how should a human being who has sinned their entire life feel about that Day? "Sawab" (correct) means only truth will be spoken. No false intercession, no lies, no excuses.

39

"Al-Haqq" means the Truth, the Real, the Certain. That Day is real, it is coming, and it is the ultimate truth. "Ma'ab" here means a path of return to Allah through obedience. Ibn Kathir explains that after establishing the reality of the Day of Judgment and describing both its terrors and rewards, Allah gives the choice: whoever wills may take a path back to Allah through faith and righteous deeds. The door is open now, in this life. As-Sa'di notes that "faman sha'a" (whoever wills) places the responsibility squarely on the individual. You have been warned, you have been shown the evidence, now the choice is yours. But this choice is only available in this life, not after death.

The surah closes with one of the most emotionally powerful endings in the Quran. The punishment is described as "qareeb" (near), because everything that is coming is near, and death can strike at any moment. On that Day, every person will see their deeds laid bare before them. The believer will see their good deeds and rejoice, while the disbeliever, upon seeing the horrifying record of their sins and the punishment awaiting them, will cry out: "Ya laytanee kuntu turaba!" (Oh, I wish I were dust!). Ibn Kathir explains that they will wish they had never been created, or that they could return to dust and cease to exist rather than face the punishment. As-Sa'di notes that this wish comes when they see the animals turned to dust after judgment between them is settled. The disbeliever, seeing this, will wish for the fate of the animals rather than face eternal punishment. This is the most desperate cry of regret, and it comes too late.

Key Lessons
  • 01
    Creation is proof of resurrection. The One who made the earth, mountains, heavens, sun, rain, and gardens from nothing can certainly bring the dead back to life. If you acknowledge Allah as Creator, you cannot logically deny His ability to resurrect.
  • 02
    Denying accountability is the root of all transgression. Verses 27-28 reveal that the people of Hell did not expect to be held accountable. When you remove the fear of consequences, you remove all moral restraint. Belief in the Last Day is what keeps a person grounded.
  • 03
    The punishment fits the crime perfectly. "Jaza'an wifaqa" (verse 26) means Allah's punishment is never unjust. It is precisely proportional to the sin. This is a reminder that Allah is both the Most Merciful and the Most Just.
  • 04
    Sleep is a daily proof of resurrection. Every night you "die" in sleep and every morning you are "resurrected." If Allah can restore your consciousness after sleep, He can restore your life after death. This daily experience should strengthen your faith.
  • 05
    Paradise is the absence of all suffering and the presence of all joy. No vain talk, no lies, overflowing cups, beautiful gardens. Paradise is not just about what you get; it is also about what you are freed from: pain, anxiety, conflict, and deception.
  • 06
    The choice is yours, but the window is limited. "Whoever wills may take a path to his Lord." The opportunity to choose the right path exists only in this life. After death, all choices are final. Every moment you delay is a moment closer to the deadline.
