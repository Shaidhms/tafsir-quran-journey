Nuh's Da'wah & His People's Rejection
The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Nuh is one of the most emotionally powerful surahs in the Quran. It is entirely dedicated to the story of Prophet Nuh (peace be upon him) and his tireless da'wah to his people. Unlike other surahs that mention Nuh briefly, this surah lets us hear his voice directly as he pours out his heart to Allah, recounting the decades of effort, rejection, and heartbreak he endured.

Nuh was sent to his people with a simple message: worship Allah alone, fear Him, and obey me. In return, Allah would forgive their sins and delay their punishment. But his people refused. They blocked their ears, covered their faces, and persisted in arrogance. Nuh tried every approach: he called them openly and privately, by night and by day, but nothing worked.

Ibn Kathir reports that Prophet Nuh (peace be upon him) is considered the first messenger sent to the people of the earth. He called his people for 950 years, as mentioned in Surah Al-Ankabut (29:14). Despite this incredibly long mission, only a handful believed in him. His patience and perseverance make him one of the five Prophets of firm resolve (Ulul-Azm).

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Introduction to Surah Nuh; Surah Al-Ankabut 29:14

In the middle section, Nuh uses a beautiful da'wah strategy, appealing to their self-interest. He tells them: seek forgiveness from Allah, and He will send rain, give you wealth and children, and make gardens and rivers for you. This connects repentance to worldly blessings, a profound lesson that istighfar (seeking forgiveness) is a key to rizq (provision).

He then points them to the signs of Allah in creation: the seven heavens in layers, the moon as a light, the sun as a lamp, how Allah grew humans from the earth like plants. Despite all of this, they stubbornly clung to their idols: Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr. The surah ends with Nuh's du'a against the disbelievers and his plea for forgiveness for himself, his parents, and the believers.

Simple Explanation

The surah opens with Allah declaring that He sent Nuh to his people with a clear mission: warn them before a painful punishment arrives. Ibn Kathir explains that "inna" (indeed) at the beginning emphasizes the certainty and importance of this divine mission. Nuh is the first messenger sent to humanity after they deviated from Tawhid (monotheism). As-Sa'di notes that the warning came "before" the punishment, showing Allah's mercy. He does not punish a people without first sending them a warner. The word "alim" (painful) describes a punishment that reaches into the depths of the soul.

2

Nuh begins his address with "ya qawmi" (O my people), a term of endearment and belonging. He does not distance himself from them. He identifies himself as a "nadhir mubin," a clear warner. Ibn Kathir explains that "mubin" means his warning is clear, plain, and leaves no room for confusion. As-Sa'di notes that Nuh's approach is gentle. He calls them "my people," establishing a bond before delivering the difficult message. This is a model of da'wah: connect first, then convey. He does not call them enemies or strangers; he reminds them that he is one of them and cares about them.

3
أَنِ اعْبُدُوا اللَّهَ وَاتَّقُوهُ وَأَطِيعُونِ
Ani'budul-laha wattaquhu wa ati'un
[Saying], "Worship Allah, fear Him, and obey me."

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

This is the core message of every prophet: worship Allah alone, have taqwa (consciousness of Allah and fear of His punishment), and obey the messenger. Ibn Kathir explains that these three commands summarize the entire religion. "U'budu" (worship) is about directing all acts of worship to Allah alone. "Ittaquhu" (fear Him) is about avoiding sins and disobedience. "Ati'un" (obey me) is about following the guidance the messenger brings. As-Sa'di adds that obedience to the messenger is linked to obedience to Allah because the messenger only conveys what Allah commands.

4

Nuh presents the reward of obedience: forgiveness of sins and an extended lifespan. Then he warns: once Allah's appointed time comes, there is no delay. Ibn Kathir explains that "min dhunubikum" (of your sins) uses "min" which some scholars interpret as meaning "all your sins" (min here being explanatory, not partitive). As-Sa'di explains that the "specified term" refers to their natural lifespan. If they believe, Allah will let them live out their full term. If they refuse, He may cut it short through punishment. The closing phrase "if you only knew" is a powerful nudge: if they truly understood the urgency, they would rush to believe.

5
قَالَ رَبِّ إِنِّي دَعَوْتُ قَوْمِي لَيْلًا وَنَهَارًا
Qala rabbi inni da'awtu qawmi laylan wa nahara
He said, "My Lord, indeed I invited my people [to truth] night and day."

Now Nuh turns to Allah and reports his efforts. He did not call his people occasionally or half-heartedly. He called them "laylan wa nahara" (night and day), meaning continuously, without break. Ibn Kathir explains that this verse begins Nuh's complaint to his Lord, not out of despair, but as a report of his tireless efforts. As-Sa'di notes that mentioning both night and day shows the completeness of his effort. He used every possible time. This is the model of a dedicated da'i (caller to Allah): persistent, consistent, never giving up despite rejection.

6
فَلَمْ يَزِدْهُمْ دُعَائِي إِلَّا فِرَارًا
Falam yazidhum du'a'i illa firara
"But my invitation increased them not except in flight [from the truth]."

Word by Word

فَلَمْfalamBut not

Simple Explanation

The heartbreaking result: the more Nuh called them, the more they ran away. "Firara" comes from "farra" meaning to flee, to run away. They did not just ignore him; they actively fled from the truth. Ibn Kathir explains that this is one of the saddest statements in the Quran. A prophet dedicates his entire life to his people, and the result is the opposite of what he hoped for. As-Sa'di notes that the problem was not Nuh's method but the hardness of their hearts. When hearts are sealed, even the most eloquent da'wah only drives them further away.

7
وَإِنِّي كُلَّمَا دَعَوْتُهُمْ لِتَغْفِرَ لَهُمْ جَعَلُوا أَصَابِعَهُمْ فِي آذَانِهِمْ وَاسْتَغْشَوْا ثِيَابَهُمْ وَأَصَرُّوا وَاسْتَكْبَرُوا اسْتِكْبَارًا
Wa inni kullama da'awtuhum litaghfira lahum ja'alu asabi'ahum fi adhanihim wastagshaw thiyabahum wa asarru wastakbarus-tikbara

Simple Explanation

This verse paints a vivid picture of their rejection. They physically blocked their ears with their fingers and pulled their clothes over their heads so they could neither hear nor see Nuh. Then they persisted in their disbelief and became arrogant. Ibn Kathir explains that "istighsha' thiyabahum" (covering with garments) means they covered their faces and heads so they would not see Nuh or hear his words. "Asarru" means they became stubborn and determined in their rejection. "Istakbaru istikbara" uses the cognate accusative (maf'ul mutlaq), emphasizing the extreme degree of their arrogance. As-Sa'di notes the escalation: first blocking ears (refusing to listen), then covering themselves (refusing to see), then persisting (refusing to change), then arrogance (actively opposing). Each stage is worse than the last.

8

Thumma inni da'awtuhum jihara

Nuh did not give up. When private calling failed, he switched to public proclamation. "Jihara" means openly, loudly, in public gatherings. Ibn Kathir explains that Nuh used public forums and gatherings to deliver his message so it could reach the maximum number of people. As-Sa'di notes that this shows a wise da'i adapts his methods. When one approach does not work, he tries another. Nuh's shift from private to public shows he was strategic, not just repetitive.

9

Nuh combined both approaches: public announcements and private, one-on-one conversations. "A'lantu" is public proclamation and "asrartu" is private, confidential advice. Ibn Kathir explains that Nuh left no method untried. He spoke publicly in gatherings and also approached individuals privately, hoping that a personal touch might soften their hearts. As-Sa'di adds that "israra" (with the cognate accusative) emphasizes the thoroughness of his private efforts. Some people are more receptive in private, away from the peer pressure of the crowd. Nuh understood this and adapted. This is a masterclass in da'wah methodology.

10
فَقُلْتُ اسْتَغْفِرُوا رَبَّكُمْ إِنَّهُ كَانَ غَفَّارًا
Faqultus-taghfiru rabbakum innahu kana ghaffara
"And said, 'Ask forgiveness of your Lord. Indeed, He is ever a Perpetual Forgiver.'"

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

Nuh shifts his approach. Instead of only warning about punishment, he appeals to their desires. Start with istighfar (seeking forgiveness), because Allah is "Ghaffar," an intensive form meaning He forgives abundantly, repeatedly, and always. Ibn Kathir explains that this verse begins one of the most famous passages in the Quran about the benefits of istighfar. "Ghaffar" is a sighah mubalaghah (intensive form) meaning Allah's forgiveness is not limited or occasional. He forgives continuously and abundantly. As-Sa'di notes that Nuh first established Allah's attribute of forgiveness so his people would feel hope, not despair. No matter how many sins they had committed, Allah's forgiveness could cover them all.

11
يُرْسِلِ السَّمَاءَ عَلَيْكُم مِّدْرَارًا
Yursilis-sama'a 'alaykum midrara
"He will send rain from the sky upon you in [continuing] showers."

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

The first worldly benefit of istighfar: rain. "Midrara" means abundant, continuous rain, not just a drizzle but showers that bring life to the land. Ibn Kathir explains that "as-sama'" here refers to rain (as the sky is the source of rain). "Midrara" is an intensive form meaning the rain will come abundantly and continuously. As-Sa'di connects this to a profound principle: istighfar (seeking forgiveness) is a direct cause of provision and blessings. When a community turns to Allah in repentance, He opens the gates of His mercy upon them. This is why scholars often recommend istighfar for those facing drought, financial hardship, or any difficulty.

12

More benefits of istighfar: wealth, children, gardens, and rivers. Nuh promises them everything they desire in this world as a result of turning to Allah. Gardens and rivers represent prosperity and abundance. As-Sa'di notes the comprehensive nature of these blessings: rain for agriculture (verse 11), wealth for trade and livelihood, children for legacy and strength, gardens for beauty and sustenance, and rivers for water and irrigation. All of this comes from one simple act: istighfar. This passage is frequently cited by scholars as evidence that seeking forgiveness brings both spiritual and material blessings.

13
مَّا لَكُمْ لَا تَرْجُونَ لِلَّهِ وَقَارًا

Simple Explanation

Nuh expresses astonishment: what is wrong with you that you do not recognize Allah's greatness? "Waqar" means grandeur, dignity, and reverence. Ibn Kathir explains that "tarjuna" here carries the meaning of recognizing, expecting, and hoping. The question is rhetorical, expressing disbelief at their attitude. Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) interpreted this as: "What is wrong with you that you do not glorify Allah as He deserves to be glorified?" As-Sa'di explains that this verse rebukes their lack of awe before Allah. How can someone who sees the heavens, the earth, and all of creation not feel a sense of reverence for the One who created it all?

14
وَقَدْ خَلَقَكُمْ أَطْوَارًا
Wa qad khalaqakum atwara
"While He has created you in stages."

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

Allah created humans through stages: a drop of fluid, a clinging clot, a lump of flesh, then bones, then clothed with flesh, then a new creation. "Atwara" is the plural of "tawr" meaning a stage or phase. Ibn Kathir explains that this refers to the stages of creation in the womb: nutfah (sperm-drop), alaqah (clot), mudghah (lump), then formation of bones, flesh, and spirit. As-Sa'di points out that the fact that Allah created you through these intricate, progressive stages is itself proof of His power and wisdom. Each stage is a miracle. How can you witness this and still not acknowledge His grandeur?

15

Nuh now directs their attention to the cosmos. "Tibaqa" means layered one on top of another, perfectly aligned. Ibn Kathir explains that the seven heavens are stacked in layers, each one above the other, with a vast distance between them. This is a sign of Allah's immense power: creating structures of such magnitude and perfection. As-Sa'di notes that Nuh uses the rhetorical question "alam taraw" (do you not see?) to make them look up and think. The evidence is right above their heads. Seven heavens, perfectly constructed, without any flaw or crack. This is not random; it is the work of an all-powerful Creator.

16
وَجَعَلَ الْقَمَرَ فِيهِنَّ نُورًا وَجَعَلَ الشَّمْسَ سِرَاجًا

The moon is described as "nur" (light) and the sun as "siraj" (lamp). These are two different Arabic words with distinct meanings. Ibn Kathir explains that "nur" refers to reflected, cool light, while "siraj" refers to a blazing, self-generating light source. Remarkably, modern science confirms that the moon reflects light while the sun generates its own. As-Sa'di notes that the precision of Quranic language is itself a miracle. The Quran never calls the moon a "siraj" or the sun a "nur." Each word is chosen with divine precision. The moon lights up the night gently for travelers and worshippers, while the sun provides heat and energy for life during the day.

17
وَاللَّهُ أَنبَتَكُم مِّنَ الْأَرْضِ نَبَاتًا

Allah caused humans to grow from the earth like plants grow from soil. "Anbatakum" uses the verb for plant growth applied to humans. Ibn Kathir explains that this refers to the creation of Adam (peace be upon him) from clay/earth, and by extension all of humanity, since our sustenance comes from the earth. The food we eat comes from the soil, becomes part of us, and forms the very cells of our bodies. As-Sa'di notes the beautiful metaphor: just as plants grow from the earth through stages (seed, sprout, stem, leaves, fruit), humans also grow through stages. The earth is our origin and our sustenance. This should make us humble.

18

The cycle of life: from the earth you came, to the earth you return (burial), and from the earth you will be brought out again (resurrection). Ibn Kathir explains that "yu'idukum fiha" refers to burial after death, and "yukhrijukum" refers to resurrection on the Day of Judgment. "Ikhraja" (with the cognate accusative) emphasizes the certainty and completeness of this extraction. As-Sa'di notes the beautiful symmetry: earth is our origin, our temporary resting place, and the place from which we will be resurrected. This three-stage connection to the earth reminds us that we are not permanent residents of this world. We are travelers passing through.

19
وَاللَّهُ جَعَلَ لَكُمُ الْأَرْضَ بِسَاطًا
Wallahu ja'ala lakumul-arda bisata
"And Allah has made for you the earth an expanse."

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

Allah spread the earth out like a carpet for humans to live on. "Bisata" comes from "basat" meaning to spread out, like spreading a carpet or a mat. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah made the earth stable and flat enough for human habitation. It is firm under your feet, not shaking or tilting, so you can build homes, plant crops, and travel. As-Sa'di notes that the earth being an "expanse" is a blessing we take for granted. Imagine if the earth were unstable, or too mountainous to traverse, or constantly shifting. Allah designed it perfectly for human life.

20
لِّتَسْلُكُوا مِنْهَا سُبُلًا فِجَاجًا
Litasluku minha subulan fijaja
"That you may follow therein roads of passage."

Simple Explanation

The purpose of the earth being spread out: so you can travel through wide roads and passages. "Fijaj" means wide, spacious paths between mountains and valleys. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah placed natural pathways through mountains and between lands so people could travel for trade, pilgrimage, and seeking knowledge. As-Sa'di notes that "subul fijaja" (wide roads) highlights how Allah made travel possible and even comfortable. The earth is not just habitable; it is traversable. This is another blessing that points to Allah's care and planning for human needs.

21
قَالَ نُوحٌ رَّبِّ إِنَّهُمْ عَصَوْنِي وَاتَّبَعُوا مَن لَّمْ يَزِدْهُ مَالُهُ وَوَلَدُهُ إِلَّا خَسَارًا
Qala Nuhur-rabbi innahum 'asawni wattaba'u man lam yazidhu maluhu wa waladuhu illa khasara

Nuh reports to Allah: my people disobeyed me and followed wealthy leaders whose wealth and children only increased them in loss. "Khasar" means loss, ruin, destruction. Ibn Kathir explains that the common people followed the elite and wealthy among them, thinking their wealth and status meant they were on the right path. But in reality, their wealth and children only increased their arrogance and distanced them further from the truth. As-Sa'di notes a powerful lesson: wealth and children are not signs of Allah's pleasure. They can be a test that leads to ruin if they distract a person from Allah's obedience. The very things they thought were blessings became curses because they used them to oppose the truth.

22
وَمَكَرُوا مَكْرًا كُبَّارًا
Wa makaru makran kubbara
"And they conspired an immense conspiracy."

Word by Word

وَمَكَرُواwa makaruAnd they conspired
مَكْرًاmakranA conspiracy / plot
كُبَّارًاkubbaraImmense / enormous
Simple Explanation

They did not just passively reject; they actively plotted against Nuh and the message. "Kubbara" is an intensified form of "kabir" (big), meaning their conspiracy was enormous, far-reaching, and deeply rooted. Ibn Kathir explains that their "makr" (plotting) included threatening believers, spreading propaganda against Nuh, and using their social influence to prevent people from listening to him. As-Sa'di notes that "kubbara" is the most intensive form possible in Arabic, beyond "kabir" and even "akbar." This tells us their plotting was of the highest degree. They pulled out every trick to fight the truth.

23
وَقَالُوا لَا تَذَرُنَّ آلِهَتَكُمْ وَلَا تَذَرُنَّ وَدًّا وَلَا سُوَاعًا وَلَا يَغُوثَ وَيَعُوقَ وَنَسْرًا
Wa qalu la tadharunna alihatakum wa la tadharunna Waddan wa la Suwa'an wa la Yaghuth wa Ya'uqa wa Nasra
"And said, 'Never leave your gods and never leave Wadd or Suwa' or Yaghuth and Ya'uq and Nasr.'"

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

The leaders rallied their people to never abandon their idols, naming five specific ones: Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr. Ibn Kathir narrates from Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) that these were originally names of righteous people from the generation before Nuh. After they died, Shaytan whispered to the people to make images of them to "remember their worship." Over time, the next generation forgot the original purpose and began worshipping the statues themselves. This is reported in Sahih Al-Bukhari (Hadith 4920). As-Sa'di explains that this is the origin story of idol worship on earth. It began with exaggerating the status of righteous people, then making images of them, and finally worshipping those images. This is why Islam strictly forbids making images of prophets or righteous people.

24
وَقَدْ أَضَلُّوا كَثِيرًا ۖ وَلَا تَزِدِ الظَّالِمِينَ إِلَّا ضَلَالًا
Wa qad adallu kathiran wa la tazidiz-zalimina illa dalala
"And already they have misled many. And, [my Lord], do not increase the wrongdoers except in error."

Simple Explanation

The idols (and the leaders who promoted them) have already misled many people. Nuh now makes a du'a against the wrongdoers: increase them only in misguidance. This is a du'a of a prophet who has exhausted every effort over centuries. Ibn Kathir explains that "adallu kathira" shows the idols were not a minor issue; they corrupted many generations. Nuh's du'a "do not increase them except in error" came after 950 years of patient da'wah. This was not a hasty curse but a considered plea after all hope of their guidance was lost. As-Sa'di explains that a prophet does not make such a du'a lightly. Nuh only made this du'a after Allah informed him that no more of his people would believe (as mentioned in Surah Hud 11:36).

25
مِّمَّا خَطِيئَاتِهِمْ أُغْرِقُوا فَأُدْخِلُوا نَارًا فَلَمْ يَجِدُوا لَهُم مِّن دُونِ اللَّهِ أَنصَارًا
Mimma khati'atihim ughriqu fa-udkhilu naran falam yajidu lahum min dunil-lahi ansara
"Because of their sins they were drowned and put into the Fire, and they found not for themselves besides Allah [any] helpers."

The consequence: they were drowned in the flood and then entered the Fire. Note the swift transition from drowning to Fire, using "fa" (then immediately). Ibn Kathir explains that "ughriqu" (they were drowned) refers to the Great Flood that destroyed Nuh's people. "Fa-udkhilu nara" (then they were entered into Fire) refers to the punishment of the grave (Barzakh), which begins immediately after death. The use of past tense for future events indicates their certainty. As-Sa'di notes the devastating conclusion: "they found no helpers besides Allah." Their idols, their wealth, their leaders, their children, none of these could save them. When Allah's punishment came, every false support system collapsed.

26
وَقَالَ نُوحٌ رَّبِّ لَا تَذَرْ عَلَى الْأَرْضِ مِنَ الْكَافِرِينَ دَيَّارًا

Nuh makes the ultimate du'a: do not leave a single disbeliever alive on earth. "Dayyar" comes from "dar" (home) and means even a single dweller or inhabitant. Ibn Kathir explains that this du'a came only after Allah revealed to Nuh: "None of your people will believe except those who have already believed" (Hud 11:36). With this divine knowledge, Nuh knew that further da'wah would be futile and that the remaining disbelievers would only corrupt future generations. As-Sa'di adds that Nuh did not make this du'a out of personal anger but out of protective concern for the future of monotheism on earth, as he explains in the next verse.

27

Ibn Kathir explains that Nuh knew from Allah's revelation that no more people would believe, and that those who remained would actively work to mislead the few believers. Their children would be raised on shirk and kufr. As-Sa'di notes that Nuh's concern was not personal but for the ummah (community). He was protecting the future of Tawhid on earth. After 950 years, he knew this generation was beyond hope, and leaving them would only poison future generations.

28

Simple Explanation

The surah closes with one of the most comprehensive du'as in the Quran. Nuh asks forgiveness for himself first (showing humility even though he is a prophet), then his parents, then whoever enters his house as a believer, then all believing men and women until the Day of Judgment, and finally asks that the wrongdoers be increased only in destruction. Ibn Kathir explains that "baytiya" (my house) can mean his actual home, his masjid, or the ark (the ship that carried the believers). "Tabara" means complete destruction and ruin. As-Sa'di notes the beautiful structure of this du'a: it starts with the individual (himself), expands to family (parents), then to the immediate community (his household), then to the entire ummah of believers (all believing men and women of all time). This teaches us to include others in our du'as and to pray for all believers. The surah begins with a mission of mercy (warning before punishment) and ends with a du'a of mercy (forgiveness for believers) and justice (destruction for oppressors).

Key Lessons
  • 01
    Patience in da'wah is a prophetic quality. Nuh called his people for 950 years without giving up. He tried every method: public, private, night, day, warnings, incentives. This is the ultimate model of persistence. If you are calling people to good, never measure success by immediate results.
  • 02
    Istighfar is the key to worldly and spiritual blessings. Verses 10-12 teach that seeking forgiveness from Allah brings rain, wealth, children, gardens, and rivers. Scholars throughout history have recommended istighfar for anyone facing difficulty, poverty, or drought. This is one of the most practical lessons in the entire Quran.
  • 03
    Arrogance is the deadliest spiritual disease. Nuh's people did not reject the truth because of lack of evidence. They rejected it out of arrogance, stubbornly covering their ears and faces. Arrogance prevents a person from accepting the truth even when it is clear and obvious.
  • 04
    Wealth and status are not signs of truth. The people followed wealthy leaders whose riches only increased them in loss. In every age, people are tempted to follow the rich and powerful, assuming their success means they are right. This surah corrects that misconception.
  • 05
    Idol worship began with exaggerating righteous people. The five idols mentioned were originally names of righteous men. Shaytan led people to make images of them, then to worship those images. This is why Islam prohibits making images of prophets and righteous people, and why excessive praise of anyone besides Allah is dangerous.
  • 06
    Include all believers in your du'as. Nuh's final du'a includes himself, his parents, his household, and all believing men and women of all time. This teaches generosity in supplication and reminds us that we are part of a larger ummah that transcends time and place.
