The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Mutaffifin was revealed in Makkah and addresses one of the most practical and widespread sins: cheating in business transactions. The word "mutaffifin" comes from "tatfif," meaning to give less than what is due. It refers to those who demand their full rights when buying but shortchange others when selling. This surah connects dishonest business practices directly to denial of the Day of Judgment.

The surah opens with a powerful warning: "Woe to the defrauders!" This is not just about marketplace cheating. Scholars explain it covers every form of taking more than you deserve and giving less than what you owe, whether in business, relationships, or dealings with Allah Himself (taking His blessings but not fulfilling His commands).

Ibn Kathir reports that when the Prophet (peace be upon him) arrived in Madinah, the people there were among the worst in giving short measure. Then Allah revealed this surah, and they reformed their business practices. Some scholars consider this surah to be among the last Makkan revelations or early Madinan, as it addresses both the Makkan themes of resurrection and the Madinan concern with social justice.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Introduction to Surah Al-Mutaffifin; An-Nasa'i

The middle section introduces two profound concepts: Sijjin (the record of the wicked, kept in the lowest place) and Illiyyun (the record of the righteous, kept in the highest place). The wicked denied the Day of Judgment and are sealed off from their Lord, while the righteous are rewarded with luxury, reclining on couches and drinking sealed nectar.

The surah concludes with a stunning reversal: in this world, the disbelievers used to laugh at the believers, mock them, and wink at each other in ridicule. But on the Day of Judgment, it is the believers who will be on couches, looking at the disbelievers and laughing. The final verse asks the rhetorical question: have the disbelievers been repaid for what they used to do? Justice is served, perfectly and completely.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَيْلٌ لِّلْمُطَفِّفِينَ
Waylul-lil-mutaffifin
Woe to those who give less [than due].

Word by Word

وَيْلٌwaylunWoe / destruction
لِّلْمُطَفِّفِينَlil-mutaffifinTo the defrauders
Simple Explanation

"Wayl" is one of the most severe words of warning in the Quran. Ibn Kathir explains that some scholars say "Wayl" is a valley in Hellfire where the pus and blood of the people of the Fire flow. Others say it simply means destruction and loss. "Mutaffifin" comes from "tatfif," which means to give something small, deficient, or less than what is due. As-Sa'di explains that this refers to those who cheat in weights and measures, taking full measure when they receive from others but giving less when they measure or weigh for others. The surah opens with the most severe warning for what might seem like a "small" sin, showing that dishonesty in dealings is no small matter with Allah.

2

"Iktalu" means to measure or weigh goods when receiving them. "Yastawfun" means they demand every last bit, they take their full right and more. Ibn Kathir explains that when these people are buying, they make sure they get every grain, every drop, every ounce owed to them. They are meticulous about their own rights. As-Sa'di points out the hypocrisy: they know the concept of full measure perfectly well when it benefits them. The problem is not ignorance; it is greed and selfishness. They apply one standard when receiving and another when giving.

3

But when they are the ones measuring or weighing for others (selling), they deliberately give less. "Yukhsirun" comes from "khasara" meaning loss. They cause others to lose by giving them short measure. Ibn Kathir explains that this verse exposes the double standard: full rights for themselves, shortchanged rights for others. As-Sa'di broadens this principle beyond the marketplace. He says this applies to anyone who demands their full rights from others but fails to give others their due. This includes employers who demand full work but withhold fair wages, and even those who expect respect and kindness from others but fail to reciprocate.

4

This is a rhetorical question dripping with astonishment. "Do these cheaters not realize they will be raised from their graves?" Ibn Kathir explains that the root cause of their fraud is disbelief in the afterlife. If they truly believed they would stand before Allah and answer for every transaction, they would never dare cheat. As-Sa'di makes a powerful connection: fraud and dishonesty are symptoms of a deeper disease, the denial of accountability. When a person truly believes in the Day of Judgment, it transforms every dealing they have. This verse links economics directly to theology.

5
لِيَوْمٍ عَظِيمٍ
Li-yawmin 'azim
For a tremendous Day.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

"Azim" means great, mighty, and tremendous. This is no ordinary day. Ibn Kathir explains that calling it "azim" (tremendous) is meant to shake the listener. It is a Day so immense in its terror and magnitude that it dwarfs everything else. As-Sa'di notes that the use of the indefinite form "yawmin" (a day) rather than the definite "al-yawm" (the day) adds to the sense of awe. It is as if language itself cannot fully define or contain the magnitude of that Day. The cheaters thought they were being clever. But cleverness will not help when the scales of that tremendous Day are set up.

6

All of humanity will stand before Allah, the Lord of all worlds. Ibn Kathir cites a hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him) reported in Sahih Bukhari and Muslim: "People will stand on the Day of Resurrection in their sweat up to the middle of their ears." This standing will last for 50,000 years. As-Sa'di explains that saying "Lord of the worlds" is significant. He is not just Lord of humans, but of everything that exists. The One before whom you will stand owns and controls every realm of existence. The same person who cheated for a few extra coins will stand barefoot and naked before the Master of the entire universe.

7

"Kalla" is a strong word of rebuke, like saying "Absolutely not! Stop and pay attention!" Now the surah shifts to the records of deeds. "Fujjar" are the wicked, sinful people. Their record is in "Sijjin." Ibn Kathir explains that "Sijjin" comes from "sijn" meaning prison. It is the lowest, most confined, most constricting place. Some scholars say it is a register kept beneath the seventh earth, or the lowest level of the earth. As-Sa'di explains that the location of the record reflects the status of the person. The wicked person's record is thrown down to the lowest possible place, matching their low and debased choices in life.

8
وَمَا أَدْرَاكَ مَا سِجِّينٌ
Wa ma adraka ma sijjin
And what can make you know what is Sijjin?

Word by Word

مَاmaWhat is

Simple Explanation

This is a classic Quranic rhetorical device used to magnify something beyond human comprehension. "What can make you know what Sijjin is?" means it is so terrible that no human description can do it justice. Ibn Kathir explains that whenever the Quran uses this phrasing ("wa ma adraka"), it indicates something of immense magnitude that is beyond the listener's current knowledge. As-Sa'di notes that this builds suspense and dread. Before even explaining what Sijjin is, the Quran tells you that it is beyond your ability to fully grasp. This is meant to instill fear in the hearts of those who dare to cheat and sin.

9
كِتَابٌ مَّرْقُومٌ
Kitabum-marqum
It is [their destination] a register inscribed.

Word by Word

كِتَابٌkitabunA register / book
مَّرْقُومٌmarqumInscribed / written
Simple Explanation

"Marqum" means inscribed, written, and permanently recorded. Nothing can be erased or changed. Ibn Kathir explains that this is a comprehensive record that contains every deed, every word, and every intention of the wicked. It is inscribed in a way that is permanent and irrefutable. As-Sa'di explains that "marqum" also carries the meaning of being sealed and finalized. There is no editing, no revision, no appeal. Every act of fraud, every lie, every moment of arrogance is permanently inscribed in this register. The cheaters thought no one was watching. But every single transaction was being recorded.

10
وَيْلٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ لِّلْمُكَذِّبِينَ
Wayluy-yawma'idhil-lil-mukadhdhibin
Woe, that Day, to the deniers.

Word by Word

وَيْلٌwaylunWoe / destruction
يَوْمَئِذٍyawma'idhinThat Day
لِّلْمُكَذِّبِينَlil-mukadhdhibinTo the deniers
Simple Explanation

"Wayl" returns again, this time specifically for "that Day." The "mukadhdhibin" are those who deny the truth, who call the Day of Judgment a lie. Ibn Kathir explains that the "wayl" that was general in verse 1 is now made specific to the Day of Judgment. On that Day, the destruction will actually arrive. In this world, "wayl" is a warning. On that Day, it becomes reality. As-Sa'di connects this back to the defrauders: the reason they cheated was because they denied this Day. Now that the Day has arrived, their denial cannot protect them. The very thing they said would never happen is happening, and there is no escape.

11
الَّذِينَ يُكَذِّبُونَ بِيَوْمِ الدِّينِ

"Yawm ad-Din" is the Day of Recompense, the Day when every deed is repaid in full. "Din" here means recompense, repayment, and judgment. Ibn Kathir explains that these people actively deny this Day. It is not that they are unsure; they call it a lie. They mock the very idea that they will be held accountable. As-Sa'di notes the irony: the defrauders cheated in their measurements, and now they will face the ultimate measurement. Allah's scales are perfect. Not a single atom's weight of good or evil will be missed. The Day they denied is the Day that will settle all accounts.

12

Two devastating descriptions: "mu'tadin" (one who transgresses boundaries, who crosses every limit) and "athim" (deeply sinful, one who is immersed in sin). Ibn Kathir explains that only the most transgressive and sinful people deny the Day of Judgment. A reasonable, fair-minded person would accept the truth. But the transgressor's sins have become so heavy that accepting accountability would mean confronting their own wickedness, so they choose denial instead. As-Sa'di explains that sin leads to more sin: transgression leads to denial, and denial leads to more transgression. It is a vicious cycle where each sin makes the next one easier.

13

"Asatir al-Awwalin" means "tales of the ancients" or "legends of old." This was the standard dismissal used by the Quraysh when the Quran was recited to them. Instead of reflecting on its message, they waved it away as fairy tales. Ibn Kathir explains that this response shows the ultimate arrogance: when the Creator of the universe speaks to you through His revelation, you dismiss it as old stories. As-Sa'di notes that their response was not based on evidence or reasoning. They did not examine the Quran and find it false. They simply did not want to hear it because accepting it would mean changing their ways and giving up their sins.

14

This is one of the most important verses about the spiritual heart. "Rana" means a stain, rust, or covering that accumulates. The Prophet (peace be upon him) explained this verse in a hadith reported by At-Tirmidhi and Ibn Majah: "When a servant commits a sin, a black dot is placed on his heart. If he repents and seeks forgiveness, his heart is polished. But if he sins again, another dot is added until the blackness covers the entire heart. That is the 'ran' (stain) that Allah mentioned." Ibn Kathir explains that their sins have accumulated so much that their hearts are completely sealed. They cannot see truth even when it is right in front of them. As-Sa'di warns that this is the greatest danger of persistent sin: it does not just damage your deeds, it damages your ability to perceive truth itself.

15

This is considered one of the most devastating punishments described in the Quran. "Mahjubun" means veiled, blocked, screened off. They will be prevented from seeing Allah. Imam Ash-Shafi'i said about this verse: "When He veiled these people (the wicked) out of His anger, this indicates that those people (the believers) will see Him out of His pleasure." Ibn Kathir explains that being denied the sight of Allah is the severest form of punishment, just as seeing Allah is the greatest reward in Paradise. As-Sa'di notes the perfect justice: they chose to be veiled from Allah in this life by ignoring His signs and commands, so they are veiled from Him on the Day of Judgment. Their punishment matches their crime exactly.

16

"Thumma" (then) indicates sequence: first they are veiled from Allah's sight, then they are thrown into the Hellfire. Ibn Kathir explains that being veiled from Allah's mercy is the spiritual punishment, and the Hellfire is the physical punishment. They receive both. As-Sa'di notes that "lasalu" contains emphasis (the "la" is for emphasis), meaning they will certainly and inevitably enter it. There is no doubt, no negotiation, and no escape. The defrauders who thought they were getting away with a few extra coins will now pay a price that never ends.

Adding insult to injury. After being veiled from Allah and thrown into the Fire, they are told: "This is what you used to deny." Ibn Kathir explains that this statement is said to increase their humiliation and regret. As-Sa'di notes that the passive voice "yuqalu" (it will be said) without specifying who says it adds to the universality of the condemnation. The angels, the record, the very reality around them all testify against them. There is nowhere to hide from the truth they spent their lives running from.

18

Now the surah shifts to the beautiful contrast. After describing the fate of the wicked (Sijjin), it introduces the record of the righteous: 'Illiyyun. "Abrar" means the righteous, the pious, those who are true to their word and their Lord. "'Illiyyun" comes from "'uluw" meaning height and elevation. Ibn Kathir explains it is the highest, most exalted place, some say it is in the seventh heaven, or beneath the Throne of Allah. As-Sa'di notes the stunning symmetry: the wicked are in Sijjin (the lowest prison) and the righteous are in 'Illiyyun (the highest elevation). Where your record is kept reflects where your soul belongs. Your deeds determine your rank.

The same rhetorical device used for Sijjin (verse 8) is now used for 'Illiyyun, but this time to magnify honor rather than dread. "What can make you know what 'Illiyyun is?" Just as the horror of Sijjin was beyond description, so too is the glory of 'Illiyyun beyond description. Ibn Kathir explains that 'Illiyyun is so lofty and magnificent that human language fails to capture it. As-Sa'di notes the beautiful parallel structure: the Quran uses the same format to describe both, showing that the consequences of good and evil are both beyond our current comprehension. But one leads to unimaginable honor, and the other to unimaginable horror.

The same phrase used in verse 9 for the record of the wicked is used here for the righteous, but the location is entirely different. Both records are "marqum" (inscribed, permanent, unchangeable), but one is in the lowest depths and the other in the highest heights. Ibn Kathir explains that the record of the righteous is inscribed with every good deed, every act of charity, every prayer, every moment of patience. As-Sa'di highlights that the permanence of this record is good news for the believer: your good deeds are permanently recorded. They will never be lost, forgotten, or diminished. Every sacrifice you made for Allah's sake is safely preserved in the most honored place.

21

"Al-Muqarrabun" are those who are brought nearest to Allah. This refers to the highest-ranking angels. Ibn Kathir explains that the record of the righteous in 'Illiyyun is witnessed and attested to by the angels who are closest to Allah. This is an honor beyond comprehension: the most noble creation of Allah bears witness to your deeds. As-Sa'di explains that this witnessing serves as both a guarantee and an honor. It guarantees that the record is authentic and protected, and it honors the believer by having the most exalted beings as witnesses to their righteousness. While the wicked had their record thrown to the lowest place, the righteous have theirs honored by the highest company.

The "la" in "lafi" adds emphasis: they are certainly, absolutely, without any doubt in this bliss.

Simple Explanation

"Ara'ik" (singular: arikah) are luxurious couches adorned with canopies and decorations. They are the couches of royalty and honor. Ibn Kathir explains that the righteous will recline on these magnificent couches, looking at the blessings and beauty of Paradise all around them. "Yanzurun" (looking/observing) is left open-ended. Some scholars say they are looking at the beauty of Paradise, others say they are looking at the face of Allah (the greatest pleasure), and others connect it to verse 34 where they look at the disbelievers. As-Sa'di notes that the image of reclining on couches and observing conveys complete peace, security, and contentment. There is no fear, no anxiety, no rush. Just eternal relaxation and enjoyment.

24
تَعْرِفُ فِي وُجُوهِهِمْ نَضْرَةَ النَّعِيمِ
Ta'rifu fi wujuhihim nadhratan-na'im
You will recognize in their faces the radiance of pleasure.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

"Nadhrah" means a radiant glow, a freshness, a beauty that shines from within. You can see their joy on their faces. Ibn Kathir explains that the bliss of Paradise will be so complete that it shows physically on their faces. Their faces will glow with beauty, light, and happiness. As-Sa'di explains that this is the opposite of what happens to the wicked (whose faces are covered in dust and darkness, as mentioned in other surahs). The inner state of a person is reflected outwardly. Just as sin darkens the face and heart, righteousness and joy illuminate them. The believers will be so thoroughly blessed that anyone who looks at them can instantly recognize the radiance of divine pleasure upon them.

25
يُسْقَوْنَ مِن رَّحِيقٍ مَّخْتُومٍ
Yusqawna mir-rahiqim-makhtum
They will be given to drink [pure] wine, sealed.

Word by Word

يُسْقَوْنَyusqawnaThey will be given to drink
مِنminFrom

Simple Explanation

"Rahiq" is the purest, finest wine. "Makhtum" means sealed, untouched, preserved. Ibn Kathir explains that this wine of Paradise is nothing like the wine of this world. It does not intoxicate, does not cause headaches, and does not lead to sin. It is pure pleasure and delight. It is sealed, meaning it has been preserved in perfection, never opened or contaminated. As-Sa'di explains that the sealing indicates exclusivity and honor. Just as the finest wines in this world are sealed and reserved for special occasions, the wine of Paradise has been sealed and reserved for the righteous since the beginning of creation. They abstained from the forbidden wine of this world, so they are rewarded with the purest nectar of Paradise.

26
خِتَامُهُ مِسْكٌ ۚ وَفِي ذَٰلِكَ فَلْيَتَنَافَسِ الْمُتَنَافِسُونَ
Khitamuhu misk. Wa fi dhalika falyatanafasil-mutanafisun
The last of it is musk. So for this let the competitors compete.

Word by Word

خِتَامُهُkhitamuhuIts seal / last taste

Simple Explanation

"Khitamuhu misk" means the seal or the aftertaste of this wine is musk, the most exquisite fragrance. Even the last drop leaves the scent of musk. Then comes one of the most motivating phrases in the Quran: "Wa fi dhalika falyatanafasil-mutanafisun" - "For this let the competitors compete!" Ibn Kathir explains that this is a call to action: if you want to compete for something, compete for this! Not for worldly possessions that perish, but for the eternal pleasures of Paradise. As-Sa'di says this verse redirects human ambition. People compete for wealth, status, and worldly pleasures, but the true competition is for the rewards of the Hereafter. This is the race worth running, the prize worth fighting for.

27
وَمِزَاجُهُ مِن تَسْنِيمٍ
Wa mizajuhu min tasnim
And its mixture is of Tasnim.

Word by Word

وَمِزَاجُهُwa mizajuhuAnd its mixture

Simple Explanation

"Mizaj" means mixture or blend. This sealed wine is mixed with water from Tasnim. "Tasnim" comes from "sanama" meaning to elevate or rise high. Ibn Kathir explains that Tasnim is the name of a spring in Paradise, the highest and most noble spring. It flows from above, from the highest point of Paradise. As-Sa'di explains that this wine is not ordinary but is blended with the water of the most elevated spring, making it the most exquisite drink imaginable. Its name alone (Tasnim, meaning elevation) tells you about its quality and status. The next verse will explain who drinks from this spring directly.

28
عَيْنًا يَشْرَبُ بِهَا الْمُقَرَّبُونَ
'Aynay-yashrabu bihal-muqarrabun
A spring from which those near [to Allah] drink.

Word by Word

عَيْنًا'aynanA spring
يَشْرَبُyashrabuDrink

Simple Explanation

"Al-Muqarrabun" here refers to those closest to Allah among the believers, the highest rank of the people of Paradise. Ibn Kathir explains that while the general righteous (al-abrar) drink the wine mixed with Tasnim, the elite (al-muqarrabun) drink from Tasnim directly, pure and unmixed. As-Sa'di explains this reveals a hierarchy in Paradise. Even within Paradise, there are levels and degrees. The closer you were to Allah in this life through worship, sincerity, and sacrifice, the higher your rank and the more direct your access to Paradise's greatest blessings. This motivates the believer to strive for the highest level, not just entry into Paradise.

29
إِنَّ الَّذِينَ أَجْرَمُوا كَانُوا مِنَ الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا يَضْحَكُونَ

Now the surah describes a scene from this worldly life. The criminals, the disbelievers, used to laugh at the believers. They mocked them for their faith, their prayers, their modesty, and their trust in the unseen. Ibn Kathir explains that this refers to the general pattern of the disbelievers of Makkah who ridiculed the early Muslims. They made fun of the believers for being poor, weak, and "foolish" enough to believe in the afterlife. As-Sa'di notes that this laughing was born from arrogance and delusion. They thought their worldly power and wealth proved them right and the believers wrong. But the last laugh belongs to someone else entirely, as the coming verses will show.

30
وَإِذَا مَرُّوا بِهِمْ يَتَغَامَزُونَ

And when they passed by them, they would exchange derisive glances.

Simple Explanation

"Yataghamazun" comes from "ghamz" meaning to wink, nudge, or signal with the eyes. When the disbelievers would walk past the believers, they would wink at each other, nudge each other, and exchange mocking glances. It is the behavior of bullies: making someone the target of inside jokes. Ibn Kathir explains that this was a specific behavior of the Quraysh. When they would see the poor Muslims like Bilal, Ammar, and Suhayb, they would signal to each other with contempt. As-Sa'di points out that this behavior reveals deep insecurity. Truly confident people do not need to mock others. The disbelievers' need to constantly ridicule the believers showed that the believers' faith bothered them deeply.

31
وَإِذَا انقَلَبُوا إِلَىٰ أَهْلِهِمُ انقَلَبُوا فَكِهِينَ

"Fakihin" means amusing themselves, entertained, finding it all very funny. When these mockers went home to their families, they would entertain them with stories about the believers, making jokes at their expense. Ibn Kathir explains that mocking the believers was their entertainment. They had nothing better to do with their gatherings than ridicule those who worshipped Allah. As-Sa'di notes the irony: they went home "fakihin" (amused), but true joy belongs to the believers. Their amusement was temporary and based on falsehood. Meanwhile, the believers they mocked had something these mockers could never have: inner peace and a connection with their Creator.

32
وَإِذَا رَأَوْهُمْ قَالُوا إِنَّ هَـٰؤُلَاءِ لَضَالُّونَ

The ultimate projection: the ones who are actually lost call the guided ones "lost." "Dallun" means those who are astray, who have gone off the right path. The disbelievers looked at the believers and genuinely believed that faith, prayer, and obedience to Allah were signs of being misguided. Ibn Kathir explains that this is the way of every era's arrogant people: they see the truth-followers as foolish and themselves as wise. As-Sa'di makes a piercing observation: the disbelievers pronounced judgment on the believers without any authority. No one asked them. No one appointed them as judges. Yet they confidently declared the believers "lost" based on nothing but their own ignorance and arrogance. The next verse will address this directly.

33
وَمَا أُرْسِلُوا عَلَيْهِمْ حَافِظِينَ

Simple Explanation

A devastating rebuke. "Who appointed you as their guardians?" The disbelievers were never sent by Allah to watch over, judge, or guide the believers. They had no authority, no mandate, and no right to pass judgment on anyone's spiritual state. Ibn Kathir explains that this is Allah exposing the absurdity of their position: they took it upon themselves to declare others misguided when no one gave them that role. As-Sa'di adds that this verse is a universal principle. Those who spend their time judging and mocking others' faith should ask themselves: who gave you that authority? Mind your own deeds. You were not appointed as a guardian over anyone else's relationship with Allah.

34
فَالْيَوْمَ الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا مِنَ الْكُفَّارِ يَضْحَكُونَ

The ultimate reversal. The exact word "yadhakun" (laughing) used in verse 29 for the disbelievers is now used for the believers. In this world, the disbelievers laughed at the believers. On the Day of Judgment, it is the believers who laugh at the disbelievers. Ibn Kathir explains that this is perfect divine justice: every act of mockery is returned in kind, but in a way that is eternal. As-Sa'di notes the power of "fal-yawma" (so Today). The word "today" marks the turning point. All the ridicule, all the mockery, all the winking and nudging, it is all over. The tables have completely turned. The believers endured their laughter with patience, and now they are rewarded with the last laugh.

35
عَلَى الْأَرَائِكِ يَنظُرُونَ

Simple Explanation

This verse is identical to verse 23, but its meaning here is even more powerful because of the context. In verse 23, the believers are reclining on couches enjoying Paradise. Here in verse 35, they are on those same couches but now specifically watching the disbelievers face their punishment. Ibn Kathir explains that the repetition is deliberate: the same couches of luxury become the front-row seats to witness divine justice being served. As-Sa'di notes the complete scene: the believers are comfortable, at peace, reclining on the most luxurious couches, and observing the very people who used to mock them now receiving their just recompense. Every tear shed in this world from being ridiculed is now repaid with the sweetness of vindication.

36
هَلْ ثُوِّبَ الْكُفَّارُ مَا كَانُوا يَفْعَلُونَ

Simple Explanation

The surah ends with a devastating rhetorical question. "Thuwwiba" normally means "rewarded," but here it is used with biting irony for punishment. "Have the disbelievers been repaid for what they used to do?" The answer is an emphatic yes. Every mockery, every wink, every arrogant dismissal, every act of fraud has been repaid in full. Ibn Kathir explains that this closing question is addressed to the believers, as if to say: "Look at this. Justice has been served, hasn't it?" As-Sa'di notes the perfect closure of the surah. It opened with "Woe to the defrauders" and closes by confirming that justice has been delivered. Those who cheated in their measures have now been measured by Allah's perfect scales. Those who laughed at the believers have become the ones laughed at. The circle of divine justice is complete.

Key Lessons
  • 01
    Honesty in dealings is a sign of faith. The surah opens by condemning cheaters and immediately asks: do they not believe in the Day of Judgment? Dishonesty in business, relationships, or any dealing is a symptom of weak faith in accountability before Allah.
  • 02
    Sin stains the heart and blinds it to truth. Verse 14 teaches that persistent sinning creates a "ran" (stain/rust) on the heart. Over time, the heart becomes so darkened that it cannot recognize truth even when it is clearly presented. This is why repentance and seeking forgiveness must be constant.
  • 03
    Being veiled from Allah is the worst punishment. Verse 15 describes the wicked being blocked from seeing their Lord. Imam Ash-Shafi'i derived from this that the believers will see Allah, since the denial of sight is a punishment for the wicked. Seeing Allah is the greatest reward in Paradise.
  • 04
    Compete for the Hereafter, not the world. "For this let the competitors compete" (verse 26). Human ambition and competitive drive should be channeled toward earning Paradise, not accumulating worldly possessions that will all turn to dust.
  • 05
    Mocking believers has consequences. Verses 29-34 describe the complete reversal: those who mocked the believers in this world will be the ones mocked on the Day of Judgment. Every believer who endures ridicule for their faith should find comfort in knowing that the final outcome is theirs.
  • 06
    Divine justice is perfect and complete. The surah begins with injustice (cheating in measures) and ends with perfect justice (the disbelievers fully repaid). Nothing escapes Allah's accounting. Every atom of good and evil will be brought to light and repaid in full.
