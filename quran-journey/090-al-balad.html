The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Balad was revealed in Makkah during the early period of the Prophet's mission. The surah takes its name from the word "Al-Balad" (The City), referring to the sacred city of Makkah. Allah swears by this city to honor it, and specifically mentions the Prophet's presence in it.

The surah addresses a fundamental reality of human existence: life is a struggle. The Arabic word "kabad" (verse 4) means hardship, toil, and difficulty. From the moment a person is born until they leave this world, they face challenges. This is not a punishment but the nature of the test.

Ibn Kathir explains that the oath by Makkah is particularly significant because Allah is honoring the city where His Prophet resides. The phrase "wa anta hillun bihadhal-balad" has been interpreted by many scholars to mean that the Prophet is a free dweller in this city, adding to its honor and sanctity.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Tafsir As-Sa'di

The surah then critiques the arrogance of those who boast about their wealth, thinking no one sees them or has power over them. Allah reminds them of the blessings He gave: eyes, tongue, lips, and the ability to distinguish right from wrong.

The climax of the surah introduces "al-aqabah" (the steep path), which represents the difficult but rewarding path of righteousness: freeing slaves, feeding the hungry, caring for orphans, and helping the poor. The surah concludes by dividing humanity into two groups: the people of the right hand (those who chose faith and compassion) and the people of the left hand (those who rejected Allah's signs).

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
لَا أُقْسِمُ بِهَٰذَا الْبَلَدِ
La uqsimu bihadhal-balad
I swear by this city.

Word by Word

لَاlaNay / Indeed
أُقْسِمُuqsimuI swear
بِهَٰذَاbihadhaBy this
الْبَلَدِal-baladThe city
Simple Explanation

Allah begins by swearing an oath by the city of Makkah. The word "la" here is used for emphasis (not negation), strengthening the oath. Makkah is the most sacred city on earth, home to the Kaaba, and the birthplace of the Prophet (peace be upon him). By swearing by it, Allah highlights its immense significance.

2
وَأَنتَ حِلٌّ بِهَٰذَا الْبَلَدِ
Wa anta hillun bihadhal-balad
And you are free [to do what you wish] in this city.

Word by Word

وَأَنتَwa antaAnd you
حِلٌّhillunFree / lawful
بِهَٰذَاbihadhaIn this
الْبَلَدِal-baladThe city
Simple Explanation

Allah addresses the Prophet directly, honoring him as a resident of this sacred city. Some scholars say "hillun" means the Prophet is treated as lawful (i.e., his enemies don't respect his sanctity despite being in a sacred city). Others interpret it as a prophecy that Makkah would become lawful for the Prophet during its conquest. Either way, it highlights the Prophet's special connection to this blessed land.

3
وَوَالِدٍ وَمَا وَلَدَ
Wa walidin wa ma walad
And [by] the parent and that which was born [of him].

Word by Word

وَوَالِدٍwa walidinAnd a parent
وَمَاwa maAnd what
وَلَدَwaladHe begot / offspring
Simple Explanation

Allah swears by the parent and the child. Some scholars say this refers to Adam (peace be upon him) and all his descendants. Others say it refers generally to every parent and child, highlighting the sacred bond of family. This oath emphasizes the nobility of human creation and the chain of generations that Allah has established.

4
لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا الْإِنسَانَ فِي كَبَدٍ
Laqad khalaqnal-insana fi kabad
We have certainly created man into hardship.

Word by Word

لَقَدْlaqadCertainly / Indeed
خَلَقْنَاkhalaqnaWe created
الْإِنسَانَal-insanaThe human being
فِيfiIn
كَبَدٍkabadHardship / toil
Simple Explanation

This is the answer to the oath: humans were created to face hardship. "Kabad" means difficulty, toil, and struggle. From birth (a difficult process) to death, life is full of challenges. As-Sa'di explains this means that no human is free from some form of hardship, whether physical, emotional, or spiritual. This is the nature of the test of life. Knowing this helps us stop expecting a trouble-free life and instead prepare for the struggles ahead with faith.

5
أَيَحْسَبُ أَن لَّن يَقْدِرَ عَلَيْهِ أَحَدٌ
Ayahsabu an lan yaqdira 'alayhi ahad
Does he think that never will anyone overcome him?

Word by Word

لَّن يَقْدِرَlan yaqdiraNever will overpower
عَلَيْهِ'alayhiOver him

Simple Explanation

This verse addresses the arrogance of the human being who, when given strength and wealth, begins to think he is invincible. He forgets that Allah has complete power over him. Ibn Kathir notes this refers to those who become so intoxicated with their own power that they believe no one, not even Allah, can hold them accountable. This is the height of delusion.

6
يَقُولُ أَهْلَكْتُ مَالًا لُّبَدًا
Yaqulu ahlaktu malan lubada
He says, "I have spent wealth in abundance."

Word by Word

يَقُولُyaquluHe says
أَهْلَكْتُahlaktuI have spent / destroyed
مَالًاmalanWealth
لُّبَدًاlubadaIn abundance / heaped
Simple Explanation

This person boasts about how much money he has spent, wanting to show off his wealth and generosity to people. The word "ahlaktu" literally means "I destroyed" (my wealth), implying reckless spending for show. "Lubada" means piles upon piles. But this spending was not for Allah's sake; it was to show off. True charity in Islam is done sincerely for Allah, not for the praise of people.

7
أَيَحْسَبُ أَن لَّمْ يَرَهُ أَحَدٌ
Ayahsabu an lam yarahu ahad
Does he think that no one has seen him?

Word by Word

أَنanThat
لَّمْ يَرَهُlam yarahuHas not seen him
أَحَدٌahadAnyone
Simple Explanation

Does this person think that Allah does not see what he does with his wealth? Whether he spends it sincerely or for show, whether he earned it lawfully or unlawfully, Allah sees everything. As-Sa'di explains that this is a reminder that Allah is Al-Basir (The All-Seeing) and Al-Khabir (The All-Aware). Nothing is hidden from Him, not even the intentions behind our actions.

8
أَلَمْ نَجْعَل لَّهُ عَيْنَيْنِ
Alam naj'al lahu 'aynayn
Have We not made for him two eyes?

Word by Word

أَلَمْalamHave not
نَجْعَلnaj'alWe made
لَّهُlahuFor him
عَيْنَيْنِ'aynaynTwo eyes
Simple Explanation

Allah now reminds the ungrateful person of His blessings. Two eyes to see the world, to read the signs of Allah, to witness the beauty of creation, and to navigate through life. The dual form "aynayn" emphasizes the precision of Allah's design. These blessings should lead to gratitude, not arrogance. Every time you see something, remember: that ability is a gift from Allah.

9
وَلِسَانًا وَشَفَتَيْنِ
Wa lisanan wa shafatayn
And a tongue and two lips?

Word by Word

وَلِسَانًاwa lisananAnd a tongue
وَشَفَتَيْنِwa shafataynAnd two lips
Simple Explanation

A tongue to speak, taste, and express. Two lips to form words, to eat, to smile. These are gifts that enable communication, the sharing of knowledge, the expression of gratitude (dhikr), and the ability to connect with others. The tongue is one of the most powerful tools a person has: it can build or destroy. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Whoever believes in Allah and the Last Day, let him speak good or remain silent." (Sahih Bukhari 6018)

10
وَهَدَيْنَاهُ النَّجْدَيْنِ
Wa hadaynahun-najdayn
And have shown him the two ways?

Word by Word

وَهَدَيْنَاهُwa hadaynahuAnd We showed him
النَّجْدَيْنِan-najdaynThe two paths
Simple Explanation

"An-najdayn" literally means "the two elevated paths" or "the two highways." Ibn Kathir and As-Sa'di explain this as the path of good and the path of evil. Allah gave every human being the ability to distinguish between right and wrong through fitrah (natural disposition), intellect, and revelation. The choice is ours. This verse establishes human moral responsibility: you cannot claim ignorance when you were shown both roads clearly.

11
فَلَا اقْتَحَمَ الْعَقَبَةَ
Falaq-tahamal-'aqabah
But he has not broken through the difficult pass.

Word by Word

فَلَاfalaBut not
اقْتَحَمَiqtahamaHe has attempted
الْعَقَبَةَal-'aqabahThe steep path
Simple Explanation

"Al-aqabah" means a steep, difficult mountain pass. The word "iqtahama" means to rush into something difficult, to break through a barrier. Despite being shown both paths, this person has not attempted the harder but more rewarding path of righteousness. The next verses will explain what this steep path consists of. It requires sacrifice, but that is exactly what makes it valuable.

12
وَمَا أَدْرَاكَ مَا الْعَقَبَةُ
Wa ma adraka mal-'aqabah
And what can make you know what is the difficult pass?

Word by Word

وَمَاwa maAnd what
أَدْرَاكَadrakaWill make you know
مَاmaWhat is
الْعَقَبَةُal-'aqabahThe steep path
Simple Explanation

This is a rhetorical question used throughout the Quran to build anticipation and emphasize the greatness of what is about to be described. "Wa ma adraka" is a formula that appears in several surahs (Al-Haqqah, Al-Qari'ah, Al-Infitar, etc.) and always introduces something of tremendous importance. Allah is about to reveal the deeds that constitute this steep, rewarding path.

13
فَكُّ رَقَبَةٍ
Fakku raqabah
It is the freeing of a slave.

Word by Word

فَكُّfakkuFreeing / releasing
رَقَبَةٍraqabahA neck / slave
Simple Explanation

The first component of the steep path: freeing a slave. "Raqabah" literally means "neck," used metaphorically for a person in bondage. Islam systematically worked to abolish slavery by making the freeing of slaves one of the most rewarded deeds. It is the expiation for many sins and broken oaths. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Whoever frees a Muslim slave, Allah will save every part of his body from the Fire." (Sahih Bukhari 2517). In modern times, this extends to freeing people from any form of oppression and bondage.

14
أَوْ إِطْعَامٌ فِي يَوْمٍ ذِي مَسْغَبَةٍ
Aw it'amun fi yawmin dhi masghabah
Or feeding on a day of severe hunger.

Word by Word

أَوْawOr
إِطْعَامٌit'amunFeeding
فِي يَوْمٍfi yawminOn a day
ذِيdhiOf / possessing
مَسْغَبَةٍmasghabahSevere hunger
Simple Explanation

The second component: feeding others, specifically on a day when food is scarce and hunger is widespread. "Masghabah" means extreme, intense hunger. Charity given during times of hardship is worth far more than charity given in ease. When you yourself are struggling but still give to others, that is the steep path. As-Sa'di notes that specifying "a day of severe hunger" means the reward is greater when the need is most desperate.

15
يَتِيمًا ذَا مَقْرَبَةٍ
Yatiman dha maqrabah
An orphan of near relationship.

Word by Word

يَتِيمًاyatimanAn orphan
ذَاdhaPossessing / of
مَقْرَبَةٍmaqrabahNear kinship
Simple Explanation

Feed specifically an orphan who is a relative. Islam places great emphasis on maintaining family ties (silat al-rahm). An orphan within your own family has a double right upon you: the right of kinship and the right of being an orphan. By specifying "dha maqrabah" (of near relationship), the Quran reminds us to look after our own extended family first before looking elsewhere. Sometimes the people closest to us are the ones we overlook the most.

16
أَوْ مِسْكِينًا ذَا مَتْرَبَةٍ
Aw miskinan dha matrabah
Or a needy person in misery.

Word by Word

أَوْawOr
مِسْكِينًاmiskinanA needy person
ذَاdhaPossessing / of
مَتْرَبَةٍmatrabahClinging to dust / utter poverty
Simple Explanation

"Miskin" is someone in severe poverty. "Dha matrabah" literally means "clinging to the dust," describing someone so poor they have nothing and lie on the bare ground. This is the lowest level of material existence. Feeding such a person is one of the greatest acts of worship. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "He is not a believer whose stomach is filled while the neighbor beside him goes hungry." (Al-Adab Al-Mufrad, authenticated by Al-Albani)

17
ثُمَّ كَانَ مِنَ الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا وَتَوَاصَوْا بِالصَّبْرِ وَتَوَاصَوْا بِالْمَرْحَمَةِ
Thumma kana minal-ladhina amanu wa tawasaw bis-sabri wa tawasaw bil-marhamah
And then being among those who believed and advised one another to patience and advised one another to compassion.

Word by Word

ثُمَّthummaThen
كَانَkanaBeing
مِنَ الَّذِينَminal-ladhinaAmong those who
آمَنُواamanuBelieved

Simple Explanation

This is the crown of the steep path: faith combined with community. "Tawasaw" means to mutually advise, encourage, and remind each other. It's not a one-way street; it's believers supporting each other. Two qualities are highlighted: "sabr" (patience in hardship, persistence in obedience, and restraint from sin) and "marhamah" (mercy, compassion, tenderness). A community built on patience and mercy is the ideal Muslim community. Good deeds without faith are incomplete, and faith without good deeds is empty.

18
أُولَٰئِكَ أَصْحَابُ الْمَيْمَنَةِ
Ula'ika ashabul-maymanah
Those are the companions of the right.

Word by Word

أُولَٰئِكَula'ikaThose
أَصْحَابُashabuCompanions / people of
الْمَيْمَنَةِal-maymanahThe right hand
Simple Explanation

Those who climb the steep path, who free others, feed the hungry, care for orphans and the poor, believe in Allah, and encourage patience and mercy among themselves, they are "ashab al-maymanah" (the people of the right hand). In the Quran, the right hand symbolizes honor, success, and salvation. On the Day of Judgment, those who receive their record in their right hand are the successful ones (see Surah Al-Haqqah 69:19). This is the ultimate reward for choosing the harder but nobler path.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

In contrast, those who rejected Allah's signs, who refused to climb the steep path, who chose arrogance over compassion, they are "ashab al-mash'amah" (the people of the left hand). "Mash'amah" comes from "shu'm" which means ill omen, misfortune. These are the ones who will receive their record in their left hand on the Day of Judgment (see Surah Al-Haqqah 69:25). The Quran presents a clear binary: every person will end up in one of these two groups based on their choices.

20
عَلَيْهِمْ نَارٌ مُّؤْصَدَةٌ
'Alayhim narun mu'sadah
Over them will be fire closed in.

Word by Word

عَلَيْهِمْ'alayhimOver them
نَارٌnarunFire
مُّؤْصَدَةٌmu'sadahClosed in / sealed
Simple Explanation

The surah ends with the consequence for the people of the left hand: fire that is "mu'sadah" (sealed, closed, locked over them). This means there is no escape, no exit. Ibn Kathir explains that the gates of Hellfire will be shut upon them, enclosing them completely. This is mentioned in Surah Al-Humazah (104:8) as well: "It will be closed down over them." This is not meant to cause despair but to serve as a powerful warning: the choices we make in this life have eternal consequences. Choose the steep path while you still can.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Balad
  • ◆
    Life is meant to be a struggle.

    Allah created humans "fi kabad" (in hardship). Expecting a problem-free life is unrealistic. Instead, prepare for challenges with faith, patience, and the right mindset.

  • ◆
    Wealth without purpose is worthless.

    Boasting about spending money is not generosity. True generosity is spending for Allah's sake, sincerely, especially when it is difficult and when others need it most.

  • ◆
    Allah sees everything.

    No action, intention, or thought is hidden from Allah. The arrogant person who thinks no one watches him is deeply mistaken. Live with the awareness that Allah is always watching (ihsan).

  • ◆
    The steep path is hard but leads to success.

    Freeing the oppressed, feeding the hungry, caring for orphans, helping the destitute: these are difficult acts. But this is exactly the path that leads to being among the "people of the right hand."

  • ◆
    Faith must be paired with patience and mercy.

    Good deeds alone are not enough. They must be rooted in faith (iman), supported by patience (sabr), and expressed through compassion (marhamah). These three together form the complete picture.

  • ◆
    Every person faces a choice between two paths.

    Allah showed us both paths clearly. The choice between right and left, good and evil, compassion and arrogance is ours to make. And the consequences of that choice are eternal.
