The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Ma'un is one of the most powerful short surahs in the Quran. It draws a direct connection between faith in the Day of Judgment and how we treat other people, especially the weak and vulnerable.

The surah begins by asking a question: have you seen the person who denies the Day of Recompense? Then it describes the signs of such denial: mistreating orphans and refusing to feed the poor. These are not just bad habits. They are symptoms of a heart that does not truly believe in accountability before Allah.

The surah then shifts to a shocking warning: it addresses people who pray but are heedless of their prayer, who show off their deeds, and who refuse even the smallest acts of kindness. This shows that worship without sincerity and compassion is hollow and rejected.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Tafsir As-Sa'di

The word "Al-Ma'un" refers to small, everyday items that people lend to each other: a pot, a bucket, salt, water. Withholding even these tiny acts of kindness is a sign of a spiritually dead heart. The surah teaches us that true faith must be reflected in both worship and kindness to others.

Ibn Kathir explains that this surah was revealed about those hypocrites and disbelievers who combined denial of the afterlife with cruelty to the weak. As-Sa'di notes that the surah pairs two types of failure: failure in one's relationship with Allah (insincere prayer) and failure in one's relationship with people (refusing kindness).

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
أَرَأَيْتَ الَّذِي يُكَذِّبُ بِالدِّينِ
Ara'aytal-ladhi yukadh-dhibu bid-din
Have you seen the one who denies the Recompense?

Word by Word (right to left)

أَرَأَيْتَara'aytaHave you seen
الَّذِيalladhiThe one who
يُكَذِّبُyukadh-dhibuDenies / rejects
بِالدِّينِbid-dinThe Recompense / Judgment
Simple Explanation

Allah begins with a rhetorical question to the Prophet (peace be upon him) and to all of us: "Have you seen?" This grabs attention. "Ad-Din" here means the Day of Judgment and the system of reward and punishment. The person described does not believe they will ever be held accountable for their actions. This disbelief in accountability is the root cause of all the bad behaviors described next.

2
فَذَٰلِكَ الَّذِي يَدُعُّ الْيَتِيمَ
Fa-dhalikal-ladhi yadu'-ul-yatim
For that is the one who drives away the orphan.

Word by Word (right to left)

فَذَٰلِكَfa-dhalikaFor that is
الَّذِيalladhiThe one who
يَدُعُّyadu'uDrives away / pushes
الْيَتِيمَal-yatimThe orphan
Simple Explanation

The word "yadu'u" means to push away harshly, to repel with force. An orphan is a child who has lost their father. Instead of showing compassion, this person drives the orphan away with cruelty. Ibn Kathir explains that this person does not give the orphan their rights, nor shows them any kindness. When someone truly believes in the Day of Judgment, they know that Allah will ask them about how they treated the vulnerable.

3
وَلَا يَحُضُّ عَلَىٰ طَعَامِ الْمِسْكِينِ
Wa la yahuddu 'ala ta'amil-miskin
And does not encourage the feeding of the poor.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَلَاwa laAnd does not
يَحُضُّyahudduEncourage / urge
عَلَىٰ'alaUpon / towards
طَعَامِta'amiFeeding / food of
الْمِسْكِينِal-miskinThe poor / needy
Simple Explanation

Notice: it does not just say "does not feed the poor." It says "does not even encourage others to feed the poor." This is a deeper level of neglect. This person neither feeds the needy themselves nor motivates anyone else to do so. As-Sa'di explains that if someone does not even encourage feeding the poor, then certainly they themselves are even less likely to do it. A believer should be a catalyst for good in their community.

4
فَوَيْلٌ لِّلْمُصَلِّينَ
Fa-waylul-lil-musallin
So woe to those who pray.

Word by Word (right to left)

فَوَيْلٌfa-waylunSo woe (destruction)
لِّلْمُصَلِّينَlil-musallinTo those who pray
Simple Explanation

"Wayl" is a word of severe warning, meaning destruction or a valley in Hellfire. This verse is shocking on purpose: woe to those who PRAY? But the next verse explains who is meant. It is not all who pray, but a specific type of person who prays. This verse must be read together with verse 5. Allah is warning against a particular kind of worshipper, not against prayer itself.

5
الَّذِينَ هُمْ عَن صَلَاتِهِمْ سَاهُونَ
Alladhina hum 'an salatihim sahun
Those who are heedless of their prayer.

Word by Word (right to left)

الَّذِينَalladhinaThose who
هُمْhumThey
عَن'anFrom / of
صَلَاتِهِمْsalatihimTheir prayer
سَاهُونَsahunHeedless / neglectful
Simple Explanation

The key word is "'an" (from/of). Allah says they are heedless OF their prayer, not IN their prayer. Ibn Kathir and the companions (like Ibn Abbas and Mus'ab ibn Sa'd) explained: this means they delay their prayer beyond its proper time, pray lazily, or miss it altogether without care. If it said "fi salatihim" (in their prayer), it would mean occasional distraction, which happens to everyone. But "'an salatihim" means a complete disregard for the prayer itself.

6
الَّذِينَ هُمْ يُرَاءُونَ
Alladhina hum yura'un
Those who make show of their deeds.

Word by Word (right to left)

الَّذِينَalladhinaThose who
هُمْhumThey
يُرَاءُونَyura'unShow off / do for display
Simple Explanation

"Yura'un" comes from "riya'" which means showing off or doing things to be seen by people rather than for Allah. The Prophet (peace be upon him) called riya' "the minor shirk" (associating partners with Allah). These people pray or do good deeds not for Allah's sake, but so people will praise them and think highly of them. Their worship is a performance, not a genuine connection with their Creator.

7
وَيَمْنَعُونَ الْمَاعُونَ
Wa yamna'un al-ma'un
And withhold simple assistance.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَيَمْنَعُونَwa yamna'unAnd they withhold / refuse
الْمَاعُونَal-ma'unSimple assistance / small kindnesses
Simple Explanation

"Al-Ma'un" refers to small everyday items that people commonly lend to each other: a cooking pot, a bucket, salt, water, a needle, or even an axe. Ibn Abbas, Ibn Mas'ud, and others from the companions explained it this way. These are things that cost nothing significant to share. The person described is so stingy and hard-hearted that they will not even lend their neighbor a pot or a cup of sugar. If someone refuses even these tiny acts of kindness, imagine how far they are from giving charity or helping in bigger ways.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Ma'un
  • ◆
    Faith and kindness are inseparable.

    True belief in the Day of Judgment naturally leads to compassion for others. If someone claims to believe but mistreats the weak, their faith is questionable.

  • ◆
    Prayer without sincerity is worthless.

    Going through the motions of prayer while being heedless of its purpose and timing is not real worship. Allah wants presence of heart, not empty rituals.

  • ◆
    Showing off destroys good deeds.

    Riya' (doing deeds for people's approval) is called "minor shirk" by the Prophet. Sincerity (ikhlas) is what makes any deed count before Allah.

  • ◆
    Small kindnesses matter enormously.

    Lending a neighbor a pot, sharing salt, offering water. These tiny acts reflect the state of your heart. Never underestimate the weight of small good deeds.

  • ◆
    Encourage others to do good.

    It is not enough to just do good yourself. A believer also encourages and motivates others to help the poor and needy. Be a catalyst for kindness.
