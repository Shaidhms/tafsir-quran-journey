The Story Behind This Surah

This surah was revealed in Makkah and delivers a stern warning against two destructive habits: backbiting (talking badly about someone behind their back) and slandering (mocking someone to their face). The Arabic word "humazah" refers to someone who mocks and insults people openly, while "lumazah" refers to someone who does it behind their backs.

Some scholars say this surah was revealed about specific individuals among the Quraysh who used to mock the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the early Muslims. Others, like Ibn Kathir, explain that the warning is general and applies to anyone who engages in this behavior.

The surah connects two sins together: backbiting/slandering and obsessive love of wealth. The person described here not only mocks others but also hoards money, counting it over and over, thinking wealth will make them live forever. Allah warns that such a person will be thrown into Al-Hutamah (the Crusher), a terrifying name for Hellfire that crushes and breaks everything thrown into it.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Tafsir As-Sa'di

The surah then describes this punishment in vivid detail: it is the fire of Allah, eternally fueled, which reaches deep into the hearts of the sinners. It will be closed over them in towering columns, leaving no escape. This imagery emphasizes that the punishment is not just physical but penetrates to the core of their being.

As-Sa'di explains that the fire reaching the hearts is significant because the heart is where arrogance, contempt for others, and love of wealth reside. The punishment matches the crime perfectly.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَيْلٌ لِّكُلِّ هُمَزَةٍ لُّمَزَةٍ
Waylul li-kulli humazatil lumazah
Woe to every scorner and mocker.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَيْلٌwaylunWoe / Destruction
لِّكُلِّli-kulliTo every
هُمَزَةٍhumazatinScorner (face-to-face)
لُّمَزَةٍlumazatinMocker (behind the back)
Simple Explanation

"Waylun" is one of the strongest words of warning in the Quran - it means terrible destruction and punishment. "Humazah" comes from "hamz" which means to crush or break, referring to someone who attacks others openly with insults and mockery. "Lumazah" comes from "lamz" which means to hint or gesture, referring to backbiting and mocking people behind their backs. Both forms of hurting people are condemned equally.

2
الَّذِي جَمَعَ مَالًا وَعَدَّدَهُ
Alladhi jama'a maalan wa 'addadah
Who collects wealth and continuously counts it.

Word by Word (right to left)

الَّذِيalladhiThe one who
جَمَعَjama'aCollected / gathered
مَالًاmaalanWealth / money
وَعَدَّدَهُwa 'addadahAnd counted it repeatedly
Simple Explanation

"Jama'a" means to gather and amass, implying this person is obsessively accumulating wealth from every direction. "'Addadah" comes from "'adad" (number/count) and the form used here implies doing it again and again. This person keeps counting their money repeatedly, finding pleasure and security in the numbers rather than using the wealth for good. Ibn Kathir notes this describes someone whose entire life revolves around accumulating and counting wealth.

3
يَحْسَبُ أَنَّ مَالَهُ أَخْلَدَهُ
Yahsabu anna maalahu akhladah
He thinks that his wealth will make him immortal.

Word by Word (right to left)

يَحْسَبُyahsabuHe thinks / assumes
أَنَّannaThat
مَالَهُmaalahuHis wealth
أَخْلَدَهُakhladahWill make him immortal
Simple Explanation

"Yahsabu" means he assumes or supposes, implying a false belief. "Akhladah" comes from "khuld" meaning eternity or immortality. This person has deluded himself into thinking that wealth can somehow protect him from death or extend his life indefinitely. As-Sa'di explains that this is the ultimate delusion: thinking money can buy what only Allah controls. No matter how rich someone becomes, death comes to everyone without exception.

4
كَلَّا ۖ لَيُنبَذَنَّ فِي الْحُطَمَةِ
Kalla, la-yunbadhanna fil-hutamah
No! He will surely be thrown into the Crusher.

Word by Word (right to left)

كَلَّاkallaNo! / Absolutely not!
لَيُنبَذَنَّla-yunbadhannaHe will surely be thrown
فِيfiInto
الْحُطَمَةِal-hutamahThe Crusher
Simple Explanation

"Kalla" is a strong word of rejection and rebuke - Allah is forcefully rejecting the delusion mentioned in the previous verse. "Yunbadhanna" means to be thrown or cast away with contempt, like something worthless. "Al-Hutamah" comes from "hatm" meaning to crush and break into pieces. It is a name for Hellfire that emphasizes its crushing, breaking nature. Just as this person crushed others with their words, they will be crushed by the fire. The punishment mirrors the crime.

5
وَمَا أَدْرَاكَ مَا الْحُطَمَةُ
Wa maa adraaka mal-hutamah
And what can make you know what the Crusher is?

Word by Word (right to left)

وَمَاwa maaAnd what
أَدْرَاكَadraakaCan make you know
مَاmaaWhat
الْحُطَمَةُal-hutamahThe Crusher
Simple Explanation

This rhetorical question "wa maa adraaka" (and what will make you know) is used in the Quran to emphasize something beyond human comprehension. It creates a sense of awe and terror - the Crusher is so terrible that no human mind can fully grasp its reality. Ibn Kathir explains that whenever Allah uses this phrase, He follows it with an explanation, which comes in the next verses.

6
نَارُ اللَّهِ الْمُوقَدَةُ
Naarullahil-muqadah
It is the fire of Allah, eternally fueled.

Word by Word (right to left)

نَارُnaaruFire of
اللَّهِAllahiAllah
الْمُوقَدَةُal-muqadahThe eternally fueled / kindled
Simple Explanation

The fire is attributed to Allah Himself ("Naarullah"), which is extremely significant. Ibn Kathir points out that Allah rarely attributes things directly to Himself in this way, and when He does, it emphasizes their greatness and severity. "Al-Muqadah" means continuously kindled, burning without end. Unlike worldly fires that need fuel and eventually die out, this fire is maintained by Allah's power and never extinguishes.

7
الَّتِي تَطَّلِعُ عَلَى الْأَفْئِدَةِ
Allatee tattali'u 'alal-af'idah
Which mounts directed at the hearts.

Word by Word (right to left)

الَّتِيallateeWhich (feminine)
تَطَّلِعُtattali'uMounts / rises upon
عَلَى'alaUpon / over
الْأَفْئِدَةِal-af'idahThe hearts
Simple Explanation

"Tattali'u" means to rise up, mount, or reach toward something. "Al-Af'idah" is the plural of "fu'aad" which refers to the innermost part of the heart. As-Sa'di beautifully explains that the fire targets the hearts specifically because the heart is where the diseases of arrogance, contempt for others, and obsessive love of wealth reside. The punishment reaches the very source of the sin. Some scholars say this means the fire causes the most intense pain at the heart, which is the most sensitive part of the body.

8
إِنَّهَا عَلَيْهِمْ مُّؤْصَدَةٌ
Innaha 'alayhim mu'sadah
Indeed, it will be closed down upon them.

Word by Word (right to left)

إِنَّهَاinnahaIndeed it
عَلَيْهِمْ'alayhimUpon them
مُّؤْصَدَةٌmu'sadahClosed / sealed shut
Simple Explanation

"Mu'sadah" means completely closed, sealed, and locked with no opening or exit. Ibn Kathir explains that the doors of Hellfire will be shut upon its inhabitants so that they can never leave. There is no escape, no reprieve, and no hope of exit. This imagery is especially terrifying because even in the worst worldly prisons, there is some hope of release. Here, there is none. The fire surrounds them from every direction with no way out.

9
فِي عَمَدٍ مُّمَدَّدَةٍ
Fee 'amadim mumaddadah
In extended columns.

Word by Word (right to left)

فِيfeeIn
عَمَدٍ'amadinColumns / pillars
مُّمَدَّدَةٍmumaddadahExtended / outstretched
Simple Explanation

"'Amad" means pillars or columns, and "mumaddadah" means stretched out or extended. Scholars differ on the exact meaning: some say the doors are sealed with pillars stretching across them to reinforce the closure, making escape even more impossible. Others say the inhabitants themselves are bound to extended pillars of fire. Either way, the image is one of absolute confinement and inescapable punishment. This final verse seals the surah with a terrifying image of permanence and finality.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Humazah
  • ◆
    Backbiting and mocking destroy communities.

    Allah begins with "Waylun" (Woe!), one of the strongest warnings in the Quran. Mocking others, whether openly or behind their backs, is a serious sin that tears apart the bonds between people.

  • ◆
    Wealth cannot save you from death.

    No matter how much money you accumulate and count, it cannot make you immortal. The delusion that wealth equals permanence is shattered by the reality of death and accountability.

  • ◆
    The punishment fits the crime.

    The fire that reaches the hearts targets the very place where arrogance, contempt, and greed resided. This is divine justice in its most precise form.

  • ◆
    There is no escape from Allah's judgment.

    The fire is described as being closed over them in extended columns. Unlike worldly prisons, there is no parole, no appeal, and no escape from the consequences of persistent arrogance and cruelty.
