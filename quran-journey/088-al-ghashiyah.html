The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Ghashiyah takes its name from the very first verse, which asks about "Al-Ghashiyah" - the Overwhelming Event that will cover and engulf everything. This refers to the Day of Judgment, an event so massive and all-encompassing that it will overwhelm every soul.

This surah was revealed in Makkah during a time when the Quraysh denied the resurrection and the afterlife. Allah responds to their denial with a vivid, powerful contrast between two groups of people on that Day: those whose faces are humiliated and exhausted from the torment of the Fire, and those whose faces are radiant and pleased in the gardens of Paradise.

Ibn Kathir explains that Al-Ghashiyah is one of the names of the Day of Judgment because it will "ghasha" (overwhelm and cover) all of creation. No one will escape it. The surah's structure moves from the terrifying to the beautiful, from punishment to reward, then to the signs in creation that prove Allah's power, and finally to the Prophet's role as a reminder.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Tafsir As-Sa'di

After the contrast between the two groups, Allah then turns the listener's attention to four signs in creation: the camel (how it was created), the sky (how it was raised), the mountains (how they were set firm), and the earth (how it was spread out). These are not random examples. They are things the Arabs of Makkah saw every single day, yet they failed to reflect on the Creator behind them.

The surah concludes by defining the Prophet's role: he is a reminder, not a controller. He cannot force faith upon anyone. But everyone will return to Allah, and Allah will call them to account. This is a powerful combination of mercy in da'wah and certainty of the final reckoning.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
هَلْ أَتَاكَ حَدِيثُ الْغَاشِيَةِ
Hal ataaka hadithul-ghashiyah
Has there reached you the report of the Overwhelming [event]?

Word by Word

هَلْhalHas / Did
أَتَاكَataakaReached you
حَدِيثُhadithuThe report / news of
الْغَاشِيَةِal-ghashiyahThe Overwhelming
Simple Explanation

Allah opens with a gripping rhetorical question to the Prophet (peace be upon him): "Has the news of the Overwhelming Event reached you?" This is not asking whether he knows - it's a dramatic way to draw attention. "Al-Ghashiyah" comes from the root "ghasha" meaning to cover, engulf, and overwhelm. The Day of Judgment is called this because its terror will overwhelm everything and everyone. No one will be able to escape or ignore it.

2
وُجُوهٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ خَاشِعَةٌ
Wujoohun yawma'idhin khashi'ah
[Some] faces, that Day, will be humbled.

Word by Word

وُجُوهٌwujoohunFaces
يَوْمَئِذٍyawma'idhinThat Day
خَاشِعَةٌkhashi'ahHumbled / humiliated
Simple Explanation

Now Allah describes the first group. "Khashi'ah" means humbled, humiliated, and cast down in disgrace. These are faces that were arrogant in this world, that refused to bow to Allah or acknowledge His signs. On that Day, the arrogance is gone. Their faces show nothing but shame and humiliation. The face reveals the inner state - and their inner state is one of utter defeat.

3
عَامِلَةٌ نَّاصِبَةٌ
'Aamilatun naasibah
Working [hard] and exhausted.

Word by Word

عَامِلَةٌ'aamilatunWorking / laboring
نَّاصِبَةٌnaasibahExhausted / fatigued
Simple Explanation

Ibn Kathir and As-Sa'di explain this verse in two ways. Some scholars say it describes their state in the Hellfire - they are laboring and exhausted from dragging chains and climbing only to fall back. Others say it describes their worldly life - they worked hard in acts of worship that were not accepted because they were done without sincerity or upon misguidance. Either way, the lesson is stark: effort without correct guidance leads to exhaustion without reward.

4
تَصْلَىٰ نَارًا حَامِيَةً
Tasla naaran haamiyah
They will [enter to] burn in an intensely hot Fire.

Word by Word

تَصْلَىٰtaslaThey will burn in
نَارًاnaaranA fire
حَامِيَةًhaamiyahIntensely hot
Simple Explanation

"Tasla" means to enter and be surrounded by fire, to feel its full burning heat. "Hamiyah" means extremely hot, blazing, at its most intense. This is not a metaphor. Allah is describing the actual punishment that awaits those who reject the truth. The fire of the Hereafter is incomparably hotter than any fire in this world. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Your fire is one-seventieth part of the Fire of Hell." (Sahih Bukhari 3265)

5
تُسْقَىٰ مِنْ عَيْنٍ آنِيَةٍ
Tusqa min 'aynin aaniyah
They will be given drink from a boiling spring.

Word by Word

تُسْقَىٰtusqaThey will be given to drink
مِنْminFrom
عَيْنٍ'ayninA spring
آنِيَةٍaaniyahBoiling hot
Simple Explanation

When they are burning with thirst and desperately seeking relief, they will be given a drink - but not cool water. They will be given boiling water from a spring that has reached its maximum heat ("aaniyah" means it has reached the peak of boiling). Instead of quenching their thirst, it will scald them from the inside. This is the opposite of what a "spring" usually represents - life and refreshment. Everything in the Hellfire is an inversion of comfort.

6
لَّيْسَ لَهُمْ طَعَامٌ إِلَّا مِن ضَرِيعٍ
Laysa lahum ta'aamun illa min daree'
For them there will be no food except from a poisonous, thorny plant.

Word by Word

طَعَامٌta'aamunFood
إِلَّاillaExcept

Simple Explanation

"Dari'" is a dry, thorny, bitter plant that even animals refuse to eat. Ibn Abbas said it is a plant in the Hellfire with thorns. It is foul, bitter, and provides no nutrition at all. The Arabs knew this plant - even their camels would avoid it. So imagine that being your only food. It cannot satisfy hunger, it cannot nourish, it only adds to the torment. The next verse confirms this.

7
لَّا يُسْمِنُ وَلَا يُغْنِي مِن جُوعٍ

يُغْنِيyughniIt avails / suffices

Simple Explanation

This verse drives the point home: their food is completely useless. "La yusminu" - it doesn't nourish or strengthen the body. "Wa la yughni min joo'" - and it doesn't even satisfy hunger. So they eat but remain starving, drink but remain burning with thirst. Every basic human need that we take for granted - food, water, comfort - is inverted in the Hellfire. This should make us grateful for every simple meal we have today.

8
وُجُوهٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ نَّاعِمَةٌ
Wujoohun yawma'idhin naa'imah
[Other] faces, that Day, will show pleasure.

Word by Word

وُجُوهٌwujoohunFaces
يَوْمَئِذٍyawma'idhinThat Day
نَّاعِمَةٌnaa'imahShowing pleasure / radiant
Simple Explanation

Now comes the beautiful contrast. "Na'imah" comes from "na'im" meaning bliss, comfort, and delight. These faces are glowing with pleasure and happiness. While the first group's faces are humiliated and exhausted, this group's faces radiate joy. The face reflects the soul's state - and their souls are at peace because they lived for Allah's pleasure in this world. What a contrast from just one verse ago!

9
لِّسَعْيِهَا رَاضِيَةٌ
Lisa'yiha raadiyah
With their effort [they are] satisfied.

Word by Word

لِّسَعْيِهَاlisa'yihaWith their effort / striving
رَاضِيَةٌraadiyahSatisfied / pleased
Simple Explanation

"Sa'y" means effort, striving, and hard work. "Radiyah" means satisfied and pleased. These people are satisfied with their efforts because they see the reward. Every prayer they prayed, every fast they kept, every act of charity, every moment of patience - it all paid off beyond imagination. In this world, you sometimes wonder: "Is my worship enough? Will it count?" This verse answers: yes, and you will be fully satisfied with what you get in return.

10
فِي جَنَّةٍ عَالِيَةٍ
Fee jannatin 'aaliyah
In an elevated garden.

Word by Word

فِيfeeIn
جَنَّةٍjannatinA garden (Paradise)
عَالِيَةٍ'aaliyahElevated / lofty
Simple Explanation

"Jannah" means a garden so lush and dense with trees that the ground is hidden. "'Aaliyah" means elevated, high, and lofty - both in physical location and in rank and status. As-Sa'di explains that it is elevated in its buildings, its levels, and its pleasures. Everything in it is high, noble, and superior. The higher the garden, the more beautiful the view, the fresher the air, the more exclusive the company.

11
لَّا تَسْمَعُ فِيهَا لَاغِيَةً
La tasma'u feeha laaghiyah
Wherein they will hear no unsuitable speech.

Word by Word

لَّاlaNot
تَسْمَعُtasma'uYou will hear
فِيهَاfeehaIn it
لَاغِيَةًlaaghiyahVain / idle / unsuitable talk
Simple Explanation

"Laghiyah" means idle, vain, false, or hurtful speech. In Paradise, there is no gossip, no backbiting, no arguments, no lies, no hurtful words. Think about how much pain is caused by words in this world - insults, rumors, harsh criticism. In Jannah, all of that is removed. Every word spoken there is beautiful, peaceful, and meaningful. This is one of the most underappreciated blessings of Paradise - perfect peace of mind.

12
فِيهَا عَيْنٌ جَارِيَةٌ
Feeha 'aynun jaariyah
Within it is a flowing spring.

Word by Word

فِيهَاfeehaIn it
عَيْنٌ'aynunA spring
جَارِيَةٌjaariyahFlowing / running
Simple Explanation

Notice the contrast with verse 5. The people of the Fire are given boiling water from a spring ("'aynin aaniyah"). The people of Paradise have a flowing spring ("'aynun jaariyah") - cool, fresh, and constantly flowing. The Arabic word "jaariyah" means continuously running, never stopping, never drying up. In the desert climate of Arabia, a flowing spring was the ultimate luxury. In Jannah, it's just one of many blessings.

13
فِيهَا سُرُرٌ مَّرْفُوعَةٌ
Feeha sururun marfoo'ah
Within it are couches raised high.

Word by Word

فِيهَاfeehaIn it
سُرُرٌsururunCouches / thrones
مَّرْفُوعَةٌmarfoo'ahRaised high / elevated
Simple Explanation

"Surur" is the plural of "sarir" - a couch, throne, or reclining seat. "Marfu'ah" means raised high and elevated. These are not ordinary seats. They are elevated thrones from which the people of Paradise can overlook the beauty of their surroundings. Ibn Kathir says they are raised so that when the people of Paradise sit on them, they can see all the blessings Allah has prepared for them. It's a position of honor and royalty.

14
وَأَكْوَابٌ مَّوْضُوعَةٌ
Wa akwaabun mawdoo'ah
And cups put in place.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd
أَكْوَابٌakwaabunCups / goblets
مَّوْضُوعَةٌmawdoo'ahPut in place / set ready
Simple Explanation

"Akwab" are cups without handles - elegant drinking vessels. "Mawdu'ah" means placed, set out, and ready to be used. They are already prepared and waiting. You don't have to ask, you don't have to wait, you don't have to serve yourself. Everything is arranged for you in advance. This paints a picture of ultimate hospitality - a banquet where the host has thought of every detail before the guest even arrives.

15
وَنَمَارِقُ مَصْفُوفَةٌ
Wa namaariqu masfoofah
And cushions lined up.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd
نَمَارِقُnamaariquCushions / pillows
مَصْفُوفَةٌmasfoofahLined up / arranged in rows
Simple Explanation

"Namariq" are luxurious cushions or pillows used for leaning and reclining. "Masfoofah" means arranged in rows, lined up beautifully. Picture the most luxurious gathering you've ever seen - cushions everywhere, arranged perfectly, inviting you to sit and relax. This is the atmosphere of Paradise: comfort, beauty, and order. No stress, no rush, no discomfort. Just pure ease and relaxation in the company of the righteous.

16
وَزَرَابِيُّ مَبْثُوثَةٌ
Wa zaraabiyyu mabthuthah
And carpets spread around.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd
زَرَابِيُّzaraabiyyuRich carpets / rugs
مَبْثُوثَةٌmabthuthahSpread out / scattered
Simple Explanation

"Zarabiyy" are rich, luxurious carpets - the finest quality. "Mabthuthah" means spread out everywhere, scattered abundantly. Not just in one room, but everywhere you look. The picture is now complete: elevated thrones, ready cups, lined-up cushions, and rich carpets spread across the ground. Allah uses things the Arabs understood as symbols of wealth and comfort, but the reality of Paradise is far beyond anything we can imagine. These descriptions are just to give us a taste.

17
أَفَلَا يَنظُرُونَ إِلَى الْإِبِلِ كَيْفَ خُلِقَتْ
Afala yanzuroona ilal-ibili kayfa khuliqat
Then do they not look at the camels - how they are created?

Word by Word

أَفَلَاafalaThen do they not
يَنظُرُونَyanzuroonaLook / reflect
إِلَىilaAt / upon
الْإِبِلِal-ibiliThe camels
كَيْفَkayfaHow
خُلِقَتْkhuliqatThey were created
Simple Explanation

After describing the Hereafter, Allah now shifts to creation. Why the camel? Because for the Arabs, the camel was the most important and remarkable animal in their daily life. It can survive days without water, carry heavy loads across scorching deserts, provide milk, meat, leather, and transportation. Its design is extraordinary: padded feet for sand, the ability to close its nostrils against sandstorms, fat stored in its hump, and the ability to drink huge amounts of water at once. All of this points to a Designer who created it with purpose.

18
وَإِلَى السَّمَاءِ كَيْفَ رُفِعَتْ

And at the sky - how it is raised?

Word by Word

السَّمَاءِas-samaa'iThe sky
كَيْفَkayfaHow
رُفِعَتْrufi'atIt was raised
Simple Explanation

From looking down at the camel, Allah directs our gaze upward. Look at the sky - how it was raised without any pillars you can see. It's a vast, immense canopy that doesn't fall, doesn't crack, and doesn't collapse. Every day we look at the sky and take it for granted. But who raised it? Who maintains it? The sky with its stars, clouds, rain, and perfect balance is one of the most visible signs of Allah's power. All you have to do is look up and reflect.

19
وَإِلَى الْجِبَالِ كَيْفَ نُصِبَتْ
Wa ilal-jibaali kayfa nusibat
And at the mountains - how they are erected?

Word by Word

وَإِلَىwa ilaAnd at
الْجِبَالِal-jibaaliThe mountains
كَيْفَkayfaHow
نُصِبَتْnusibatThey were erected / set firm
Simple Explanation

"Nusibat" means firmly planted, erected, and established. Mountains are not just piles of rock. They are deeply rooted structures - modern geology confirms they have roots extending deep into the earth, stabilizing the ground. The Quran calls them "pegs" (awatad) in Surah An-Naba. For the Arabs traveling through desert and mountain terrain, these massive structures were a daily reminder of a power far greater than themselves. Who set these enormous structures in place? Who keeps the earth stable?

20
وَإِلَى الْأَرْضِ كَيْفَ سُطِحَتْ

كَيْفَkayfaHow

"Sutihat" means spread out, made flat and accessible for living. This refers to how the earth's surface is made livable - smooth enough to walk on, build on, farm on, and travel across. Despite the earth being a sphere, its surface appears spread out and flat from our perspective, making life possible. These four signs (camel, sky, mountains, earth) form a complete picture: look at the animals around you, the sky above you, the mountains beside you, and the ground beneath you. All point to one Creator.

21
فَذَكِّرْ إِنَّمَا أَنتَ مُذَكِّرٌ
Fadhakkir innama anta mudhakkir
So remind, [O Muhammad]; you are only a reminder.

أَنتَantaYou are

Simple Explanation

After presenting the evidence of the Hereafter (verses 1-16) and the evidence in creation (verses 17-20), Allah now tells the Prophet: your job is to remind. "Fadhakkir" - just keep reminding. "Innama anta mudhakkir" - you are ONLY a reminder. You don't control hearts. You can't force anyone to believe. Your role is to deliver the message, not to guarantee the outcome. This is a profound lesson for anyone doing da'wah: present the truth with sincerity, but leave the result to Allah.

22
لَّسْتَ عَلَيْهِم بِمُصَيْطِرٍ

You are not over them a controller.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

"Musaytir" means a controller, a dictator, someone who forces others. Allah is clearly stating: you have no authority to force anyone into faith. This verse establishes one of the most important principles in Islam - there is no compulsion in religion. Faith must come from the heart willingly. The Prophet's role (and by extension, the role of every Muslim) is to invite, remind, and present the truth. The rest is between each person and Allah.

23
إِلَّا مَن تَوَلَّىٰ وَكَفَرَ

While the Prophet cannot force anyone, that doesn't mean there are no consequences. "Tawalla" means to turn away, to deliberately walk away from the truth after it has been presented. "Kafar" means to disbelieve, to reject and cover the truth. This describes someone who has heard the message, seen the signs, been reminded - and still deliberately turns their back and rejects it. This is not about someone who never heard the message. This is about active, deliberate rejection after knowing the truth.

Freedom comes with responsibility, and the ultimate reckoning is with Allah alone.

26
ثُمَّ إِنَّ عَلَيْنَا حِسَابَهُمْ

The surah ends with a powerful closing statement: "Upon Us is their hisab (reckoning/account)." Allah takes the responsibility of judgment upon Himself. Not the Prophet, not any human, not any angel - Allah alone will judge. "Hisab" means a detailed, precise accounting of every deed, word, and intention. This final verse brings everything full circle: the Overwhelming Event (verse 1) is the Day when this account will be settled. The surah began with a question about that Day and ends with the certainty that it will happen, and Allah Himself will be the Judge.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Ghashiyah
  • ◆
    The Day of Judgment is real and overwhelming.

    It will cover and engulf everything. No one can escape it, ignore it, or deny it forever. The surah opens with this reality to shake us out of heedlessness and complacency.

  • ◆
    Effort without guidance leads to exhaustion without reward.

    The description of "working hard and exhausted" (verse 3) is a warning: it's not just about working hard, but about working on the right things with the right intention. Sincerity and correct belief are essential.

  • ◆
    Every blessing of Paradise contrasts with a torment of the Fire.

    Boiling springs vs. flowing springs. Thorny food vs. abundant feasts. Humiliated faces vs. radiant faces. This contrast is meant to motivate us toward the right path while there is still time.

  • ◆
    The signs of Allah are all around us - we just need to look.

    The camel, the sky, the mountains, the earth - these are not hidden signs. They are right in front of us, every single day. Reflection on creation is one of the most powerful forms of worship.

  • ◆
    Our role is to remind, not to control.

    Even the Prophet (peace be upon him) was told he is only a reminder, not a controller. We present the truth with wisdom and kindness, and leave the results to Allah. There is no compulsion in faith.

  • ◆
    Everyone returns to Allah, and the account is with Him alone.

    No matter what happens in this world, the final reckoning is with Allah. This should give us patience when we see injustice, and urgency to prepare for the Day when every detail will be accounted for.
