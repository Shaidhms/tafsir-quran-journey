The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Qari'ah is one of the most powerful short surahs in the Quran. The word "Al-Qari'ah" comes from the Arabic root meaning "to strike" or "to knock." It refers to the Day of Judgment, which will strike the hearts and ears of all creation with terror.

Allah opens by naming the event, then immediately asks: "What is it? And what can make you understand what it is?" This rhetorical technique is used in the Quran to show that something is so immense it cannot be fully grasped by the human mind.

The surah then gives two vivid images: people will scatter like moths drawn helplessly to a flame, and the mighty mountains will crumble into fluffy, colored wool. If the strongest things on Earth cannot withstand this Day, how can any human be arrogant before Allah?

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Tafsir As-Sa'di

The second half introduces the Meezan (the Scale). Every person's deeds will be weighed. Those whose good deeds are heavy will enter a life of bliss in Jannah. Those whose scales are light will fall into the Hawiyah, a name for the deepest part of Hellfire, described as an intensely hot fire.

Ibn Kathir explains that "his mother is Hawiyah" means just as a child returns to its mother for comfort, the disbeliever's only destination is the abyss. As-Sa'di adds that the lightness of the scale means a person's faith and good deeds were insufficient, either due to disbelief or persistent neglect of what Allah commanded.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

Al-Qari'ah
The Striking Calamity.

Word by Word (right to left)

الْقَارِعَةُal-qari'ahThe Striking Calamity
Simple Explanation

The surah opens with just one word: Al-Qari'ah. It comes from "qar'a" meaning to strike or knock violently. It refers to the Day of Judgment, which will strike everything in existence with a force beyond imagination. Allah uses this dramatic opening to immediately capture attention and create a sense of awe.

2
مَا الْقَارِعَةُ
Mal-Qari'ah
What is the Striking Calamity?

Word by Word (right to left)

مَاmaWhat
الْقَارِعَةُal-qari'ahThe Striking Calamity
Simple Explanation

Allah asks a rhetorical question to build suspense and magnify the event. "What is this Qari'ah?" The question is not seeking an answer; it is meant to make us pause and reflect on how enormous this event truly is. This technique of questioning appears throughout the Quran for events of extreme significance.

3
وَمَا أَدْرَاكَ مَا الْقَارِعَةُ
Wa ma adraka mal-Qari'ah
And what can make you know what the Striking Calamity is?

Word by Word (right to left)

وَمَاwa maAnd what
أَدْرَاكَadrakaCan make you know
مَاmaWhat
الْقَارِعَةُal-qari'ahThe Striking Calamity
Simple Explanation

"Wa ma adraka" is a special Quranic phrase that indicates something is beyond human comprehension. Ibn Kathir notes that every time this phrase appears in the Quran, Allah follows it with an explanation, because the matter is so great that humans need divine clarification. It emphasizes that no amount of human imagination can truly capture the reality of the Day of Judgment.

4
يَوْمَ يَكُونُ النَّاسُ كَالْفَرَاشِ الْمَبْثُوثِ
Yawma yakunun-nasu kal-farashil-mabthuth
It is the Day when people will be like moths, dispersed.

Word by Word (right to left)

يَوْمَyawmaThe Day
يَكُونُyakunuWill be
النَّاسُan-nasuThe people
كَالْفَرَاشِkal-farashiLike moths
الْمَبْثُوثِal-mabthuthScattered / dispersed
Simple Explanation

"Farash" refers to moths or small insects that fly erratically around a light source. "Mabthuth" means scattered and spread out everywhere. The image is of people in utter confusion, running in all directions without purpose, just as moths chaotically swarm toward a flame. They are overwhelmed, disoriented, and helpless. As-Sa'di explains this shows how people will lose all sense of control and direction on that Day.

5
وَتَكُونُ الْجِبَالُ كَالْعِهْنِ الْمَنفُوشِ
Wa takunul-jibalu kal-'ihnil-manfush
And the mountains will be like wool, fluffed up.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَتَكُونُwa takunuAnd will be
الْجِبَالُal-jibaluThe mountains
كَالْعِهْنِkal-'ihniLike (dyed) wool
الْمَنفُوشِal-manfushFluffed up / carded
Simple Explanation

"Al-'ihn" specifically means dyed wool, not plain white. "Al-manfush" means carded or fluffed, like when wool is pulled apart into airy pieces. Ibn Kathir says the mountains, despite being the most solid and massive structures on Earth, will be reduced to weightless, colorful fibers floating in the air. The use of "dyed" wool may refer to the different colors of mountains (white, red, black) mentioned elsewhere in the Quran. This imagery shows that nothing in this world is permanent except Allah.

6
فَأَمَّا مَن ثَقُلَتْ مَوَازِينُهُ
Fa-amma man thaqulat mawazeenuh
Then as for one whose scales are heavy [with good deeds].

Word by Word (right to left)

فَأَمَّاfa-ammaThen as for
مَنmanThe one who
ثَقُلَتْthaqulatAre heavy
مَوَازِينُهُmawazeenuhHis scales / balances
Simple Explanation

"Mawazeen" is the plural of "meezan" (scale/balance). On the Day of Judgment, every person's deeds will be placed on a real scale. The heaviest thing that can be placed on the scale is "La ilaha illallah" (there is no god but Allah). Good deeds done with sincerity and correct faith will have weight. Ibn Kathir explains that both the deeds and the records of deeds will be weighed, and Allah will judge with perfect justice.

7
فَهُوَ فِي عِيشَةٍ رَّاضِيَةٍ
Fa-huwa fi 'eeshatin radiyah
He will be in a pleasant life.

Word by Word (right to left)

فَهُوَfa-huwaThen he (will be)
فِيfiIn
عِيشَةٍ'eeshatinA life
رَّاضِيَةٍradiyahPleasant / satisfying
Simple Explanation

"'Eeshatin radiyah" means a life that is completely satisfying, where the person is fully content. As-Sa'di explains this refers to Paradise (Jannah), where there is no sadness, no fatigue, no worry, and no end. Everything the soul desires will be there. The word "radiyah" implies not just that the life is pleasant, but that the person living it is pleased and content. It is the ultimate reward for a life spent in faith and good deeds.

8
وَأَمَّا مَنْ خَفَّتْ مَوَازِينُهُ
Wa amma man khaffat mawazeenuh
But as for one whose scales are light.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَأَمَّاwa ammaBut as for
مَنْmanThe one who
خَفَّتْkhaffatAre light
مَوَازِينُهُmawazeenuhHis scales / balances
Simple Explanation

"Khaffat" means light, as in having little weight. This person's good deeds were not enough to outweigh their sins. Ibn Kathir explains that this could be due to disbelief (which nullifies all good deeds) or due to persistent sin and negligence. The contrast between "thaqulat" (heavy) and "khaffat" (light) shows that the Day of Judgment is about real, tangible results. Every prayer, every act of charity, every moment of patience adds weight to the scale.

9
فَأُمُّهُ هَاوِيَةٌ
Fa-ummahu hawiyah
His refuge will be an abyss (Hawiyah).

Word by Word (right to left)

فَأُمُّهُfa-ummahuThen his mother
هَاوِيَةٌhawiyahThe Abyss (Hawiyah)
Simple Explanation

This is one of the most striking verses. "Umm" literally means "mother." Ibn Kathir and other scholars explain this in two ways: (1) Just as a child falls into the arms of its mother, this person will fall headfirst into the Hawiyah, which becomes their "home" or final destination. (2) The word "umm" can also mean "head" in Arabic, as in "umm ar-ra's" (top of the head), meaning they will fall into the pit head-first. "Hawiyah" comes from "hawa" meaning to fall from a great height. It is a name for the deepest part of the Hellfire.

10
وَمَا أَدْرَاكَ مَا هِيَهْ
Wa ma adraka ma hiyah
And what can make you know what that is?

Word by Word (right to left)

وَمَاwa maAnd what
أَدْرَاكَadrakaCan make you know
مَاmaWhat
هِيَهْhiyahIt is
Simple Explanation

Again Allah uses the phrase "wa ma adraka" to emphasize how incomprehensible the Hawiyah is. This is the second time this phrase appears in this surah. The first time (verse 3) it was about the Day of Judgment itself; now it is about the punishment that awaits. The repetition builds a crescendo of warning. Allah is about to reveal the final, terrifying truth.

11
نَارٌ حَامِيَةٌ
Narun hamiyah
It is a Fire, intensely hot.

Word by Word (right to left)

نَارٌnarunA fire
حَامِيَةٌhamiyahIntensely hot / blazing
Simple Explanation

The surah ends with two devastating words: "Narun hamiyah." "Nar" means fire, and "hamiyah" means intensely hot, having reached the extreme of heat. Ibn Kathir mentions that Abu Hurayrah (may Allah be pleased with him) narrated that the fire of this world is only one-seventieth of the fire of the Hereafter. This fire has been burning since its creation and its heat is beyond anything in human experience. The brevity of this ending, just two words, makes it even more impactful.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Qari'ah
  • ◆
    The Day of Judgment is real and terrifying.

    Allah uses vivid imagery to help us understand that this Day is beyond anything we can imagine. Preparation through faith and good deeds is not optional.

  • ◆
    Nothing in this world is permanent.

    If mountains, the most solid structures on Earth, will become like fluffy wool, then our wealth, status, and power mean nothing on that Day. Only deeds matter.

  • ◆
    Every deed is weighed with perfect justice.

    Allah does not wrong anyone. The Scale is precise. Even a mustard seed's weight of good will be seen. This should motivate us to never belittle any good deed.

  • ◆
    The outcome is binary: eternal bliss or eternal fire.

    There is no middle ground. Either the scale is heavy and you enter Jannah, or it is light and the Hawiyah awaits. This clarity should drive urgency in our worship.

  • ◆
    Sincere faith is the heaviest thing on the Scale.

    The testimony of faith (La ilaha illallah) and sincere belief in Allah are what give weight to all other deeds. Without faith, good deeds have no weight in the Hereafter.
