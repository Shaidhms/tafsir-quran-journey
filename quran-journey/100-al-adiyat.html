The Story Behind This Surah

This surah opens with a powerful, cinematic oath. Allah swears by war horses charging into battle - their heavy panting, the sparks flying from their hooves striking rocky ground, their dawn raids, the dust clouds they kick up, and how they plunge into the heart of the enemy. It is one of the most vivid and dramatic openings in the entire Quran.

But why does Allah swear by horses? The point is not the horses themselves. The oath builds up to a shocking contrast: these horses are loyal, obedient, and brave for their riders. They charge without hesitation. Yet the human being - the one who rides them - is ungrateful to his own Lord who gave him everything.

Ibn Kathir explains that the horses display more loyalty to their human masters than humans display to Allah. The horse obeys, charges into danger, and serves faithfully. But man, despite receiving countless blessings, is "kanud" - deeply ungrateful, counting his hardships while forgetting his blessings.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Tafsir As-Sa'di

The surah then shifts to man's obsessive love of wealth. As-Sa'di notes that "shadid" (intense/fierce) describes how tightly humans cling to material possessions, often at the expense of their relationship with Allah and their duties toward others.

Finally, the surah delivers a wake-up call: a Day is coming when graves will be overturned, every secret of the heart will be exposed, and Allah - Al-Khabir, the All-Aware - will judge with complete knowledge. Nothing will be hidden.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَالْعَادِيَاتِ ضَبْحًا
Wal-'adiyati dabha
By the racers, panting.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَالْعَادِيَاتِwal-'adiyatiBy the chargers / racers
ضَبْحًاdabhaPanting / breathing hard
Simple Explanation

Allah begins with an oath by war horses that charge at full gallop, panting heavily. The word "dabh" refers to the loud breathing sound horses make when they run hard. Ibn Kathir says this refers to horses in jihad (striving in Allah's cause), galloping so hard that their breath becomes audible. Allah swears by these creatures to highlight something important that comes next.

2
فَالْمُورِيَاتِ قَدْحًا
Fal-muriyati qadha
And the producers of sparks when striking.

Word by Word (right to left)

فَالْمُورِيَاتِfal-muriyatiAnd the ones striking fire
قَدْحًاqadhaProducing sparks
Simple Explanation

When horses gallop over rocky ground at high speed, their iron hooves strike the stones and produce sparks. This is the image Allah paints - the dramatic sight of sparks flying in the darkness as these powerful horses charge. It shows the intensity and force of their movement. As-Sa'di points out this detail emphasizes the violence and power of the charge.

3
فَالْمُغِيرَاتِ صُبْحًا
Fal-mughirati subha
And the chargers at dawn.

Word by Word (right to left)

فَالْمُغِيرَاتِfal-mughiratiAnd the raiders / chargers
صُبْحًاsubhaAt dawn / morning
Simple Explanation

Dawn raids were a common military tactic in Arabia. The horses launch their attack at the break of dawn ("subh"), when the enemy is sleeping and least prepared. Ibn Kathir mentions that the Prophet (peace be upon him) would often wait until dawn before deciding whether to engage. The timing of dawn adds to the dramatic imagery of this oath.

4
فَأَثَرْنَ بِهِ نَقْعًا
Fa-atharna bihi naq'a
Stirring up thereby clouds of dust.

Word by Word (right to left)

فَأَثَرْنَfa-atharnaThen they raised / stirred up
بِهِbihiThereby / by it
نَقْعًاnaq'aDust / clouds of dust
Simple Explanation

As the horses charge across the desert, they kick up massive clouds of dust ("naq'"). The dust fills the air and creates a dramatic scene. This continues the vivid picture - you can almost feel the ground shaking and see the dust billowing behind these powerful animals as they race forward.

5
فَوَسَطْنَ بِهِ جَمْعًا
Fa-wasatna bihi jam'a
Arriving thereby in the center collectively.

Word by Word (right to left)

فَوَسَطْنَfa-wasatnaThen they penetrated the center
بِهِbihiThereby / by it
جَمْعًاjam'aA gathering / enemy ranks
Simple Explanation

The climax of the battle scene: the horses plunge right into the middle ("wasat") of the enemy ranks ("jam'"). They don't stop at the edges - they charge straight into the heart of the enemy forces. This shows incredible bravery and obedience. As-Sa'di explains this completes the oath sequence, building from the sound of panting to the final impact of battle.

6
إِنَّ الْإِنسَانَ لِرَبِّهِ لَكَنُودٌ
Innal-insana lirabbihi lakanud
Indeed mankind, to his Lord, is ungrateful.

Word by Word (right to left)

إِنَّinnaIndeed / verily
الْإِنسَانَal-insanaThe human being
لِرَبِّهِlirabbihiTo his Lord
لَكَنُودٌlakanudSurely ungrateful
Simple Explanation

THIS is the main point of all five oaths. The word "kanud" means deeply ungrateful - someone who remembers hardships but forgets blessings, who counts what they lost but ignores what they received. Ibn Kathir explains: the horse is loyal and obedient to its rider, yet man - who has far more reason to be grateful - is disloyal and ungrateful to the One who created him, sustains him, and blesses him constantly.

7
وَإِنَّهُ عَلَىٰ ذَٰلِكَ لَشَهِيدٌ
Wa-innahu 'ala dhalika lashahid
And indeed, he is to that a witness.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَإِنَّهُwa-innahuAnd indeed he
عَلَىٰ ذَٰلِكَ'ala dhalikaTo / upon that
لَشَهِيدٌlashahidSurely a witness
Simple Explanation

There are two opinions among scholars about who "he" refers to. Some say it means man himself is a witness to his own ingratitude - deep down, he knows he is ungrateful. Others say it refers to Allah being a witness. Ibn Kathir mentions both views. Either way, the message is clear: ingratitude cannot be hidden. Whether you admit it to yourself or not, it is known.

8
وَإِنَّهُ لِحُبِّ الْخَيْرِ لَشَدِيدٌ
Wa-innahu lihubbil-khayri lashadid
And indeed he is, in love of wealth, intense.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَإِنَّهُwa-innahuAnd indeed he
لِحُبِّlihubbiIn love of
الْخَيْرِal-khayriWealth / good things
لَشَدِيدٌlashadidIntense / fierce
Simple Explanation

The word "khayr" here means wealth/material goods (not "goodness" in the moral sense). "Shadid" means intense, fierce, or extreme. Man's love for money and possessions is so strong that it often overrides his gratitude to Allah. As-Sa'di notes that this intense love of wealth leads people to withhold charity, hoard money, and forget that everything they have is a trust from Allah that will be asked about.

9
أَفَلَا يَعْلَمُ إِذَا بُعْثِرَ مَا فِي الْقُبُورِ
Afala ya'lamu idha bu'thira ma fil-qubur
But does he not know that when the contents of the graves are scattered.

Word by Word (right to left)

أَفَلَاafalaDoes he not
يَعْلَمُya'lamuKnow
إِذَاidhaWhen
بُعْثِرَbu'thiraIs scattered / overturned
مَا فِيma fiWhat is in
الْقُبُورِal-quburThe graves
Simple Explanation

Now comes the warning. "Bu'thira" means to scatter, overturn, and bring out what is buried. On the Day of Resurrection, the graves will be opened and the dead will rise. This is a rhetorical question: doesn't this ungrateful, wealth-obsessed person realize that a Day is coming when none of his wealth will matter? Ibn Kathir explains: the earth will throw out its dead, and everyone will stand before Allah for judgment.

10
وَحُصِّلَ مَا فِي الصُّدُورِ
Wa-hussila ma fis-sudur
And that within the breasts is obtained.

Word by Word (right to left)

وَحُصِّلَwa-hussilaAnd is made apparent / collected
مَا فِيma fiWhat is in
الصُّدُورِas-sudurThe breasts / chests (hearts)
Simple Explanation

"Hussila" means to extract, separate, and make apparent. "Sudur" (chests/breasts) refers to the hearts where intentions, beliefs, and secrets are stored. On that Day, everything you hid in your heart will be brought out into the open. Your secret intentions, hidden jealousy, unexpressed gratitude or ingratitude - all of it will be exposed. As-Sa'di says this means the good will be separated from the bad, and nothing will remain concealed.

11
إِنَّ رَبَّهُم بِهِمْ يَوْمَئِذٍ لَّخَبِيرٌ
Inna rabbahum bihim yawma'idhin lakhabir
Indeed, their Lord with them, that Day, is fully Acquainted.

Word by Word (right to left)

إِنَّinnaIndeed
رَبَّهُمrabbahumTheir Lord
بِهِمْbihimAbout them / with them
يَوْمَئِذٍyawma'idhinThat Day
لَّخَبِيرٌlakhabirFully Acquainted / All-Aware
Simple Explanation

The surah closes with the Name of Allah: Al-Khabir (The All-Aware, The Fully Acquainted). Allah knows everything about every person - not just their outward actions but their deepest intentions, their secrets, and everything they tried to hide. Ibn Kathir notes: Allah already knows everything NOW, but the emphasis on "that Day" means on the Day of Judgment, this knowledge will be used to judge and recompense. Nothing escapes His awareness.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Adiyat
  • ◆
    Gratitude is the foundation of faith.

    The core message: humans are ungrateful by nature. Recognizing and fighting this tendency is essential. Count your blessings before counting your problems.

  • ◆
    Even animals can teach us loyalty.

    The war horse is more faithful to its master than many humans are to Allah. A horse charges into danger without complaint, yet we complain about small tests while ignoring massive blessings.

  • ◆
    Love of wealth is a trap.

    Wealth itself is not evil, but obsessive love of it leads to hoarding, neglecting charity, and forgetting Allah. Use wealth as a tool, not as a goal.

  • ◆
    Nothing stays hidden forever.

    Every secret intention, hidden deed, and concealed motive will be brought to light on the Day of Judgment. Live as though your heart is transparent.

  • ◆
    Allah is Al-Khabir - The All-Aware.

    Knowing that Allah is fully acquainted with everything should inspire both humility and hope. Humility because nothing is hidden, and hope because even your unseen good deeds are known and rewarded.
