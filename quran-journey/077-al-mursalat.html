The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Mursalat was revealed in Makkah and is one of the most powerful warning surahs in the Quran. Its name, "Those Sent Forth," refers to the winds or angels sent by Allah. The surah is built around a devastating refrain that appears ten times: "Woe that Day to the deniers!" This repetition hammers the message home with increasing intensity, leaving no room for doubt or dismissal.

The surah opens with a series of magnificent oaths by winds that blow in succession, by storms that scatter, by verses that distinguish truth from falsehood. These oaths build to a single conclusion: what you have been promised, the Day of Judgment, is absolutely going to happen. The stars will be extinguished, the sky will be split open, the mountains will be blown away like dust.

Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (may Allah be pleased with him) narrated: "We were with the Prophet (peace be upon him) in a cave at Mina when Surah Al-Mursalat was revealed. He was reciting it and I was learning it from his mouth. Indeed, his mouth was moist with it when a snake came out at us. The Prophet said, 'Kill it!' We rushed to kill it but it escaped. The Prophet said, 'It was saved from your harm just as you were saved from its harm.'"

Source: Sahih al-Bukhari 1830, Sahih Muslim 2234

After the oaths, the surah recounts the destruction of former nations who denied their messengers, then reminds humans of their own humble origins from a drop of fluid placed in a secure lodging. It asks: did We not create you from a worthless fluid? Did We not make the earth a container for the living and the dead? Did We not place firm mountains and give you fresh water? These are reminders of Allah's power and generosity, yet the deniers turn away.

The surah closes with a scene of the Day of Judgment: the deniers cannot speak, cannot make excuses, cannot prostrate even if told to do so. Their hearts are sealed. The final verse delivers a haunting question: "Then in what message after this will they believe?" If the Quran itself cannot shake them awake, nothing will.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَالْمُرْسَلَاتِ عُرْفًا
Wal-mursalati 'urfa
By those [winds] sent forth in succession.

Word by Word

وَالْمُرْسَلَاتِwal-mursalatiBy those sent forth
عُرْفًا'urfaIn succession / one after another
Simple Explanation

The surah begins with an oath. "Al-Mursalat" refers to those sent forth. Ibn Kathir mentions multiple opinions: they may be winds sent one after another, or angels sent with revelation and commands. The word "'urfa" means in succession, like waves following each other, or in a recognized and beneficial manner. As-Sa'di explains that Allah swears by these mighty forces to establish the certainty of what follows: the Day of Judgment is real and unavoidable. The oath by winds reminds us of Allah's power over nature, a power that will one day dismantle this entire world.

2
فَالْعَاصِفَاتِ عَصْفًا
Fal-'asifati 'asfa
And the winds that blow violently.

Word by Word

فَالْعَاصِفَاتِfal-'asifatiAnd the blowing violently
عَصْفًا'asfaWith violence / fiercely
Simple Explanation

After the gentle winds come the violent storms. "'Asifat" comes from "'asf" meaning to blow with tremendous force and fury. Ibn Kathir explains these are the fierce winds that uproot trees and destroy structures. As-Sa'di notes the progression: first the winds sent gently in succession, then the violent tempests. This mirrors how Allah's warnings come first gently through messengers, then, if ignored, through devastating punishment. The escalation in the oaths mirrors the escalation of consequence for those who deny.

3
وَالنَّاشِرَاتِ نَشْرًا
Wan-nashirati nashra
And [by] the winds that spread [clouds].

Word by Word

وَالنَّاشِرَاتِwan-nashiratiAnd those that spread
نَشْرًاnashraSpreading widely
Simple Explanation

"An-Nashirat" are those that spread. Ibn Kathir says these are the winds that spread clouds across the sky, bringing rain and mercy. Others say they are angels that spread Allah's commands throughout creation. As-Sa'di explains that "nashr" means to spread, scatter, or distribute widely. Whether winds spreading rain clouds or angels spreading divine decree, the point is the same: Allah has systems that carry out His will across the entire creation. Nothing escapes His administration.

4
فَالْفَارِقَاتِ فَرْقًا

Word by Word

فَالْفَارِقَاتِfal-fariqatiAnd those that separate / distinguish

Simple Explanation

"Al-Fariqat" are those that make a clear distinction and separation. Ibn Kathir explains these are the angels who bring revelation that separates truth from falsehood, lawful from unlawful, guidance from misguidance. As-Sa'di notes that "farq" is the act of making a decisive separation. The Quran itself is called "Al-Furqan" (The Criterion) because it separates truth from falsehood. These verses that come down through angels are the ultimate standard by which all claims are judged.

5
فَالْمُلْقِيَاتِ ذِكْرًا
Fal-mulqiyati dhikra
And those [angels] that deliver a reminder.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

"Al-Mulqiyat" are those that deliver or cast down. "Dhikr" means reminder, remembrance, or message. Ibn Kathir explains these are the angels, particularly Jibril (Gabriel), who deliver revelation to the prophets. The word "alqa" means to throw or deliver, and it conveys the weight and force with which the message is brought down. As-Sa'di notes that the purpose of this delivery is "dhikr," a reminder. Revelation is not just information; it is a wake-up call, meant to remind humans of what they already know deep inside: that they were created with purpose and will return to their Creator.

6
عُذْرًا أَوْ نُذْرًا

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

The reminder delivered by angels serves two purposes. "'Udhr" means to remove excuses. No one can say on the Day of Judgment, "I was not warned." "Nudhr" means warning of consequences. Ibn Kathir explains that the revelation cuts off all excuses and delivers a clear warning of what happens to those who reject. As-Sa'di emphasizes that this verse establishes Allah's perfect justice: He never punishes without first sending clear warning through His messengers. The proof has been delivered. The excuse has been removed. What remains is the choice.

This is the conclusion of the oaths. After swearing by the winds, storms, and angels, Allah declares: what you have been promised will absolutely happen. "Waqi'" means to fall, to occur, to come to pass with certainty. Ibn Kathir explains that "ma tu'aduna" refers to the Day of Judgment, resurrection, and accountability. The word "lawaqi'" with the emphatic "la" prefix makes this an absolute guarantee. As-Sa'di notes that this verse is the answer to all the oaths: the entire purpose of those magnificent swearings was to establish this one truth. The Day of Judgment is coming, and nothing can stop it.

8
فَإِذَا النُّجُومُ طُمِسَتْ

So when the stars are obliterated.

Word by Word

فَإِذَاfa-idhaSo when

طُمِسَتْtumisatAre obliterated / extinguished
Simple Explanation

Now begins the description of how that promised Day will unfold. The stars, those brilliant lights that have guided travelers for millennia, will be "tumisat," meaning erased, extinguished, their light wiped out. Ibn Kathir explains that "tams" means to erase completely, like wiping writing off a board. The stars will lose their light and be scattered. As-Sa'di notes that the stars are mentioned first because they represent the most stable, beautiful features of the night sky. When even the stars are destroyed, you understand that nothing in this universe is permanent except Allah.

9
وَإِذَا السَّمَاءُ فُرِجَتْ

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

The sky will be torn open. "Furijat" comes from "farj" meaning to open, split, or create gaps. Ibn Kathir explains the sky will be rent asunder with massive cracks and openings. It will no longer be the seamless canopy we see above us. As-Sa'di notes this is consistent with other descriptions in the Quran (81:11, 82:1, 84:1) where the sky is described as splitting, folding, and tearing apart on the Day of Judgment. The sky that seemed so permanent and unbreakable will crumble like everything else.

Simple Explanation

The mountains will be uprooted and blown away like dust. "Nusifat" means to be carried away by the wind, to be scattered like chaff. Ibn Kathir explains that the massive mountains that seem immovable will be reduced to fine dust and blown away as if they never existed. As-Sa'di notes that mountains are mentioned because they represent the ultimate symbol of stability and permanence in this world. If even mountains are blown away like nothing, then truly nothing in this world can be relied upon. Only Allah is the Permanent, the Everlasting.

11
وَإِذَا الرُّسُلُ أُقِّتَتْ
Wa idhar-rusulu uqqitat
And when the messengers' time has come.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الرُّسُلُar-rusuluThe messengers
أُقِّتَتْuqqitatTheir time is set / appointed
Simple Explanation

The messengers are gathered at their appointed time. "Uqqitat" comes from "waqt" meaning time, and it means to have their time fixed and set. Ibn Kathir explains this refers to the messengers being gathered to testify against their nations on the Day of Judgment. Each messenger will be asked: did you deliver the message? And each nation will be asked: did the message reach you? As-Sa'di notes that the appointment of a time for the messengers means the Day of Judgment has a fixed schedule that cannot be delayed or brought forward. Everything is precisely timed by Allah.

12
لِأَيِّ يَوْمٍ أُجِّلَتْ
Li-ayyi yawmin ujjilat
For what Day were they postponed?

Word by Word

لِأَيِّli-ayyiFor what / which
يَوْمٍyawminDay
أُجِّلَتْujjilatWere they postponed / delayed
Simple Explanation

A rhetorical question to build awe and suspense. For what great Day were all these events postponed? The question is not seeking an answer; it is creating dread. Ibn Kathir explains that this magnifies the Day of Judgment by asking about its enormity. The postponement ("ujjilat" from "ajal" meaning deadline or appointed term) implies that this Day has been scheduled since the beginning of creation. As-Sa'di notes that the question forces the listener to pause and consider: what kind of Day must it be, that even the testimony of all the prophets is being saved for it?

13
لِيَوْمِ الْفَصْلِ
Li-yawmil-fasl
For the Day of Judgement.

Word by Word

لِيَوْمِli-yawmiFor the Day of
الْفَصْلِal-faslJudgement / separation / decision
Simple Explanation

The answer: for the Day of Separation, the Day of Decision. "Al-Fasl" means to separate, to judge, to make a final decisive ruling between parties. Ibn Kathir explains this is one of the names of the Day of Judgment because on that Day, Allah will separate the truthful from the liars, the believers from the disbelievers, the people of Paradise from the people of Hell. As-Sa'di notes that "fasl" implies finality. There will be no appeal, no second hearing, no overturning of the verdict. This is the ultimate and final court.

14

"Wa ma adraka" is one of the most powerful phrases in the Quran. It means: what could possibly give you any idea of what this is? It conveys that the reality of this Day is beyond human comprehension. Ibn Kathir notes that whenever this phrase appears in the Quran, it indicates something so immense that no human mind can fully grasp it. As-Sa'di explains that this question is meant to fill the heart with awe and the mind with gravity. You think you understand "the Day of Judgment," but you have no idea how terrifying it truly is.

This is the first appearance of the devastating refrain that will echo ten times throughout this surah. "Wayl" is a word of severe punishment and destruction. Some scholars say it is a valley in Hellfire. "Al-Mukadhdhibin" are those who deny, reject, and call the truth a lie. Ibn Kathir explains that "wayl" is the most severe form of warning in Arabic. As-Sa'di notes that each time this refrain appears, it follows a different proof or argument. After each evidence is presented and the case is made stronger, the warning is repeated: woe to those who still deny after all of this.

16
أَلَمْ نُهْلِكِ الْأَوَّلِينَ

A rhetorical question that demands reflection. Did We not destroy the people who came before you? Ibn Kathir explains this refers to nations like the people of Nuh, 'Ad, Thamud, and the people of Lut, all of whom denied their messengers and were utterly destroyed. The question "alam" (did We not) expects the answer "yes, of course You did." As-Sa'di notes that this is both a historical reminder and a direct threat: if Allah destroyed those who denied before, what makes you think you are safe from the same fate?

As-Sa'di notes the word "thumma" (then) indicates that this is an ongoing process. Allah's way (sunnah) with deniers does not change across time or generations. If you deny, you will follow the same path to the same end.

As-Sa'di notes that "kadhalika" (thus, in this way) means the pattern is consistent and predictable. There is no special treatment, no exceptions, no escape route for those who persist in denial. This is Allah's unchanging law.

The refrain returns for the second time. After presenting the evidence that Allah destroyed previous denying nations and will do the same to later ones, the warning is repeated. Those who deny despite seeing what happened to others are especially deserving of this warning.

Allah takes something lowly and places it in something noble, and from that combination creates a human being. This is a sign of His infinite power and wisdom.

For a precisely determined period of time. "Qadar" means measure, and "ma'lum" means known. The pregnancy has a known duration, approximately nine months, which is decreed and known by Allah. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah determines the exact time each child will spend in the womb, the exact moment of birth, and every detail of that process. As-Sa'di notes that "ma'lum" means known to Allah, even though each pregnancy varies slightly. Allah knows the exact moment of every birth before it happens. This precision in creation is yet another proof of Allah's perfect knowledge and power.

His design of the human being is a masterpiece that no creation can replicate.

As-Sa'di explains that denying resurrection after seeing the miracle of your own creation is the height of irrationality.

As-Sa'di notes the profound dual nature of the earth: the same soil that grows your food will one day consume your body. This is a reminder that life and death are inseparable, and both are under Allah's control.

The fourth repetition of the refrain. This time it follows the evidence of the earth: its function as a container for the living and dead, its towering mountains, and its sweet water. All of these are gifts from the Creator. After seeing how Allah designed the earth to sustain your life, you still deny Him? Ibn Kathir and As-Sa'di both note that each "woe" becomes heavier because the evidence becomes stronger. The deniers' position becomes more and more indefensible with each proof.

On the Day of Judgment, the deniers will be commanded: go toward the very thing you used to deny! "Intaliqu" is an imperative meaning proceed, move forward, go. This is said with harshness and contempt. Ibn Kathir explains they are being told to walk toward the Hellfire they used to mock and deny. They used to laugh at warnings of Hell; now they are being marched directly into it. As-Sa'di notes the devastating irony: what they dismissed as a fairy tale is now their inescapable reality. There is no going back, no second chance, no appeal.

30

Go toward a "shadow" of smoke that splits into three columns. The word "zill" (shadow) is used with bitter irony. Normally, a shadow provides relief from heat. Ibn Kathir explains that the smoke of Hell is so vast it divides into three branches, surrounding the deniers from all sides. As-Sa'di notes that calling it a "shadow" is a mockery of their hopes: you wanted shade and comfort? Here is your shade, made of the smoke of the fire you denied.

31

This "shadow" provides no actual shade and no protection whatsoever from the flames. It is shade in name only. "Zalilin" means something that gives cool, comfortable shade, and this smoke has none of that quality. "Yughni" means to benefit, avail, or protect. Ibn Kathir explains this is the complete opposite of what a shadow should be: instead of relief, it adds to the torment. The smoke is hot, suffocating, and provides no escape from the flames beneath. As-Sa'di notes the double negation (la...wa la) emphasizes total deprivation: no shade AND no protection. It is the worst of both worlds.

32

The fire throws sparks as massive as fortresses or palaces. "Sharar" are sparks and embers, and "qasr" means a palace or fortress. Ibn Kathir explains that the sparks flying from this fire are not tiny like sparks from a campfire; each spark is the size of a building. Imagine a fire so enormous that its flying embers are as large as palaces. As-Sa'di notes that this imagery is meant to terrify and wake people up. We can barely endure a tiny spark landing on our skin; what of a spark the size of a fortress?

33

Another vivid comparison: the sparks are like yellow or tawny camels. "Jimalat" is the plural of "jamal" (camel), and "sufr" means yellow or dark tawny. Ibn Kathir explains that the Arabs would have understood this image instantly: large, dark, fast-moving camels stampeding in herds. The sparks fly from the fire like herds of camels, massive and countless. As-Sa'di notes that "sufr" can also mean black, as the Arabs sometimes used "asfar" for very dark colors. The image is of enormous, dark projectiles raining down continuously from the inferno.

34

The fifth repetition of the refrain, this time after the terrifying description of the fire: its useless "shadow" of smoke, its sparks the size of buildings, flying like herds of dark camels. Ibn Kathir notes that the imagery of Hellfire is presented so vividly precisely so that people take warning and change course while they still can. As-Sa'di explains that the increasing intensity of the descriptions matched with the repeated refrain creates a crescendo of warning.

This is a Day when they will not be able to speak. "Yantiqun" comes from "nutq" meaning speech, articulate expression. On that Day, their mouths will be sealed. As-Sa'di notes that this does not contradict other verses where the disbelievers speak on Judgment Day; rather, the Day of Judgment has many stages, and in some stages they will speak, and in others they will be silenced.

Ibn Kathir explains that their excuses would be useless anyway because their own limbs will testify against them. As-Sa'di notes the double deprivation: they cannot speak (verse 35) AND they are not permitted to make excuses. Every avenue of escape is sealed shut.

37
وَيْلٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ لِّلْمُكَذِّبِينَ

The sixth repetition. After being told they cannot speak and cannot make excuses, woe to the deniers. In this world, the tongue was their weapon: they mocked, they argued, they denied, they made excuses. On that Day, even that weapon is taken from them. Ibn Kathir and As-Sa'di both emphasize that this stripping away of the ability to speak is one of the most humiliating aspects of their punishment. The arrogant talkers are reduced to silence.

هَٰذَا يَوْمُ الْفَصْلِ ۖ جَمَعْنَاكُمْ وَالْأَوَّلِينَ

No one is left out, no one is forgotten. As-Sa'di notes that gathering the later people with the earlier ones means everyone faces the same court, the same Judge, and the same standard of justice. History does not provide immunity.

All their worldly power, wealth, alliances, and strategies are worthless before Allah.

The seventh repetition follows the ultimate challenge: plan against Me if you can. They cannot. They are utterly powerless. After being challenged and having nothing to respond with, the refrain strikes again. Ibn Kathir notes that this is the point where the deniers' arrogance is completely shattered. They had plans and schemes in this world; now they have absolutely nothing. As-Sa'di explains that each "woe" is not just a warning but an actual description of what awaits: destruction, ruin, and eternal loss.

Now the contrast: while the deniers are in smoke and fire, the righteous are in real shade and flowing springs. "Al-Muttaqin" are those who were God-conscious, who lived their lives aware of Allah's presence and obeyed Him. "Zilal" is genuine, cool, pleasant shade, unlike the mock "shadow" of smoke described earlier. "Uyun" are flowing springs of water. Ibn Kathir explains this is the reward of Paradise: true comfort, not the false comfort the deniers received in worldly life. As-Sa'di notes the powerful contrast with verses 30-31: the deniers get smoke that provides no shade, while the believers get real shade and fresh water.

Ibn Kathir explains that the fruits of Paradise are unlike anything in this world: they are more delicious, more varied, and available without end. As-Sa'di notes that "mimma yashtahun" (from whatever they desire) means there are no limitations. Whatever your heart desires is immediately available. This is the opposite of worldly life, where desire always exceeds what is available.

Ibn Kathir explains that "bima kuntum ta'malun" (because of what you used to do) means their good deeds in this world earned them this reward. As-Sa'di notes that the word "hani'an" removes all anxiety from the enjoyment. There is no fear of it running out, no health concerns, and no guilt. Pure, unrestricted enjoyment.

As-Sa'di notes that "kadhalika" (thus, in this way) means this is Allah's consistent practice. He always rewards goodness with goodness, but His reward far exceeds what the person deserves. A few decades of good deeds earn an eternity of pleasure.

The eighth repetition. This time the refrain comes after the description of Paradise: shades, springs, fruits, eating and drinking in bliss. The contrast is devastating. The placement after the Paradise description makes the "woe" even more painful: you could have had this, but you chose denial. Ibn Kathir and As-Sa'di both note that describing Paradise right before the refrain adds the pain of loss to the pain of punishment.

Ibn Kathir explains that their worldly enjoyment is temporary and will be followed by eternal punishment. As-Sa'di notes that calling them "mujrimun" (criminals) while they eat and enjoy is a reminder that their comfort is borrowed time before their sentence is carried out.

All of it leads to this one conclusion: woe to those who deny. As-Sa'di explains that the cumulative effect of nine repetitions is meant to be overwhelming, leaving no room in the heart for doubt.

As-Sa'di notes that the ten repetitions form a complete cycle of warning: from cosmic signs to historical precedent, from human creation to earthly blessings, from hellfire descriptions to paradise descriptions, from worldly indulgence to refusal to pray. The surah ends with a haunting, rhetorical question that echoes into eternity: then in what message after the Quran will they believe? "Hadith" here means message, speech, or narration. If the Quran, with all its proofs, warnings, descriptions of Hell and Paradise, stories of destroyed nations, and reminders of creation, cannot make you believe, then what possibly could? Ibn Kathir explains that this verse declares the Quran as the ultimate and final argument. There is no book after it, no messenger after Muhammad (peace be upon him), and no additional proof to be given. Key Lessons
  • 01
    The Day of Judgment is absolutely certain. The surah opens with powerful oaths and ends with a devastating question. Between them, every conceivable proof is presented. The Day of Separation is coming, and nothing in the heavens or earth can prevent it.
  • 02
    Denial has consequences that never change. Allah destroyed previous nations for denial, and He will do the same to later ones. His way (sunnah) does not change. History is full of examples, and the deniers of every era follow the same path to the same end.
  • 03
    Your own creation is proof of resurrection. You were made from a worthless fluid, placed in a womb, and developed into a complex human being. The One who did this the first time can certainly do it again. Denying resurrection while being a living proof of Allah's creative power is deeply irrational.
  • 04
    Every blessing is a proof against the denier. The earth that holds you, the mountains that stabilize it, the water that sustains you: all are gifts from the One you deny. Every sip of water is evidence for the prosecution on the Day of Judgment.
  • 05
    Worldly enjoyment without faith is borrowed time. "Eat and enjoy a little" is not a compliment; it is a warning. The pleasures of this world are brief, and without faith, they are the last good thing you will ever experience before eternal punishment.
  • 06
    The Quran is the final argument. If the Quran cannot convince you, nothing will. This is the most powerful, most eloquent, most comprehensive message ever delivered to humanity. Rejecting it means rejecting all evidence, all logic, and all mercy.
