The Story Behind This Surah

Abu Lahab was the Prophet Muhammad's own uncle. His real name was Abdul-Uzza ibn Abdul-Muttalib. "Abu Lahab" (Father of Flame) was a nickname given because of his fiery red complexion.

When the Prophet (peace be upon him) first publicly called people to Islam from Mount Safa, he called out to each clan by name. When they gathered, he asked: "If I told you there was an army behind this mountain about to attack, would you believe me?" They all said yes. Then he called them to worship Allah alone.

Abu Lahab's response was: "May you perish! Is this what you gathered us for?" He picked up a stone to throw at his own nephew.

Source: Sahih al-Bukhari 4971

Abu Lahab and his wife Umm Jamil (Arwa bint Harb, sister of Abu Sufyan) were among the most vicious enemies of Islam. She would tie bundles of thorny branches and scatter them on the paths the Prophet walked at night, hoping to injure him.

This is the only surah in the Quran where a specific person is condemned by name. It's also a prophecy: Abu Lahab is told he will never accept Islam. And indeed, he died as a disbeliever - proving the Quran's prophecy true. If the Quran were not from Allah, Abu Lahab could have simply said "I believe" to disprove it. He never did.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse broken down word by word.

1
تَبَّتْ يَدَا أَبِي لَهَبٍ وَتَبَّ
Tabbat yada abi lahabin wa tabb
May the hands of Abu Lahab be ruined, and ruined is he.

Word by Word (right to left)

تَبَّتْtabbatperished / ruined
يَدَاyada(the two) hands (of)
أَبِيabifather (of)
لَهَبٍlahabflame
وَتَبَّwa tabband he perished
Simple Explanation

"Tabbat yada" - "may the hands perish." In Arabic, "hands" represents a person's efforts and actions. The first "tabbat" is a supplication (du'a) against him, and the second "tabb" is a statement of fact - he HAS perished. Both his efforts to stop Islam and he himself are doomed. His nickname "Abu Lahab" (Father of Flame) becomes bitterly ironic - the flame he's associated with becomes the fire he'll face.

2
مَا أَغْنَىٰ عَنْهُ مَالُهُ وَمَا كَسَبَ
Ma aghna 'anhu maluhu wa ma kasab
His wealth will not avail him or that which he gained.

Word by Word

مَاmanot / will not
أَغْنَىٰaghnaavail / benefit
عَنْهُ'anhuhim
مَالُهُmaluhuhis wealth
وَمَاwa maand what
كَسَبَkasabhe earned
Simple Explanation

Abu Lahab was a wealthy man. He thought his money and status could protect him and give him power. This verse declares: none of it will help. "Ma kasab" (what he earned) refers to both his material earnings and his children, who he was proud of. On the Day of Judgment, wealth is worthless. Status is meaningless. Only your deeds matter.

3
سَيَصْلَىٰ نَارًا ذَاتَ لَهَبٍ
Sayasla naran dhata lahab
He will burn in a Fire of blazing flame.

Word by Word

سَيَصْلَىٰsayaslahe will burn in
نَارًاnarana fire
ذَاتَdhataof / possessing
لَهَبٍlahabflame
Simple Explanation

The word "lahab" (flame) appears again - the same word in his nickname. Abu Lahab, "the Father of Flame," will enter a fire OF flame. The wordplay is devastating. "Sa" (will) makes this a definite prophecy - he WILL enter the fire. This is also proof the Quran is from Allah: it declared Abu Lahab would never believe, and he never did.

4
وَامْرَأَتُهُ حَمَّالَةَ الْحَطَبِ
Wamra'atuhu hammalatal-hatab
And his wife, the carrier of firewood.

Word by Word

وَامْرَأَتُهُwamra'atuhuand his wife
حَمَّالَةَhammalatacarrier (of)
الْحَطَبِal-hatabthe firewood
Simple Explanation

Umm Jamil, Abu Lahab's wife, was from one of the most noble families of Quraysh. She was wealthy and proud. Yet the Quran calls her "carrier of firewood" - a job done by the lowest classes. Some scholars say this refers to her literally carrying thorny branches to place on the Prophet's path. Others say it refers to her carrying gossip and slander like carrying firewood to fuel a fire of conflict.

5
فِي جِيدِهَا حَبْلٌ مِّن مَّسَدٍ
Fi jidiha hablun min masad
Around her neck is a rope of palm fiber.

Word by Word

فِيfiaround / in
جِيدِهَاjidihaher neck
حَبْلٌhabluna rope
مِّنminof / from
مَّسَدٍmasadpalm fiber
Simple Explanation

The word "jid" is a poetic Arabic word for neck - usually used to describe a beautiful neck adorned with jewelry. But instead of a beautiful necklace, she'll have a rough rope of palm fiber. The contrast is intentional: she was proud of her wealth and beauty, but in the Hereafter, her "necklace" will be a coarse rope. Some scholars say this is the rope she used to carry thorny branches; others say it describes her punishment in the Fire.

Key Lessons from Al-Masad
  • ◆
    Family ties don't guarantee salvation.

    Abu Lahab was the Prophet's own uncle. Lineage means nothing without faith and good deeds.

  • ◆
    Wealth and status cannot save you.

    All of Abu Lahab's money and social standing were worthless before Allah.

  • ◆
    Those who fight truth are ultimately destroyed.

    Abu Lahab died a painful death from a plague. His own family abandoned his body for days.

  • ◆
    The Quran's prophecies are proof of its divine origin.

    This surah predicted Abu Lahab would never believe, and he never did - despite having every reason to disprove it.

  • ◆
    Spouses influence each other's fate.

    Umm Jamil supported her husband in opposing Islam. Partners in sin share the consequences.
