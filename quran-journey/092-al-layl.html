The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Layl was revealed in Makkah and is deeply connected to Surah Ash-Shams (The Sun). While Ash-Shams speaks about purifying the soul, Al-Layl shows us two concrete paths that determine our outcome: the path of giving vs. the path of hoarding.

One of the most famous occasions linked to this surah involves Abu Bakr As-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him). He used to spend his wealth to free enslaved Muslims who were being tortured for their faith. He freed Bilal ibn Rabah, who was being crushed under boulders by his master Umayyah ibn Khalaf for saying "Ahad, Ahad" (One God, One God).

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Whoever spends a pair of something in the cause of Allah, will be called from the gates of Paradise: 'O servant of Allah, this is good!' Whoever is from the people of prayer will be called from the gate of prayer. Whoever is from the people of jihad will be called from the gate of jihad. Whoever is from the people of charity will be called from the gate of charity." Abu Bakr said: "O Messenger of Allah, will anyone be called from all of them?" He said: "Yes, and I hope you will be one of them."

Source: Sahih Bukhari 1897, Sahih Muslim 1027

The surah paints a vivid contrast. It begins with three powerful oaths: the night, the day, and the creation of male and female. Then it divides humanity into two groups: those who give, fear Allah, and believe in goodness (they get the easy path), and those who are stingy, self-sufficient, and deny goodness (they get the hard path). The surah ends with a beautiful promise: the most righteous will be kept far from the Fire and will be fully satisfied.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَاللَّيْلِ إِذَا يَغْشَىٰ
Wal-layli idha yaghsha
By the night when it covers.

Word by Word

وَwaBy (oath)
اللَّيْلِal-layliThe night
إِذَاidhaWhen
يَغْشَىٰyaghshaIt covers / envelops
Simple Explanation

Allah begins with an oath by the night when it covers everything in darkness. The night represents the state of those who cover their hearts with stinginess and denial. Just as the night hides everything, these people hide from the truth and from generosity. But the night is also temporary - it always gives way to the day.

2
وَالنَّهَارِ إِذَا تَجَلَّىٰ
Wan-nahari idha tajalla
And by the day when it appears in brightness.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd (oath)
النَّهَارِan-nahariThe day
إِذَاidhaWhen
تَجَلَّىٰtajallaIt shines / appears bright
Simple Explanation

"Tajalla" means to shine, become clear, and reveal itself. The day represents clarity, generosity, and faith. Just as daylight reveals everything hidden in darkness, faith and generosity illuminate a person's life. The contrast between night and day mirrors the contrast between the two types of people described later in this surah.

3
وَمَا خَلَقَ الذَّكَرَ وَالْأُنثَىٰ
Wa ma khalaqadh-dhakara wal-untha
And [by] He who created the male and female.

Word by Word

وَمَاwa maAnd He who
خَلَقَkhalaqaCreated
الذَّكَرَadh-dhakaraThe male
وَالْأُنثَىٰwal-unthaAnd the female
Simple Explanation

The third oath is by Allah who created male and female. Allah's creation is built on complementary pairs - night and day, male and female. This reinforces the surah's main theme: there are two types of people, two paths, two outcomes. The diversity in creation is by Allah's design, and each person chooses which path to walk.

4
إِنَّ سَعْيَكُمْ لَشَتَّىٰ
Inna sa'yakum lashatta
Indeed, your efforts are diverse.

Word by Word

إِنَّinnaIndeed / truly
سَعْيَكُمْsa'yakumYour efforts / striving
لَشَتَّىٰlashattaAre truly diverse / different
Simple Explanation

This is the answer to the three oaths. After swearing by the night, day, and the Creator, Allah states the core message: people's efforts and paths in life are vastly different. Some strive for Allah's pleasure, others strive only for worldly gain. Some give freely, others hoard. The same 24 hours, but completely different choices and outcomes.

5
فَأَمَّا مَنْ أَعْطَىٰ وَاتَّقَىٰ
Fa'amma man a'ta wattaqa
As for he who gives and fears Allah.

Word by Word

فَأَمَّاfa'ammaSo as for
مَنْmanThe one who
أَعْطَىٰa'taGives
وَاتَّقَىٰwattaqaAnd fears (Allah)
Simple Explanation

Now begins the first profile. "A'ta" (gives) is mentioned without specifying WHAT to give - meaning give everything: money, time, effort, kindness, knowledge. "Ittaqa" (has taqwa) means being conscious of Allah in every action. The generous, God-conscious person is the first type. Giving and taqwa go hand in hand because true generosity comes from trusting Allah.

6
وَصَدَّقَ بِالْحُسْنَىٰ
Wa saddaqa bil-husna
And believes in the best [reward].

Word by Word

وَصَدَّقَwa saddaqaAnd believes / confirms
بِالْحُسْنَىٰbil-husnaIn the best (reward)
Simple Explanation

"Al-Husna" refers to the best outcome - Paradise, Allah's reward, and the promise that goodness always returns. Ibn Kathir explains this as believing in La ilaha illAllah and all it entails. When you truly believe that Allah's reward is real and better than anything this world offers, giving becomes easy. You're not losing wealth - you're investing it with the best return.

7
فَسَنُيَسِّرُهُ لِلْيُسْرَىٰ
Fasanuyassiruhu lil-yusra
We will ease him toward ease.

Word by Word

فَسَنُيَسِّرُهُfasanuyassiruhuThen We will ease him
لِلْيُسْرَىٰlil-yusraToward ease
Simple Explanation

This is a beautiful promise: when you give, fear Allah, and believe in His reward, Allah makes your entire life easier. Not just one thing - your whole path becomes smoother. The more you give, the easier it becomes to give. The more taqwa you have, the easier obedience becomes. Good deeds open doors to more good deeds. It's a positive spiral that Allah facilitates for those who take the first step.

8
وَأَمَّا مَن بَخِلَ وَاسْتَغْنَىٰ
Wa amma man bakhila wastaghna
But as for he who withholds and considers himself free of need.

Word by Word

وَأَمَّاwa ammaBut as for
مَنmanThe one who
بَخِلَbakhilaIs stingy / withholds
وَاسْتَغْنَىٰwastaghnaAnd considers himself self-sufficient
Simple Explanation

Now the opposite type. "Bakhila" is stinginess - withholding what should be given. "Istaghna" is the dangerous belief that you don't need Allah. This combination is toxic: I won't give because I earned it myself, and I don't need anyone's help. This arrogance blinds a person from seeing that everything they have is from Allah in the first place.

9
وَكَذَّبَ بِالْحُسْنَىٰ
Wa kadhdhaba bil-husna
And denies the best [reward].

Word by Word

وَكَذَّبَwa kadhdhabaAnd denies / rejects
بِالْحُسْنَىٰbil-husnaThe best (reward)
Simple Explanation

The same "Al-Husna" from verse 6, but this person rejects it. They deny that there is a Day of Judgment, a Paradise, or any divine reward. When you don't believe in the Hereafter, why would you sacrifice anything in this world? This denial is the root cause of their stinginess - if this life is all there is, holding on to every penny seems logical. But it's built on a false premise.

10
فَسَنُيَسِّرُهُ لِلْعُسْرَىٰ
Fasanuyassiruhu lil-'usra
We will ease him toward difficulty.

Word by Word

فَسَنُيَسِّرُهُfasanuyassiruhuThen We will ease him
لِلْعُسْرَىٰlil-'usraToward difficulty
Simple Explanation

Notice the powerful wordplay: "nuyassiruhu" (We will make easy) paired with "al-'usra" (difficulty). Allah will make it EASY for this person to fall into DIFFICULTY. Sin becomes easy, evil becomes normalized, bad habits become automatic. Just as good deeds lead to more good deeds, bad deeds create a downward spiral. The stingy person finds it increasingly hard to do good and increasingly easy to do wrong.

11
وَمَا يُغْنِي عَنْهُ مَالُهُ إِذَا تَرَدَّىٰ
Wa ma yughni 'anhu maluhu idha taradda
And what will his wealth avail him when he falls?

Word by Word

وَمَاwa maAnd what / not
يُغْنِيyughniWill avail / benefit
عَنْهُ'anhuHim
مَالُهُmaluhuHis wealth
إِذَاidhaWhen
تَرَدَّىٰtaraddaHe falls / perishes
Simple Explanation

A devastating rhetorical question. "Taradda" means to fall into ruin - falling into the grave or falling into Hell. When that moment comes, all the wealth that was hoarded so carefully becomes completely useless. You can't bribe death. You can't buy your way out of the grave. The very thing you sacrificed your soul to protect cannot protect you when it matters most.

12
إِنَّ عَلَيْنَا لَلْهُدَىٰ
Inna 'alayna lal-huda
Indeed, [incumbent] upon Us is guidance.

Word by Word

إِنَّinnaIndeed
عَلَيْنَا'alaynaUpon Us
لَلْهُدَىٰlal-hudaSurely is the guidance
Simple Explanation

Allah takes upon Himself the responsibility of providing guidance. He sent prophets, revealed books, and gave every human a fitrah (natural disposition toward truth). No one can say "I didn't know" because Allah made sure the path was clear. The guidance is available - the question is whether you choose to follow it.

13
وَإِنَّ لَنَا لَلْآخِرَةَ وَالْأُولَىٰ
Wa inna lana lal-akhirata wal-ula
And indeed, to Us belongs the Hereafter and the first [life].

Word by Word

وَإِنَّwa innaAnd indeed
لَنَاlanaTo Us belongs
لَلْآخِرَةَlal-akhirataThe Hereafter
وَالْأُولَىٰwal-ulaAnd the first (this life)
Simple Explanation

Both worlds belong to Allah. He is the King of this life and the next. So when the stingy person hoards wealth thinking it's "theirs," they're holding onto something that was never really theirs. And when the generous person gives, they're simply returning to Allah what was always His. This verse puts everything in perspective.

14
فَأَنذَرْتُكُمْ نَارًا تَلَظَّىٰ
Fa'andhartukum naran talazza
So I have warned you of a Fire that blazes.

Word by Word

فَأَنذَرْتُكُمْfa'andhartukumSo I have warned you
نَارًاnaranA Fire
تَلَظَّىٰtalazzaThat blazes fiercely
Simple Explanation

"Talazza" means to blaze intensely, with flames that rage and roar. This is a direct, clear warning. Allah doesn't hide what's at stake. The warning is an act of mercy - like a sign saying "DANGER: CLIFF AHEAD." The person who ignores it has no excuse. Allah warned clearly, through His Book, His messengers, and through this very surah.

15
لَا يَصْلَاهَا إِلَّا الْأَشْقَى
La yaslaha illa al-ashqa
None will [enter to] burn therein except the most wretched.

Word by Word

لَاlaNot / none
يَصْلَاهَاyaslahaWill burn in it
إِلَّاillaExcept
الْأَشْقَىal-ashqaThe most wretched
Simple Explanation

"Al-Ashqa" is the superlative form - the MOST wretched, the most miserable. This isn't just someone who makes mistakes; it's someone who persistently denies truth and turns away from guidance despite having every opportunity to accept it. The next verse describes exactly who this wretched person is.

16
الَّذِي كَذَّبَ وَتَوَلَّىٰ
Alladhi kadhdhaba wa tawalla
The one who denied and turned away.

Word by Word

الَّذِيalladhiThe one who
كَذَّبَkadhdhabaDenied / rejected
وَتَوَلَّىٰwa tawallaAnd turned away
Simple Explanation

Two actions define the most wretched: "kadhdhaba" (denied the truth) and "tawalla" (turned their back on it). It's not just intellectual rejection - it's active avoidance. They heard the message, understood it could be true, but chose to walk away. Denial plus turning away equals the worst outcome.

17
وَسَيُجَنَّبُهَا الْأَتْقَى
Wa sayujannabaha al-atqa
But the most righteous one will be kept far from it.

Word by Word

وَسَيُجَنَّبُهَاwa sayujannabahaAnd will be kept far from it
الْأَتْقَىal-atqaThe most righteous / God-fearing
Simple Explanation

Beautiful contrast: "Al-Ashqa" (most wretched) enters the Fire, while "Al-Atqa" (most righteous) is kept FAR from it. "Yujannab" means not just saved from it, but distanced from it entirely. Many scholars say this verse was revealed about Abu Bakr As-Siddiq, the most righteous of the Prophet's companions, who spent his wealth freeing enslaved Muslims.

18
الَّذِي يُؤْتِي مَالَهُ يَتَزَكَّىٰ
Alladhi yu'ti malahu yatazakka
The one who gives [from] his wealth to purify himself.

Word by Word

الَّذِيalladhiThe one who
يُؤْتِيyu'tiGives
مَالَهُmalahuHis wealth
يَتَزَكَّىٰyatazakkaTo purify himself
Simple Explanation

"Yatazakka" comes from the same root as "zakah" - purification. Giving charity doesn't decrease your wealth; it purifies it and purifies your soul. The righteous person doesn't give to show off or to get something in return. They give because they understand that wealth is a test, and sharing it is how you pass that test.

19
وَمَا لِأَحَدٍ عِندَهُ مِن نِّعْمَةٍ تُجْزَىٰ
Wa ma li'ahadin 'indahu min ni'matin tujza
And not to repay any favor to anyone.

Word by Word

وَمَاwa maAnd not
لِأَحَدٍli'ahadinFor anyone
عِندَهُ'indahuWith him
مِن نِّعْمَةٍmin ni'matinAny favor
تُجْزَىٰtujzaTo be repaid
Simple Explanation

This righteous person isn't giving to return a favor or to create an obligation. No one did them a favor that they're paying back. Their giving is purely selfless, purely for Allah. This is the highest form of charity: when there's absolutely no worldly motive. Abu Bakr freed Bilal not because Bilal could pay him back, but purely because it was the right thing to do before Allah.

20
إِلَّا ابْتِغَاءَ وَجْهِ رَبِّهِ الْأَعْلَىٰ
Illa btighaa'a wajhi rabbihil-a'la
But only seeking the Face of his Lord, Most High.

Word by Word

إِلَّاillaExcept / only
ابْتِغَاءَibtighaa'aSeeking
وَجْهِwajhiThe Face of
رَبِّهِrabbihiHis Lord
الْأَعْلَىٰal-a'laThe Most High
Simple Explanation

The only motivation: seeking the pleasure of Allah, the Most High. "Wajh" (Face) represents the essence and pleasure of Allah. "Al-A'la" (The Most High) reminds us of Allah's supreme greatness. When your only goal is Allah's pleasure, every act of giving becomes an act of worship. This is the secret of ikhlas (sincerity) - doing everything purely for Allah, expecting nothing from anyone else.

21
وَلَسَوْفَ يَرْضَىٰ
Wa lasawfa yarda
And he is going to be satisfied.

Word by Word

وَلَسَوْفَwa lasawfaAnd surely will
يَرْضَىٰyardaBe satisfied / pleased
Simple Explanation

The surah ends with a powerful, definitive promise. "Lasawfa" - it WILL certainly happen. "Yarda" - complete, total satisfaction. After all the sacrifice, all the giving, all the selfless devotion to Allah, this person WILL be pleased with what Allah gives them. Just like the ending of Surah Ad-Duha ("fatarda"), Allah guarantees satisfaction. When you give for Allah, you never lose - you always end up more than satisfied.

Key Lessons from Surah Al-Layl
  • ◆
    Your choices define your path, not your circumstances.

    Everyone faces the same choice: give or hoard, believe or deny. The night and day come for everyone equally. What matters is what YOU do with what you have.

  • ◆
    Giving creates ease, hoarding creates hardship.

    Allah makes the path of good deeds easier for those who give, and makes the path of destruction easier for those who hoard. Small choices compound into life trajectories.

  • ◆
    Wealth cannot save you when it matters most.

    When death comes, all the money in the world is worthless. The only currency that counts is what you sent ahead through good deeds and charity.

  • ◆
    The highest generosity has no strings attached.

    The most righteous person gives purely for Allah's sake, not to repay favors or gain recognition. True sincerity (ikhlas) is doing good with no worldly motive.

  • ◆
    Allah guarantees satisfaction for those who seek His Face.

    The surah ends with a divine promise: those who give selflessly for Allah will be FULLY satisfied. You never lose when you invest with Allah.
