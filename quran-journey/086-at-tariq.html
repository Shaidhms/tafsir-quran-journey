The Story Behind This Surah

Surah At-Tariq was revealed in Makkah during the early period of the Prophet's mission, when the Quraysh were deeply resistant to the message of resurrection and accountability. They mocked the idea that dead bones could be brought back to life and dismissed the Quran as poetry or sorcery.

The name "At-Tariq" means "the nightcomer" or "the knocker at the door." It refers to a bright star that appears at night, piercing through the darkness. The Arabs called any star that appeared at night a "tariq" because it comes (knocks) at night. Allah then explains it as "the piercing star" (an-najm ath-thaqib) - a star whose light pierces through the darkness of the night sky.

Ibn Kathir narrates that Abu Talib was once sitting with the Prophet (peace be upon him) when a star shot across the sky. The Prophet asked, "What do you say about this?" Abu Talib replied, "We say a great man has been born or a great man has died." The Prophet said, "It is not thrown for the death or birth of anyone, but rather when Allah decrees a matter..." This context shows how the Arabs were deeply connected to the night sky, making Allah's oath by the piercing star especially powerful.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Surah At-Tariq

The surah follows a powerful logical structure. First, Allah swears by the piercing star and declares that every soul has a guardian over it (verses 1-4). Then He turns to human origins - you were created from a fluid, so surely the One who created you can bring you back (verses 5-8). On the Day when all secrets are exposed, man will have no power or helper (verses 9-10).

Finally, Allah swears by the sky that returns rain and the earth that splits open with growth - both signs of resurrection in nature itself. The Quran is declared "a decisive word" - not amusement - and Allah warns the plotters that He too is planning, and His plan will prevail (verses 11-17). The surah ends with a firm command: give the disbelievers respite, a little while.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَالسَّمَاءِ وَالطَّارِقِ
Was-sama'i wat-tariq
By the sky and the night comer.

Word by Word

وَwaBy (oath)
السَّمَاءِas-sama'iThe sky
وَwaAnd (oath)
الطَّارِقِat-tariqThe night comer
Simple Explanation

Allah opens this surah with a powerful oath, swearing by the vast sky and by "at-tariq" - the nightcomer. The word "tariq" comes from "tarq" meaning to knock or strike. The Arabs called any star visible at night a "tariq" because it arrives (knocks) during the night, like a visitor at your door. Allah swears by this celestial phenomenon to grab our attention and prepare us for a profound truth about to be revealed.

2
وَمَا أَدْرَاكَ مَا الطَّارِقُ
Wa ma adraka mat-tariq
And what can make you know what is the night comer?

Word by Word

وَمَاwa maAnd what
أَدْرَاكَadrakaCan make you know
مَاmaWhat
الطَّارِقُat-tariquThe night comer
Simple Explanation

This rhetorical question is a classic Quranic style used to build anticipation and emphasize the greatness of what is being described. "Wa ma adraka" - "And what could possibly make you know?" This phrase appears in the Quran whenever Allah wants to elevate the status of something beyond human comprehension. It creates suspense, making the listener lean in for the answer that follows in the next verse.

3
النَّجْمُ الثَّاقِبُ
An-najmu ath-thaqib
It is the piercing star.

Word by Word

النَّجْمُan-najmuThe star
الثَّاقِبُath-thaqibThe piercing / penetrating
Simple Explanation

The answer: it is "an-najm ath-thaqib" - the piercing star. The word "thaqib" means something that pierces through, penetrates, or burns brightly. Ibn Kathir explains it as a star so bright that its light pierces through the layers of darkness. Some scholars say it refers to any bright star, others say it refers to shooting stars (meteors) that pierce the night sky. The imagery is stunning - even in total darkness, there is a light that breaks through. Nothing can hide from Allah's awareness.

4
إِن كُلُّ نَفْسٍ لَّمَّا عَلَيْهَا حَافِظٌ
In kullu nafsin lamma 'alayha hafiz
There is no soul but that it has over it a protector.

Word by Word

إِنinNot / Indeed
كُلُّkulluEvery
نَفْسٍnafsinSoul
لَّمَّاlammaSurely / except
عَلَيْهَا'alayhaOver it
حَافِظٌhafizA guardian / protector
Simple Explanation

This is the answer to the oath - the main point Allah is making. Just as the piercing star watches over the night sky, every single soul has a "hafiz" (guardian/watcher) over it. Ibn Kathir explains this refers to the angels who record every deed. As-Sa'di adds that this means Allah Himself is watching over every soul, and the angels are recording everything - good and bad. Nothing goes unnoticed. You are never truly alone, and you are never truly unaccounted for.

5
فَلْيَنظُرِ الْإِنسَانُ مِمَّ خُلِقَ
Fal-yanzuril-insanu mimma khuliq
So let man observe from what he was created.

Word by Word

فَلْيَنظُرِfal-yanzuriSo let him observe
الْإِنسَانُal-insanuThe human being
مِمَّmimmaFrom what
خُلِقَkhuliqaHe was created
Simple Explanation

Now Allah shifts to the evidence for resurrection. He commands the human being to look at his own origin. "Fal-yanzur" - let him look, reflect, think deeply. This is an invitation to intellectual humility. Before you deny resurrection, first consider where you came from. How were you created in the first place? If you understand your humble beginning, you will understand that the One who started your creation can easily repeat it.

6
خُلِقَ مِن مَّاءٍ دَافِقٍ
Khuliqa min ma'in dafiq
He was created from a fluid, ejected.

Word by Word

خُلِقَkhuliqaHe was created
مِنminFrom
مَّاءٍma'inA fluid / water
دَافِقٍdafiqEjected / gushing forth
Simple Explanation

The answer: you were created from "ma'in dafiq" - a gushing fluid. This refers to the reproductive fluid from which human life begins. The point is deeply humbling. You, with all your pride and arrogance, started as a tiny, insignificant drop of fluid. You had no say in your own creation, no control over your own beginning. The One who turned that drop into a thinking, walking, talking human being - can He not bring you back after death?

7
يَخْرُجُ مِن بَيْنِ الصُّلْبِ وَالتَّرَائِبِ
Yakhruju min baynis-sulbi wat-tara'ib
Emerging from between the backbone and the ribs.

Word by Word

يَخْرُجُyakhrujuIt emerges / comes out
مِن بَيْنِmin bayniFrom between
الصُّلْبِas-sulbiThe backbone
وَwaAnd
التَّرَائِبِat-tara'ibThe ribs / chest bones
Simple Explanation

This verse describes the origin point of this fluid. "As-sulb" refers to the backbone and "at-tara'ib" refers to the ribs or chest area. Classical scholars like Ibn Kathir explain that this refers to the area between the backbone and the chest from which the reproductive fluid originates. The description highlights Allah's intimate knowledge of human biology. He knows exactly how He created you, down to the finest detail, and this knowledge is evidence of His absolute power to recreate you.

8
إِنَّهُ عَلَىٰ رَجْعِهِ لَقَادِرٌ
Innahu 'ala raj'ihi laqadir
Indeed, He [i.e., Allah], to return him [to life], is Able.

Word by Word

إِنَّهُinnahuIndeed He
عَلَىٰ'alaUpon / to
رَجْعِهِraj'ihiHis return / bringing him back
لَقَادِرٌlaqadirCertainly Able
Simple Explanation

This is the conclusion of the argument. The One who created you from a drop of fluid - from nothing into a complex, living being - is CERTAINLY able to bring you back after death. "Laqadir" - the "la" at the beginning is for emphasis: He is ABSOLUTELY, UNDENIABLY Able. As-Sa'di explains that this is the most obvious logical argument. The first creation was harder (from our perspective), yet Allah did it effortlessly. So how could the second creation (resurrection) be difficult for Him?

9
يَوْمَ تُبْلَى السَّرَائِرُ
Yawma tublas-sara'ir
The Day when secrets will be put on trial.

Word by Word

يَوْمَyawmaThe Day
تُبْلَىtublaWill be tested / exposed
السَّرَائِرُas-sara'irThe secrets / hidden things
Simple Explanation

On the Day of Judgment, "as-sara'ir" - all the hidden things - will be exposed and tested. This includes hidden intentions, secret sins, private thoughts, concealed beliefs, and everything you kept hidden from people. Ibn Kathir explains this means all that was concealed in the hearts will be laid open and examined. Nothing will remain hidden. In this world, people can fake piety, hide corruption, and wear masks. On that Day, every mask falls. Every secret is revealed. This is why sincerity matters more than appearance.

10
فَمَا لَهُ مِن قُوَّةٍ وَلَا نَاصِرٍ
Fama lahu min quwwatin wa la nasir
Then he will have no power or any helper.

Word by Word

فَمَاfamaThen not
لَهُlahuFor him
مِنminAny
قُوَّةٍquwwatinPower / strength
وَلَاwa laAnd no
نَاصِرٍnasirHelper
Simple Explanation

On that Day, man will be completely powerless. He will have no "quwwa" (personal strength to defend himself) and no "nasir" (helper or ally to rescue him). In this world, people rely on wealth, connections, status, and power. All of that will be stripped away. As-Sa'di explains that this verse cuts off all hope from anything other than Allah. Neither your own abilities nor anyone else's support will benefit you. Only your deeds and Allah's mercy will matter.

11
وَالسَّمَاءِ ذَاتِ الرَّجْعِ
Was-sama'i dhatir-raj'
By the sky which returns [rain].

Word by Word

وَwaBy (oath)
السَّمَاءِas-sama'iThe sky
ذَاتِdhatiPossessor of
الرَّجْعِar-raj'The return (rain)
Simple Explanation

A new oath begins. Allah swears by the sky "dhat ar-raj'" - the sky that returns. Ibn Kathir and As-Sa'di explain that "raj'" here refers to rain, which the sky returns again and again, season after season. Water evaporates, rises to the sky, forms clouds, and returns as rain in a continuous cycle. This cycle of return is itself a sign of resurrection - just as rain returns to bring dead earth back to life, Allah will return the dead to life on the Day of Judgment.

12
وَالْأَرْضِ ذَاتِ الصَّدْعِ
Wal-ardi dhatis-sad'
And [by] the earth which cracks open.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd (oath)
الْأَرْضِal-ardiThe earth
ذَاتِdhatiPossessor of
الصَّدْعِas-sad'The splitting / cracking open
Simple Explanation

And the earth "dhat as-sad'" - the earth that splits open. When rain falls on dry, cracked earth, plants push through the soil, splitting it open with new life. Seeds that were buried, seemingly dead, suddenly burst forth. Ibn Kathir explains this as the earth cracking open for vegetation and plants to grow. This is a visible, tangible analogy for resurrection - dead seeds buried in dead earth, brought to life by water from the sky. You witness "resurrection" every spring, yet you deny the final one?

13
إِنَّهُ لَقَوْلٌ فَصْلٌ
Innahu laqawlun fasl
Indeed, it [i.e., the Quran] is a decisive statement.

Word by Word

إِنَّهُinnahuIndeed it
لَقَوْلٌlaqawlunSurely a word / statement
فَصْلٌfaslDecisive / separating
Simple Explanation

After the two oaths (by the returning sky and the splitting earth), this is the answer: the Quran is "qawlun fasl" - a decisive, definitive word. "Fasl" means that which separates truth from falsehood, right from wrong. It is the final word on every matter. Ibn Kathir says it means the Quran is truthful, serious, and just - it separates between truth and falsehood like a sharp blade. There is no ambiguity in its message.

14
وَمَا هُوَ بِالْهَزْلِ
Wa ma huwa bil-hazl
And it is not amusement.

Word by Word

وَمَاwa maAnd not
هُوَhuwaIt
بِالْهَزْلِbil-hazlAmusement / jest
Simple Explanation

And it is NOT "hazl" - not a joke, not entertainment, not something to be taken lightly. "Hazl" means jest, play, amusement - the opposite of serious speech. The Quraysh treated the Quran as poetry, as stories, as entertainment to dismiss. Allah firmly corrects this: every word of the Quran is serious, purposeful, and true. As-Sa'di explains that this means the Quran was revealed in absolute truth and seriousness, and whoever treats it casually has gravely misjudged its nature.

15
إِنَّهُمْ يَكِيدُونَ كَيْدًا
Innahum yakiduna kayda
Indeed, they are planning a plan.

Word by Word

إِنَّهُمْinnahumIndeed they
يَكِيدُونَyakidunaAre planning
كَيْدًاkaydaA plan / scheme
Simple Explanation

The disbelievers of Quraysh were constantly plotting against the Prophet and Islam. They schemed to discredit him, harm his followers, and extinguish the message. "Yakiduna kayda" - they are plotting their plots. The repetition of the root "k-y-d" (kayd - plotting) emphasizes the intensity and persistence of their scheming. But notice the calm, almost dismissive tone. Allah acknowledges their plots not with alarm, but as a prelude to showing how insignificant human scheming is compared to His plan.

16
وَأَكِيدُ كَيْدًا
Wa akidu kayda
But I am planning a plan.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd
أَكِيدُakiduI am planning
كَيْدًاkaydaA plan
Simple Explanation

And I (Allah) am also planning. This is one of the most powerful, chilling verses in the Quran. They plot, and Allah plots. But there is no comparison between the two. Their "kayd" is weak, limited, temporary. Allah's "kayd" is all-encompassing, unstoppable, and perfectly timed. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah's "plan" refers to His gradual, step-by-step response. He gives the plotters time, lets them think they are succeeding, and then His plan unfolds and theirs collapses entirely. History proved this - every scheme against Islam was eventually dismantled.

17
فَمَهِّلِ الْكَافِرِينَ أَمْهِلْهُمْ رُوَيْدًا
Famahhilil-kafirina amhilhum ruwayda
So allow time for the disbelievers. Leave them awhile.

Word by Word

فَمَهِّلِfamahhilSo give respite to
الْكَافِرِينَal-kafirinaThe disbelievers
أَمْهِلْهُمْamhilhumGrant them respite
رُوَيْدًاruwaydaA little while / gently
Simple Explanation

The surah ends with a command to the Prophet: be patient with them. Give them a little time. "Ruwayda" is the diminutive form of "rawda" (a while), meaning "just a little while." The use of the diminutive makes the threat even more powerful - it's not a long wait, it's a SHORT wait. Their time is almost up. As-Sa'di says this verse is both a consolation to the Prophet and a severe warning to the disbelievers. Don't rush for their punishment - it's already on its way. Allah's plan unfolds at the perfect time, and no one can delay it or escape it.

Key Lessons from Surah At-Tariq
  • ◆
    Every soul is being watched and recorded.

    Just as the piercing star watches over the night sky, Allah has appointed guardians over every soul. Nothing you do, say, or think goes unrecorded. This should inspire both awe and accountability in how we live our lives.

  • ◆
    Your humble origin is proof of resurrection.

    You were created from a drop of fluid. The One who made you into a complex, intelligent being from something so insignificant can certainly bring you back after death. Denying resurrection while acknowledging your own creation is a logical contradiction.

  • ◆
    On the Day of Judgment, all secrets will be exposed.

    The things we hide from people - our real intentions, our private sins, our secret thoughts - will all be laid bare. This makes sincerity ("ikhlas") more important than appearance. What matters is not what people see, but what Allah sees.

  • ◆
    Nature itself testifies to resurrection.

    The sky returns rain season after season. The earth cracks open and dead seeds come to life. These natural cycles are visible, tangible reminders that bringing the dead back to life is not just possible, but part of how Allah designed the universe.

  • ◆
    The Quran is decisive speech, not entertainment.

    It separates truth from falsehood with absolute clarity. Those who treat it casually or dismiss it as mere literature have profoundly misjudged its nature. Every word carries weight and purpose.

  • ◆
    Human plotting cannot match Allah's plan.

    No matter how elaborate or powerful the schemes of those who oppose truth, Allah's plan will always prevail. History has proven this repeatedly. The believers should remain patient and trust in Allah's timing.
