The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Ad-Duha was revealed during one of the most emotionally difficult moments in the Prophet's early mission. The revelation had paused for a period (scholars differ: some say 12 days, others up to 40 days). During this silence, the Prophet (peace be upon him) was deeply worried that Allah had abandoned him.

To make matters worse, a woman from the Quraysh (reported to be Umm Jamil, the wife of Abu Lahab) mocked him saying: "It seems your Lord has forsaken you and is displeased with you!" This hurt the Prophet deeply.

Jundub ibn Sufyan reported: "The Prophet (peace be upon him) became ill and did not pray at night (tahajjud) for two or three nights. Then a woman came and said, 'O Muhammad, I think your devil has forsaken you.' Then Allah revealed: 'By the morning brightness, and by the night when it covers with darkness, your Lord has not taken leave of you, nor has He detested you.'"

Source: Sahih Bukhari 1124, Sahih Muslim 1797

Then this beautiful surah descended like a warm embrace. Allah swears by the morning brightness and the still night to reassure His beloved Prophet. The structure is powerful: first, reassurance (verses 1-5), then three reminders of past favors (verses 6-8), and finally three commands in response (verses 9-11).

The three past favors mirror the three commands: You were an orphan, so don't oppress orphans. You were lost, so don't turn away those seeking guidance. You were poor, so proclaim Allah's blessings. This surah teaches that our past struggles should make us more compassionate, not less.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
وَالضُّحَىٰ
Wad-duha
By the morning brightness.

Word by Word

وَwaBy (oath)
الضُّحَىٰad-duhaThe morning brightness
Simple Explanation

Allah begins by swearing an oath by the "duha" - the bright morning light when the sun has fully risen and the day is radiant. This is a time of warmth, clarity, and hope. By choosing this specific time, Allah is telling the Prophet: just as the morning always comes after the dark night, relief will always follow difficulty. The brightness will return.

2
وَاللَّيْلِ إِذَا سَجَىٰ
Wal-layli idha saja
And [by] the night when it covers with darkness.

Word by Word

وَwaAnd (oath)
اللَّيْلِal-layliThe night
إِذَاidhaWhen
سَجَىٰsajaIt covers / becomes still
Simple Explanation

The word "saja" means when the night becomes still, calm, and covers everything in darkness. It's not a scary darkness - it's peaceful. Allah swears by both the bright morning AND the still night because both are His creation, both serve a purpose. The night of difficulty is not punishment - it's part of the cycle. Rest comes in darkness, and action comes in light.

3
مَا وَدَّعَكَ رَبُّكَ وَمَا قَلَىٰ
Ma wadda'aka rabbuka wa ma qala
Your Lord has not taken leave of you, nor has He detested [you].

Word by Word

مَاmaNot
وَدَّعَكَwadda'akaHas He abandoned you
رَبُّكَrabbukaYour Lord
وَمَاwa maAnd not
قَلَىٰqalaHas He detested / hated
Simple Explanation

This is the direct answer to the mockers. "Ma wadda'aka" - He has NOT abandoned you. "Wa ma qala" - and He does NOT hate you. Notice "Rabbuka" (YOUR Lord) - the personal, intimate form. Allah uses the word "Rabb" (the one who nurtures, cares for, and raises you) to emphasize closeness. When the world tells you God has forgotten you, this verse says: He never left.

4
وَلَلْآخِرَةُ خَيْرٌ لَّكَ مِنَ الْأُولَىٰ
Wa lal-akhiratu khayrun laka minal-ula
And the Hereafter is better for you than the first [life].

Word by Word

وَلَwa laAnd surely
الْآخِرَةُal-akhiratuThe Hereafter
خَيْرٌkhayrunBetter
لَّكَlakaFor you
مِنَminaThan
الْأُولَىٰal-ulaThe first (this life)
Simple Explanation

Every stage that comes next will be better than what came before. This applies both to the Prophet's mission (which grew from difficulty to triumph) and to the ultimate comparison: the Hereafter vs this world. No matter how hard this life gets, what Allah has prepared for you next is incomparably better. This is a promise that should shift our perspective entirely.

5
وَلَسَوْفَ يُعْطِيكَ رَبُّكَ فَتَرْضَىٰ
Wa lasawfa yu'tika rabbuka fatarda
And your Lord is going to give you, and you will be satisfied.

Word by Word

وَلَسَوْفَwa lasawfaAnd surely will
يُعْطِيكَyu'tikaHe give you
رَبُّكَrabbukaYour Lord
فَتَرْضَىٰfatardaAnd you will be satisfied
Simple Explanation

This is one of the most hopeful verses in the Quran. "Lasawfa" - Allah will CERTAINLY give you. Not maybe, not perhaps - certainly. And not just give you a little - He will give you until you are FULLY satisfied ("fatarda"). The Prophet (peace be upon him) said about this verse: "Then I will not be satisfied while any of my Ummah remains in the Fire." This shows the depth of his concern for us.

6
أَلَمْ يَجِدْكَ يَتِيمًا فَآوَىٰ
Alam yajidka yatiman fa'awa
Did He not find you an orphan and give [you] refuge?

Word by Word

أَلَمْalamDid not
يَجِدْكَyajidkaHe find you
يَتِيمًاyatimanAn orphan
فَآوَىٰfa'awaAnd gave refuge
Simple Explanation

The Prophet's father died before he was born, and his mother passed away when he was just six years old. He was a complete orphan in a society where orphans had no rights or protection. But Allah arranged for his grandfather Abdul Muttalib, and then his uncle Abu Talib, to take care of him. Allah didn't leave him alone - He provided shelter and love through these family members. This is the first of three reminders of Allah's past care.

7
وَوَجَدَكَ ضَالًّا فَهَدَىٰ
Wa wajadaka dallan fahada
And He found you lost and guided [you].

Word by Word

وَوَجَدَكَwa wajadakaAnd He found you
ضَالًّاdallanLost / unaware
فَهَدَىٰfahadaAnd He guided
Simple Explanation

The word "dall" here doesn't mean the Prophet was astray in sin. Scholars like As-Sa'di explain it means he was unaware of the specific details of revelation and religious law before prophethood. He was searching for truth in a society of ignorance. Allah then guided him with the Quran and prophethood - giving him the clearest path anyone has ever walked. If Allah can guide you from total confusion to absolute clarity, why would He ever abandon you?

8
وَوَجَدَكَ عَائِلًا فَأَغْنَىٰ
Wa wajadaka 'a'ilan fa'aghna
And He found you poor and made [you] self-sufficient.

Word by Word

وَوَجَدَكَwa wajadakaAnd He found you
عَائِلًا'a'ilanPoor / in need
فَأَغْنَىٰfa'aghnaAnd made self-sufficient
Simple Explanation

The Prophet grew up without wealth. But Allah enriched him - first through his marriage to Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), who was a successful businesswoman, and later through the contentment and satisfaction in his heart. True "ghina" (richness) in Islam is richness of the soul, not just the wallet. The Prophet himself said: "Richness is not having many possessions, but richness is the richness of the soul." (Sahih Bukhari 6446)

9
فَأَمَّا الْيَتِيمَ فَلَا تَقْهَرْ
Fa'ammal yatima fala taqhar
So as for the orphan, do not oppress [him].

Word by Word

فَأَمَّاfa'ammaSo as for
الْيَتِيمَal-yatimaThe orphan
فَلَاfalaThen do not
تَقْهَرْtaqharOppress / dominate
Simple Explanation

Because YOU were an orphan and Allah sheltered you, now don't oppress orphans. The word "taqhar" means to dominate, crush, or treat harshly. This connects directly to verse 6: your own experience of vulnerability should make you MORE compassionate to those who are vulnerable now. The Prophet was the most caring person toward orphans - he said: "I and the caretaker of an orphan will be like these two in Paradise" and he held up his index and middle fingers together. (Sahih Bukhari 5304)

10
وَأَمَّا السَّائِلَ فَلَا تَنْهَرْ
Wa ammas-sa'ila fala tanhar
And as for the petitioner, do not repel [him].

Word by Word

وَأَمَّاwa ammaAnd as for
السَّائِلَas-sa'ilaThe one who asks
فَلَاfalaThen do not
تَنْهَرْtanharRepel / scold harshly
Simple Explanation

"Sa'il" can mean someone asking for money, food, or help - but it also means someone asking for knowledge and guidance. "Tanhar" means to scold harshly, speak rudely, or turn someone away with anger. Whether someone comes to you for material help or for knowledge, respond with kindness. Even if you can't help, a gentle word costs nothing. Because you yourself were once in need, and Allah responded to your need with grace.

11
وَأَمَّا بِنِعْمَةِ رَبِّكَ فَحَدِّثْ
Wa amma bini'mati rabbika fahaddith
But as for the favor of your Lord, report [it].

Word by Word

وَأَمَّاwa ammaAnd as for
بِنِعْمَةِbini'matiThe favor / blessing of
رَبِّكَrabbikaYour Lord
فَحَدِّثْfahaddithThen proclaim / speak about
Simple Explanation

The final command: talk about Allah's blessings openly. Don't hide the good that Allah has done for you. "Haddith" means to speak, narrate, tell others. This includes being grateful verbally, sharing your story of how Allah helped you, and using your blessings to inspire others. Gratitude is not just a private feeling - it's meant to be expressed and shared. When you acknowledge Allah's blessings publicly, you inspire others to recognize their own blessings too.

Key Lessons from Surah Ad-Duha
  • ◆
    Allah never abandons those who turn to Him.

    When the world tells you God has forgotten you, this surah says: He never left. The pause in blessings is not rejection - it's part of His plan.

  • ◆
    Dark periods are temporary, like the night before dawn.

    Allah swears by BOTH the bright morning and the still night. Both are His creation. Both serve a purpose. The night of difficulty always gives way to the morning of relief.

  • ◆
    The Hereafter is always better than this world.

    No matter how hard this life gets, what's coming next is incomparably better. This perspective transforms how we handle every trial.

  • ◆
    Your past struggles should fuel your compassion.

    You were an orphan - so be kind to orphans. You were lost - so guide others. You were poor - so share your blessings. Our hardships are meant to make us more caring, not more bitter.

  • ◆
    Proclaim Allah's blessings openly.

    Gratitude is not just private. Share your story, acknowledge what Allah has done for you, and inspire others to see their own blessings. Silence about blessings is a form of ingratitude.
