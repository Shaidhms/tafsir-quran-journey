The Story Behind This Surah

Surah Al-Muzzammil was revealed in the early Makkan period and is one of the earliest surahs revealed to the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The name "Al-Muzzammil" means "The One Wrapped in Garments" or "The Enshrouded One," referring to the Prophet who would wrap himself in his cloak during the night. This surah establishes the foundational spiritual practice that would sustain the Prophet and his companions through the intense trials ahead: the night prayer (Qiyam al-Layl).

The surah opens with a tender yet commanding address: "O you who wraps himself [in clothing], arise [to pray] the night, except for a little." Allah is preparing His Messenger for the enormous task of carrying the Quran to humanity. Before the mission comes the training, and that training is standing before Allah in the stillness of the night, reciting His words slowly and deliberately (tarteel).

Ibn Kathir reports that the early Muslims took the night prayer so seriously that when this surah was first revealed, the companions would stand in prayer until their feet swelled. They prayed for nearly a year in this manner until verse 20 was revealed, lightening the obligation. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "The best prayer after the obligatory prayers is the night prayer." (Sahih Muslim, 1163)

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Introduction to Surah Al-Muzzammil; Sahih Muslim

The middle portion shifts to patience and reliance on Allah. The Prophet is told to be patient over the insults of the disbelievers, to leave their judgment to Allah, and to take Allah alone as his Wakeel (Disposer of affairs). The surah warns the deniers of chains, choking food, and a Day when the earth and mountains will convulse.

The final verse (verse 20) is remarkably long and represents Allah's mercy in lightening the night prayer obligation. Knowing that some believers would be ill, traveling, or fighting in His cause, Allah reduced the requirement and instead emphasized: establish the regular prayer, give zakah, and lend Allah a goodly loan through charity. Whatever good you send ahead for yourselves, you will find it with Allah, better and greater in reward.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
يَا أَيُّهَا الْمُزَّمِّلُ
Ya ayyuhal-muzzammil
O you who wraps himself [in clothing].

Word by Word

يَا أَيُّهَاya ayyuhaO you
الْمُزَّمِّلُal-muzzammilThe one who wraps himself
Simple Explanation

Allah addresses His Prophet with a gentle, intimate title. "Muzzammil" comes from "tazammala" meaning to wrap oneself in garments. The Prophet (peace be upon him) would wrap himself in his cloak at night. Ibn Kathir explains that this was one of the earliest revelations, when the Prophet was still adjusting to receiving divine communication. He would wrap himself seeking comfort and warmth. As-Sa'di notes that by addressing him this way, Allah is using a description of his state rather than his name, which conveys tenderness and closeness. It is as if Allah is saying: "You who seeks comfort in your garments, rise and seek true comfort in standing before Me."

2
قُمِ اللَّيْلَ إِلَّا قَلِيلًا
Qumil-layla illa qalila
Arise [to pray] the night, except for a little.

Word by Word

قُمِqumiArise / Stand

إِلَّاillaExcept
قَلِيلًاqalilaA little
Simple Explanation

The command to stand in prayer at night. "Qum" is an imperative verb meaning "stand up" or "arise." The night prayer (Qiyam al-Layl) is established here as one of the first commands given to the Prophet. Ibn Kathir explains that "except a little" means you do not have to pray the entire night, but most of it. As-Sa'di notes that the night was chosen because it is the time of stillness, when distractions fade and the heart can focus entirely on connecting with Allah. The night prayer was initially obligatory for the Prophet and his companions before being lightened in verse 20.

3
نِّصْفَهُ أَوِ انقُصْ مِنْهُ قَلِيلًا
Nisfahu awi-nqus minhu qalila
Half of it, or subtract from it a little.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

Allah specifies the amount: pray half the night, or a little less than half. "Nisf" means half, and "inqus" means to reduce or subtract. Ibn Kathir explains that this gives flexibility. The minimum is about a third of the night. As-Sa'di notes the wisdom here: Allah does not burden His servants beyond their capacity. He gives a range, not a rigid rule. You can pray half the night, or reduce it slightly to about a third. This flexibility is a mercy, allowing the worshiper to find their own sustainable rhythm of night worship.

4

Or increase beyond half the night, and recite the Quran with tarteel. "Zid" means add more. "Rattil" means to recite slowly, clearly, and beautifully, giving each letter its due. "Tarteel" is the noun form emphasizing this quality. Ibn Kathir explains that tarteel means to recite at a measured, deliberate pace, not rushing through the words. Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) defined tarteel as "making the letters clear and knowing where to stop." As-Sa'di says the purpose of tarteel is reflection and understanding. The Quran is not meant to be speed-read but savored. Every word carries meaning, and tarteel allows the heart to absorb it.

5
إِنَّا سَنُلْقِي عَلَيْكَ قَوْلًا ثَقِيلًا
Inna sanulqi 'alayka qawlan thaqila
Indeed, We will cast upon you a heavy word.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

This is one of the most profound descriptions of the Quran in the entire Quran itself. "Qawlan thaqila" means a heavy, weighty word. Ibn Kathir explains that the Quran is "heavy" in multiple senses: heavy in its obligations and commandments, heavy in its meaning and depth, and physically heavy when it descended upon the Prophet. When revelation came, the Prophet would sweat profusely even on cold days, and if he was riding a camel, the animal would be forced to sit down under the weight. As-Sa'di adds that the "heaviness" also refers to the gravity of its message. The Quran carries the weight of divine law, guidance for all humanity, and the distinction between truth and falsehood. The night prayer is the preparation needed to carry this weight.

6
إِنَّ نَاشِئَةَ اللَّيْلِ هِيَ أَشَدُّ وَطْئًا وَأَقْوَمُ قِيلًا
Inna nashi'atal-layli hiya ashaddu wat'an wa aqwamu qila
Indeed, the hours of the night are more effective for concurrence [of heart and tongue] and more suitable for words.

Word by Word

The night hours are the best time for worship because the heart and tongue are in harmony. "Nashi'ah" refers to the hours of the night when one rises from sleep to pray. "Ashaddu wat'an" means stronger in impact, where the heart, eyes, ears, and tongue all align together. Ibn Kathir explains that at night, there are no distractions: no visitors, no noise, no worldly business. The heart is free to focus entirely on Allah. "Aqwamu qila" means the words spoken at night are more correct and clear. As-Sa'di adds that during the day, a person is busy with worldly affairs, but at night, the soul is calm, the environment is still, and the recitation has its deepest effect on the heart. This is why the greatest scholars and worshipers throughout Islamic history treasured the night prayer above all voluntary worship.

7
إِنَّ لَكَ فِي النَّهَارِ سَبْحًا طَوِيلًا
Inna laka fin-nahari sabhan tawila
Indeed, for you by day is prolonged occupation.

Word by Word

إِنَّinnaIndeed

Simple Explanation

During the day, you are occupied with many responsibilities. "Sabhan" comes from "sabaha" which means to move about, to be engaged in activity. "Tawila" means long or extended. Ibn Kathir explains that the daytime is filled with the demands of spreading the message, dealing with people, and attending to worldly needs, so the night becomes the special time for deep worship. As-Sa'di notes the beautiful balance Allah sets here: the day is for your duties among people, and the night is for your private connection with your Lord. This teaches us that Islam does not demand we abandon the world, but rather that we balance worldly engagement with spiritual devotion.

8
وَاذْكُرِ اسْمَ رَبِّكَ وَتَبَتَّلْ إِلَيْهِ تَبْتِيلًا
Wadhkuris-ma rabbika wa tabattal ilayhi tabtila
And remember the name of your Lord and devote yourself to Him with [complete] devotion.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

Remember Allah's name constantly and devote yourself entirely to Him. "Udhkur" means to remember, mention, and keep in mind. "Tabattal" comes from "batala" meaning to cut off. It means to cut yourself off from everything else and devote yourself completely to Allah. Ibn Kathir explains that "tabattul" means to dedicate your worship, your heart, and your focus entirely to Allah, turning away from all distractions. As-Sa'di adds that this does not mean abandoning worldly responsibilities (monasticism), but rather making Allah the center of everything. Your work, your rest, your relationships should all orbit around your consciousness of Allah. True tabattul is when your heart is with Allah even while your hands are busy with the world.

9
رَّبُّ الْمَشْرِقِ وَالْمَغْرِبِ لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا هُوَ فَاتَّخِذْهُ وَكِيلًا
Rabbul-mashriqi wal-maghribi la ilaha illa Huwa fattakhidhhu Wakila
[He is] the Lord of the East and the West; there is no deity except Him, so take Him as Disposer of [your] affairs.

Word by Word

رَّبُّrabbuLord of

Simple Explanation

He is the Lord of the East and the West. There is no god but Him, so take Him as your Wakeel. "Mashriqi" (East) is where the sun rises, and "Maghribi" (West) is where it sets, meaning Allah controls everything between them, the entire world. "Wakeel" means the one you entrust your affairs to, your guardian, manager, and protector. Ibn Kathir explains the logical flow: since He alone is Lord of everything and there is no god beside Him, it only makes sense to rely on Him alone. As-Sa'di adds that tawakkul (reliance on Allah) is not passive. It means you do your best, then trust the outcome entirely to Allah. This verse is the foundation of inner peace: when you take Allah as your Wakeel, you stop carrying the weight of the world on your shoulders.

10
وَاصْبِرْ عَلَىٰ مَا يَقُولُونَ وَاهْجُرْهُمْ هَجْرًا جَمِيلًا
Wasbir 'ala ma yaquluna wahjurhum hajran jamila
And be patient over what they say and avoid them with gracious avoidance.

Word by Word

Be patient with the hurtful words of the disbelievers and distance yourself from them gracefully. "Isbir" is the command for patience (sabr). "Uhjurhum" means to leave them, distance yourself. "Hajran jamila" is a beautiful concept: gracious avoidance. Not avoidance with anger, bitterness, or revenge, but with dignity and grace. Ibn Kathir explains this was revealed when the Quraysh were mocking and insulting the Prophet. He was told not to retaliate but to maintain his composure. As-Sa'di notes that "hajr jameel" means you leave without creating a scene, without cursing them back, without harboring hatred. You simply walk away with dignity. This is one of the highest forms of character. The Prophet embodied this throughout his life in Makkah.

11
وَذَرْنِي وَالْمُكَذِّبِينَ أُولِي النَّعْمَةِ وَمَهِّلْهُمْ قَلِيلًا

Leave the matter of the wealthy deniers to Me, and give them just a little more time. "Dharnee" is a powerful word: "Leave Me alone with them," as if Allah is saying, "Step aside, I will handle them." "Mukadhdhibeen" are those who actively deny the truth. "Ulee an-na'mah" means possessors of luxury and ease. Ibn Kathir explains that their wealth and comfort made them arrogant enough to deny the truth. They felt they did not need God because they had everything. "Mahhilhum" means grant them a brief delay. As-Sa'di notes the terrifying implication: the respite is short, and what comes after it is severe. Allah's patience has a limit. Their luxury was not a sign of Allah's approval but a test, and their time was running out.

12
إِنَّ لَدَيْنَا أَنكَالًا وَجَحِيمًا
Inna ladayna ankalan wa jaheema
Indeed, with Us [for them] are shackles and burning fire.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

With Us are heavy chains and a blazing fire for them. "Ankal" is the plural of "nikl" meaning heavy iron shackles or fetters that bind the hands and feet. "Jaheem" is one of the names of Hellfire, referring to its intense blazing heat. Ibn Kathir explains that these are prepared for the wealthy deniers mentioned in the previous verse. Their luxury in this world will be replaced by chains in the next. As-Sa'di notes the sharp contrast: in this life, they enjoyed freedom and ease. In the next, they will be bound in chains and surrounded by fire. The punishment matches the crime of arrogance: those who refused to be bound by Allah's laws will be physically bound in the Hereafter.

13
وَطَعَامًا ذَا غُصَّةٍ وَعَذَابًا أَلِيمًا

And food that chokes and a painful punishment.

And food that sticks in the throat and causes choking, and a painful punishment. "Ghussah" refers to something that gets stuck in the throat and cannot be swallowed or expelled. It causes intense discomfort and choking. Ibn Kathir explains this refers to the thorny zaqqum tree and other food of Hellfire that is so repulsive it lodges in the throat. The inhabitants of Hell will be forced to eat yet unable to swallow. As-Sa'di notes the horrifying detail: in this world, these people ate the finest foods while ignoring Allah's commands. In the Hereafter, their food will be a source of torment, not nourishment. "Adhaban aleema" means a punishment that is excruciatingly painful, affecting every fiber of their being.

14

On that Day, the earth and mountains will shake violently and the mountains will crumble into loose, flowing sand. "Tarjufu" means to convulse and tremble with great violence. Ibn Kathir explains that the mighty mountains, which seem so permanent and unshakeable, will be reduced to nothing more than flowing sand dunes. This imagery would have been vivid for the Arabs who knew how easily sand dunes shift and collapse. As-Sa'di notes that if the mountains, the most solid and immovable structures on earth, are destroyed on that Day, what chance does a frail human being have? This verse shatters any illusion of worldly permanence and security.

15
إِنَّا أَرْسَلْنَا إِلَيْكُمْ رَسُولًا شَاهِدًا عَلَيْكُمْ كَمَا أَرْسَلْنَا إِلَىٰ فِرْعَوْنَ رَسُولًا

We have sent you a Messenger who will be a witness over you, just as We sent Musa (Moses) to Pharaoh. "Shahid" means witness. The question is: will you respond like Pharaoh or will you respond with faith? As-Sa'di notes that mentioning Pharaoh is deliberate because the Quraysh knew his story and knew his terrible end. The implicit message is devastating: "Do you want to end up like Pharaoh?"

16

Pharaoh disobeyed the messenger (Musa), so We destroyed him with a devastating punishment. "Asa" means to disobey and rebel. "Akhadhnahu" means We seized him, grabbed him. Ibn Kathir explains that Pharaoh's punishment was drowning in the Red Sea, one of the most dramatic and well-known destructions in history. Despite all his power, army, and wealth, he was helpless before Allah's punishment. As-Sa'di draws the lesson clearly: disobeying the messenger always leads to destruction. The Quraysh are being warned that they face the same fate if they follow Pharaoh's path of rejection and arrogance.

When even young children, who have no sins and no worries, will turn grey from sheer terror, what will happen to adults burdened with sins? As-Sa'di notes this is an unanswerable rhetorical question. There is no way to protect yourself from that Day except through faith and good deeds. Disbelief leaves you completely exposed and defenseless.

18

The sky, which seems so strong and eternal, will shatter. "Kana wa'duhu maf'ula" is a powerful closing statement: Allah's promise has always been fulfilled and will always be fulfilled. As-Sa'di adds that this phrase should give both hope to believers (Allah's promise of reward is certain) and terror to disbelievers (Allah's promise of punishment is equally certain).

Simple Explanation

This entire surah is a reminder. Whoever chooses can take a path to his Lord. "Tadhkirah" means a reminder, an admonition. "Sabeel" means a path, a way. Ibn Kathir explains that Allah has laid out the evidence, the warnings, the promises, and the guidance. Now the choice is yours. "Faman sha'a" means whoever wills. Allah does not force faith on anyone. The path to Allah is open, the guidance has been given, and the reminder has been delivered. As-Sa'di notes that this verse establishes human free will and responsibility. You cannot blame anyone else for your choices. The reminder came to you. If you choose to walk toward Allah, you will find the path. If you choose to turn away, you bear the consequences.

Indeed, your Lord knows that you stand [in prayer] almost two thirds of the night or half of it or a third of it, and [so do] a group of those with you. And Allah determines the night and the day. He has known that you will not be able to keep up with it, so He has turned to you in forgiveness. So recite what is easy [for you] of the Quran. He has known that there will be among you those who are ill and others traveling through the land seeking [something] of the bounty of Allah, and others fighting for the cause of Allah. So recite what is easy from it and establish prayer and give zakah and loan Allah a goodly loan. And whatever good you put forward for yourselves, you will find it with Allah. It is better and greater in reward. And seek forgiveness of Allah. Indeed, Allah is Forgiving and Merciful.

Simple Explanation

This is the longest verse in the surah and one of the most merciful passages in the Quran. It was revealed much later than the rest of the surah (some scholars say about a year later, during the Madinan period). Ibn Kathir narrates that after the initial command to pray most of the night, the Prophet and his companions would stand in prayer until their feet swelled. They did this for about a year, and Allah, out of His mercy, lightened the obligation with this verse.

Allah acknowledges their effort: "Your Lord knows that you stand almost two-thirds of the night, or half of it, or a third of it, and so do a group of those with you." Then comes the relief: "He knew that you would not be able to sustain it, so He has turned to you in forgiveness." The word "fataba" (He turned) shows this lightening is an act of divine mercy and forgiveness, not a concession of weakness.

The new instruction: "Recite what is easy for you of the Quran." Then Allah lists the legitimate excuses: illness, travel for livelihood, and fighting in His cause. For all these situations, the lighter obligation applies.

As-Sa'di explains the four remaining commands: (1) Establish the obligatory prayer; (2) Give zakah; (3) Loan Allah a goodly loan (charity beyond zakah); and (4) Seek forgiveness. The "goodly loan" is a beautiful metaphor: when you give charity, you are lending to Allah, and He will repay you with something far greater. The verse closes with a promise and a comfort: whatever good you send ahead, you will find it with Allah, better and greater. And seek Allah's forgiveness, for He is truly Forgiving, truly Merciful. This ending wraps the entire surah in a blanket of hope and mercy.

Key Lessons
  • 01
    The night prayer is the believer's secret weapon. Before any great mission comes great preparation. Allah prepared His Prophet for carrying the Quran to humanity through Qiyam al-Layl. The night prayer aligns the heart, tongue, and mind in a way no daytime worship can match. If you want spiritual strength, the night is where you build it.
  • 02
    Recite the Quran slowly and with reflection (tarteel). Speed-reading the Quran misses the point. Every word is heavy with meaning. Tarteel means giving each letter its due, pausing to reflect, and allowing the words to penetrate the heart. Quality of recitation matters more than quantity.
  • 03
    The Quran is a heavy word that requires preparation. Living by the Quran is not easy. It demands changes in character, habits, priorities, and relationships. The night prayer is the training ground that builds the spiritual muscle needed to carry this weight.
  • 04
    Take Allah as your Wakeel (Disposer of affairs). When you truly rely on Allah, anxiety loses its grip. He is the Lord of the East and the West. Nothing is outside His control. Do your best, then hand the results over to Him. This is the essence of tawakkul.
  • 05
    Respond to harm with gracious avoidance, not revenge. "Hajran jamila" (gracious avoidance) is a masterclass in dignity. When people attack you, you do not sink to their level. You walk away with your character intact. This is strength, not weakness.
  • 06
    Allah's mercy lightens the burden when He sees your sincere effort. The companions pushed themselves to their limits in night prayer, and Allah responded with mercy, reducing the obligation. This shows that Allah does not want to burden you. He wants to see your sincere effort, and then He makes things easier.
  • 07
    Whatever good you send ahead, you will find it with Allah, multiplied. Every charity, every prayer, every good deed is a deposit in your account with Allah. And unlike worldly investments, this one is guaranteed by the Creator of the universe to be returned better and greater.
