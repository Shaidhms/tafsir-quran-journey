The Story Behind This Surah

Surah At-Takwir was revealed in Makkah and is one of the most powerful and vivid surahs in the entire Quran. The name "At-Takwir" means "the folding up" or "the overthrowing," referring to the sun being wrapped up and extinguished on the Day of Judgment. This surah delivers a rapid-fire sequence of twelve cosmic catastrophes that will mark the end of the world, followed by a powerful affirmation of the Quran's divine origin.

The surah opens with an astonishing cascade of events: the sun folded up, stars falling, mountains set in motion, pregnant camels abandoned, wild beasts gathered, seas set ablaze. Each image is more terrifying than the last, painting the total collapse of the natural order that humans take for granted.

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Whoever would be pleased to look at the Day of Judgment as if he sees it with his own eyes, let him recite: 'When the sun is wrapped up' (Surah 81), 'When the sky breaks apart' (Surah 82), and 'When the sky has split open' (Surah 84)." This hadith is reported by At-Tirmidhi, Ahmad, and Al-Hakim, from the narration of Ibn Umar.

Source: Tafsir Ibn Kathir, Introduction to Surah At-Takwir; At-Tirmidhi, Ahmad, Al-Hakim

Among the most striking verses is the mention of the buried infant girl being asked for what sin she was killed. This refers to the pre-Islamic Arab practice of burying baby girls alive. On the Day of Judgment, these innocent victims will be given justice. The scrolls of deeds will be laid open, the sky stripped away, Hellfire set ablaze, and Paradise brought near.

The second half shifts to affirm the authenticity of the Quran and its noble carrier, Jibreel (Gabriel). The Quran is not the word of a madman or a poet. It is brought by a mighty, trusted angel to the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The surah closes with a profound question: "Where are you going?" This is nothing but a reminder for all of mankind, for whoever among you wills to take a straight path. But you cannot will except as Allah wills, the Lord of all the worlds.

Verse by Verse - With Word-by-Word Arabic

Each verse is broken down word by word. Learn the Arabic gradually by connecting each word to its meaning.

1
إِذَا الشَّمْسُ كُوِّرَتْ
Idhas-shamsu kuwwirat
When the sun is wrapped up [in darkness].

Word by Word

إِذَاidhaWhen
الشَّمْسُash-shamsuThe sun
كُوِّرَتْkuwwiratIs wrapped up / folded
Simple Explanation

The surah begins with a staggering cosmic event. "Kuwwirat" comes from "takwir" which means to wrap something up, like wrapping a turban around the head. The sun, this massive ball of fire that lights up the entire world, will be folded up and its light extinguished. Ibn Kathir explains that the sun will be thrown into darkness, losing all its light and heat. As-Sa'di notes that "idha" (when) establishes certainty. This is not a possibility but an inevitability. The very thing that all life depends on will be switched off by Allah's command.

2
وَإِذَا النُّجُومُ انكَدَرَتْ
Wa idhan-nujumu inkadarat
And when the stars fall, losing their lustre.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
النُّجُومُan-nujumuThe stars
انكَدَرَتْinkadaratFall / scatter / lose lustre
Simple Explanation

"Inkadarat" means to fall, scatter, and lose their brightness. The stars that have been fixed in the sky since creation will come crashing down. Ibn Kathir explains this means the stars will be thrown down and dispersed, falling from their orbits. As-Sa'di adds that the stars losing their lustre and falling signifies the complete collapse of the celestial order. Everything that humans looked up to as permanent and unchanging will be destroyed in an instant.

3
وَإِذَا الْجِبَالُ سُيِّرَتْ
Wa idhal-jibalu suyyirat
And when the mountains are set in motion.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الْجِبَالُal-jibaluThe mountains
سُيِّرَتْsuyyiratAre set in motion / moved
Simple Explanation

"Suyyirat" means to be set in motion, to be moved from their places. The mountains, the most massive and stable structures on earth, will be uprooted and made to move like clouds. Ibn Kathir explains that the mountains will be removed from their foundations and blown away as dust. As-Sa'di notes that mountains are mentioned because they represent permanence and stability in the human mind. If even these immovable giants are set drifting, then nothing in this world is truly permanent.

4
وَإِذَا الْعِشَارُ عُطِّلَتْ
Wa idhal-'isharu 'uttilat
And when full-term pregnant she-camels are neglected.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الْعِشَارُal-'isharuThe pregnant she-camels
عُطِّلَتْ'uttilatAre neglected / abandoned
Simple Explanation

"Al-'ishar" refers to she-camels that are ten months pregnant, about to give birth. In Arab society, this was the most prized and valuable possession a person could own. "Uttilat" means to be left unattended and neglected. Ibn Kathir explains that the terror of the Day of Judgment will be so overwhelming that people will abandon even their most precious possessions. As-Sa'di adds that the Arabs would never leave a pregnant she-camel unattended under normal circumstances. This image powerfully conveys the level of panic and terror that will grip humanity on that Day.

5
وَإِذَا الْوُحُوشُ حُشِرَتْ
Wa idhal-wuhushu hushirat
And when the wild beasts are gathered.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الْوُحُوشُal-wuhushuThe wild beasts
حُشِرَتْhushiratAre gathered together
Simple Explanation

"Hushirat" means to be gathered together, assembled. Wild animals that normally flee from each other and from humans will be gathered together in one place. Ibn Kathir explains that the wild beasts will be resurrected and gathered so that Allah may establish justice even among animals, then they will be turned to dust. As-Sa'di notes that if even animals are gathered for judgment, how much more so will human beings, who were given intelligence and free will, be held accountable?

6
وَإِذَا الْبِحَارُ سُجِّرَتْ
Wa idhal-biharu sujjirat
And when the seas are set on fire.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الْبِحَارُal-biharuThe seas
سُجِّرَتْsujjiratAre set ablaze / overflowing
Simple Explanation

"Sujjirat" has been interpreted in two ways by the scholars. Ibn Kathir mentions that it means the seas will be set on fire, blazing with flames. Ali ibn Abi Talib said it means they will be filled with fire. Other scholars, as As-Sa'di notes, say it means the seas will overflow, bursting their boundaries and merging together. Both interpretations convey the same message: the oceans, which cover most of the earth and are themselves a sign of Allah's power, will be completely transformed. Water turning to fire is the ultimate reversal of the natural order.

7
وَإِذَا النُّفُوسُ زُوِّجَتْ
Wa idhan-nufusu zuwwijat
And when the souls are paired [with their bodies].

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
النُّفُوسُan-nufusuThe souls
زُوِّجَتْzuwwijatAre paired / joined
Simple Explanation

"Zuwwijat" means to be paired or coupled together. Ibn Kathir mentions several interpretations: (1) Souls will be reunited with their bodies for resurrection, (2) People will be grouped with those similar to them, as the righteous will be with the righteous and the wicked with the wicked, and (3) Each soul will be paired with its deeds. As-Sa'di favors the meaning that people of similar types will be gathered together. The good will be with the good, and the evil with the evil. On that Day, your companions will be determined by your deeds, not your social status.

8
وَإِذَا الْمَوْءُودَةُ سُئِلَتْ
Wa idhal-maw'udatu su'ilat
And when the girl [who was] buried alive is asked.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الْمَوْءُودَةُal-maw'udatuThe girl buried alive
سُئِلَتْsu'ilatIs asked
Simple Explanation

"Al-maw'udah" is the infant girl who was buried alive. This was a barbaric practice among some pre-Islamic Arabs who buried their daughters alive out of fear of poverty or shame. "Su'ilat" means she will be asked, questioned. But notice the profound justice: the victim is asked, not the murderer. Ibn Kathir explains that asking the victim is a more devastating indictment of the killer than questioning the killer himself. As-Sa'di notes that if even the innocent victim is questioned, imagine the terror of the one who committed the crime. This verse is a powerful declaration that Allah will bring justice for every oppressed soul.

9
بِأَيِّ ذَنبٍ قُتِلَتْ
Bi-ayyi dhanbin qutilat
For what sin she was killed.

Word by Word

بِأَيِّbi-ayyiFor what / which
ذَنبٍdhanbinSin / crime
قُتِلَتْqutilatShe was killed
Simple Explanation

This verse completes the question: "For what sin was she killed?" The answer, of course, is that she had no sin at all. She was an innocent baby. The question is rhetorical and meant to condemn the perpetrators. Ibn Kathir explains that this is one of the most emotionally powerful verses in the Quran. The baby girl, who never committed any wrong, will testify on the Day of Judgment. As-Sa'di notes that this verse abolishes the entire concept of honor killing and infanticide. Islam came to declare the sanctity of every human life, especially the most vulnerable. The Quran gave baby girls a voice fourteen centuries ago.

10
وَإِذَا الصُّحُفُ نُشِرَتْ
Wa idhas-suhufu nushirat
And when the pages [of deeds] are made public.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الصُّحُفُas-suhufuThe pages / scrolls
نُشِرَتْnushiratAre spread open / made public
Simple Explanation

"As-suhuf" refers to the scrolls or records of deeds. "Nushirat" means to be spread open, unfolded, and made public for all to see. Ibn Kathir explains that every person's record of deeds, which the angels have been writing throughout their life, will be opened and displayed. Nothing will be hidden. As-Sa'di notes that every action, every word, and every intention has been recorded. On that Day, the scrolls will be laid open, and each person will read their own record. Imagine reading every single thing you ever did, good and bad, with no chance to edit or delete anything.

11
وَإِذَا السَّمَاءُ كُشِطَتْ
Wa idhas-sama'u kushitat
And when the sky is stripped away.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
السَّمَاءُas-sama'uThe sky
كُشِطَتْkushitatIs stripped away / peeled off
Simple Explanation

"Kushitat" means to peel away, strip off, or remove like skin from an animal. The sky will be peeled away and removed entirely. Ibn Kathir explains that the sky, which has served as a canopy over the earth, will be torn away and rolled up. As-Sa'di notes that "kashata" is a very strong word, implying a violent stripping away. The sky that we see as a beautiful, protective ceiling will be ripped off like a piece of cloth. This is yet another element of the total destruction of the familiar universe.

12
وَإِذَا الْجَحِيمُ سُعِّرَتْ
Wa idhal-jahimu su''irat
And when Hellfire is set ablaze.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الْجَحِيمُal-jahimuThe Hellfire
سُعِّرَتْsu''iratIs set ablaze / intensified
Simple Explanation

"Su''irat" means to be set ablaze, kindled, and intensified. Hellfire, which already exists, will be further stoked and made even more ferocious on the Day of Judgment. Ibn Kathir explains that the Hellfire will be made to blaze with an intensity beyond anything it has ever experienced before, in preparation for receiving its inhabitants. As-Sa'di notes that the fire is being prepared just as Paradise is being prepared. Both destinations are real, and both are being readied. This should motivate every person to work toward Paradise and flee from the Fire.

13
وَإِذَا الْجَنَّةُ أُزْلِفَتْ
Wa idhal-jannatu uzlifat
And when Paradise is brought near.

Word by Word

وَإِذَاwa idhaAnd when
الْجَنَّةُal-jannatuParadise
أُزْلِفَتْuzlifatIs brought near
Simple Explanation

"Uzlifat" means to be brought close, drawn near. While Hellfire is set ablaze, Paradise is brought near for the believers. Ibn Kathir explains that Paradise will be brought close to the righteous so they can see its beauty and know their reward is near. As-Sa'di adds a beautiful contrast: Hellfire is intensified (punishment increased) while Paradise is brought near (reward made accessible). The two are mentioned side by side to show that every soul will go to one of these two destinations. This concludes the twelve "when" statements (verses 1-13) that describe the events of the Day of Judgment.

14
عَلِمَتْ نَفْسٌ مَّا أَحْضَرَتْ
'Alimat nafsun ma ahdarat
A soul will [then] know what it has brought [with it].

Word by Word

عَلِمَتْ'alimatWill know
نَفْسٌnafsunA soul
مَّاmaWhat
أَحْضَرَتْahdaratIt has brought / presented
Simple Explanation

This is the answer to all twelve preceding "when" clauses. When all these catastrophic events occur, then every soul will know exactly what it has brought forward. "Ahdarat" means to present or bring forward. Your deeds are the only luggage you carry into the afterlife. Ibn Kathir explains that this is the moment of ultimate clarity. All the distractions of the world are gone, and every person sees with perfect vision what they have sent ahead. As-Sa'di notes that "nafsun" (a soul) is used in the indefinite form, meaning every single soul without exception. No one will be able to claim ignorance on that Day.

15
فَلَا أُقْسِمُ بِالْخُنَّسِ
Fala uqsimu bil-khunnas
So I swear by the retreating stars.

Word by Word

فَلَاfalaSo indeed, I swear
أُقْسِمُuqsimuI swear
بِالْخُنَّسِbil-khunnasBy the retreating stars
Simple Explanation

The surah now shifts to a series of oaths. "La uqsimu" is an emphatic form of swearing, meaning "I do indeed swear." "Al-khunnas" refers to stars (or planets) that retreat, that move backward in their apparent motion across the sky. Ibn Kathir explains that "al-khunnas" refers to the planets (such as Jupiter, Saturn, Mars, Venus, and Mercury) which appear to move forward, then retreat backward in their orbits as seen from earth. This is known today as retrograde motion. As-Sa'di notes that Allah swears by His creation to draw attention to its magnificence and to emphasize the truth of what follows.

16
الْجَوَارِ الْكُنَّسِ
Al-jawaril-kunnas
Those that run [their courses] and disappear.

Word by Word

الْجَوَارِal-jawariThose that run / flow
الْكُنَّسِal-kunnasThose that hide / disappear
Simple Explanation

"Al-jawari" means those that flow, move, or run in their courses. "Al-kunnas" means those that hide, sweep away, or disappear. Ibn Kathir explains these are the same celestial bodies mentioned in the previous verse. They run across the sky and then disappear from view, either by setting below the horizon or by being hidden in the light of the sun during the day. As-Sa'di notes that these moving, disappearing celestial bodies are signs of Allah's power. He controls their precise movements, and they obey His command perfectly. The oath by these magnificent celestial phenomena prepares the listener for the important truth about to be stated.

17
وَاللَّيْلِ إِذَا عَسْعَسَ
Wal-layli idha 'as'as
And by the night as it departs.

Word by Word

وَاللَّيْلِwal-layliAnd by the night
إِذَاidhaWhen
عَسْعَسَ'as'asDeparts / draws away
Simple Explanation

"As'as" is a unique Arabic word that can mean both "when the night comes" and "when the night departs." Ibn Kathir mentions both interpretations but notes that the stronger opinion is that it refers to the night departing, drawing away at the end. As-Sa'di explains that the transition between night and day is one of Allah's great signs. The night that covers everything in darkness gradually lifts, and the first light of dawn appears. This daily miracle is so routine that we barely notice it, yet it is a sign of Allah's absolute control over creation.

18
وَالصُّبْحِ إِذَا تَنَفَّسَ
Was-subhi idha tanaffas
And by the dawn when it breathes.

Word by Word

وَالصُّبْحِwas-subhiAnd by the morning
إِذَاidhaWhen
تَنَفَّسَtanaffasIt breathes / appears
Simple Explanation

"Tanaffas" literally means "to breathe." The dawn is described as breathing, as if the morning light is the first breath of a new day. This is one of the most beautiful metaphors in the Quran. Ibn Kathir explains that "tanaffas" refers to the light of dawn spreading gradually, like a gentle breath spreading across the sky. As-Sa'di notes that the image of the dawn breathing conveys freshness, life, and renewal. After the darkness of night, the dawn brings light and hope. These oaths by the retreating stars, the departing night, and the breathing dawn are preparing the listener for a critical truth about the Quran.

19
إِنَّهُ لَقَوْلُ رَسُولٍ كَرِيمٍ
Innahu laqawlu rasulin karim
Indeed, it [the Quran] is the word of a noble messenger.

Simple Explanation

This is the truth that all the previous oaths were building up to: the Quran is the word brought by a noble messenger. Ibn Kathir explains that this "noble messenger" is Jibreel (Gabriel), the angel who delivered the Quran from Allah to Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The word "karim" means noble, honorable, and generous. Jibreel is described as noble because of his high rank among the angels and his trustworthiness. As-Sa'di notes that calling the Quran "the word of a messenger" means it is delivered by a messenger; the actual source is Allah. The messenger (Jibreel) is the carrier, not the author.

Yet despite his power, he has a secure, honored position before Allah. As-Sa'di notes that these qualities of Jibreel serve to validate the Quran. The one who brought this message is not a weak or insignificant being, but the most powerful and most trusted of all angels.

21
مُّطَاعٍ ثَمَّ أَمِينٍ
Muta'in thamma amin
Obeyed there [in the heavens] and trustworthy.

Word by Word

Jibreel is "muta'" (obeyed) among the angels in the heavens and "amin" (trustworthy). Ibn Kathir explains that Jibreel commands the other angels and they obey him, because Allah has given him that authority. And he is "amin," the most trustworthy being in existence when it comes to delivering Allah's message. He never adds to it, never subtracts from it, and never changes it. As-Sa'di emphasizes that "amin" is the ultimate credential. The Quran was delivered by someone who is absolutely incapable of tampering with the message. This directly refutes anyone who claims the Quran has been altered or corrupted.

22
وَمَا صَاحِبُكُم بِمَجْنُونٍ
Wa ma sahibukum bimajnun
And your companion [Muhammad] is not [at all] mad.

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

Now the surah defends the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The Quraysh called him a madman, a poet, a sorcerer. Allah responds: "Your companion is not mad." The word "sahibukum" (your companion) is significant. Ibn Kathir explains that calling him "your companion" reminds the Quraysh that they know Muhammad personally. He lived among them for 40 years before prophethood. They knew his character, his honesty, his intelligence. How could they now call him mad? As-Sa'di notes that "sahib" implies intimacy and familiarity. You know this man. You have seen his behavior for decades. He is the most sane, rational, and truthful person among you.

23
وَلَقَدْ رَآهُ بِالْأُفُقِ الْمُبِينِ

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) saw Jibreel in his true angelic form on the clear horizon. Ibn Kathir explains that this refers to the occasion when the Prophet saw Jibreel in his original form, with 600 wings, filling the entire horizon from east to west. This happened twice: once at the beginning of revelation and once during the Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj). As-Sa'di notes that this verse serves as proof. The Prophet did not just hear a voice; he saw the angel with his own eyes on the open, clear horizon. This was a real, visible experience, not a hallucination or a dream.

24
وَمَا هُوَ عَلَى الْغَيْبِ بِضَنِينٍ

"Danin" means stingy, withholding. The Prophet (peace be upon him) does not withhold any of the revelation he receives. He delivers everything faithfully. Ibn Kathir explains that some recitations read "zanin" (with a "z") meaning "suspicious" or "accused," and others read "danin" (with a "d") meaning "stingy." Both are authentic recitations. Under the first reading: he is not someone to be suspected regarding the unseen revelation. Under the second: he does not withhold any of it. As-Sa'di notes that both meanings affirm the Prophet's complete honesty and transparency. He delivered every single verse, even those that were personally difficult for him.

25

The Quran is brought by Jibreel, the noblest angel, not by a cursed devil. As-Sa'di adds that the devils have no access to this revelation. They are expelled from the heavens and cannot approach it. The chain of transmission is pure: from Allah to Jibreel to Muhammad (peace be upon him). There is no room for Shaytan anywhere in this chain.

26
فَأَيْنَ تَذْهَبُونَ

So where are you going?

Word by Word

Simple Explanation

One of the most powerful rhetorical questions in the entire Quran. "So where are you going?" After all these proofs, after the description of the Day of Judgment, after the credentials of the messenger and the authenticity of the message, where else could you possibly go? Ibn Kathir explains that this is a rebuke to those who turn away from the Quran. Where will you go? What other guidance will you follow? What other path will save you? As-Sa'di notes that this question is meant to shake the listener awake. You are running away from the truth, but where are you running to? There is no escape from Allah and no alternative to His guidance.

لِّلْعَالَمِينَlil-'alaminFor all the worlds / mankind
Simple Explanation

The Quran is described as a "dhikr" (reminder) for all of creation. "Lil-'alamin" means for all the worlds, all of humanity and jinn. Ibn Kathir explains that the Quran is not limited to the Arabs, nor to a specific time period. It is a universal reminder for every human being until the Day of Judgment. As-Sa'di notes that calling it a "reminder" implies that the truth is already within the human nature (fitrah). The Quran does not introduce something alien; it reminds you of what your soul already knows. You were created to worship Allah, and the Quran reminds you of this purpose.

28
لِمَن شَاءَ مِنكُمْ أَن يَسْتَقِيمَ
Liman sha'a minkum an yastaqim
For whoever wills among you to take a straight course.

أَن يَسْتَقِيمَan yastaqimTo take a straight course
Simple Explanation

"Yastaqim" means to be upright, to walk the straight path. The Quran is a reminder for those who want to walk straight. Ibn Kathir explains that this verse establishes human free will: "whoever wills among you." You have the choice to accept or reject guidance. As-Sa'di notes that "istiqamah" (uprightness) is the essence of Islam. It means to walk on the straight path of Allah's commands and prohibitions without deviating to the right or left. The Quran offers you this path, but the choice is yours. This verse emphasizes personal responsibility and agency.

29
وَمَا تَشَاءُونَ إِلَّا أَن يَشَاءَ اللَّهُ رَبُّ الْعَالَمِينَ
Wa ma tasha'una illa an yasha'allahu rabbul-'alamin
And you do not will except that Allah wills, Lord of the worlds.

Simple Explanation

The surah closes with the ultimate statement of Divine sovereignty. You have free will, but your will operates within Allah's will. "Rabbul-'alamin" (Lord of all the worlds) reminds us that Allah is the Master of everything. Ibn Kathir explains that when verse 28 was revealed affirming human choice, some people said "it is up to us then." So verse 29 was revealed to clarify: yes, you choose, but your choice itself is under Allah's permission. As-Sa'di explains this beautifully: Allah gave you the ability to choose, but He is the one who gave you that ability. Your free will is a gift from Allah, not independent of Him. This is the balanced Islamic understanding: humans have real choices and real responsibility, but nothing happens outside of Allah's ultimate will and knowledge. The surah ends reminding us that Allah is "Rabbul-'alamin," the Lord of all creation, whose authority is absolute and whose wisdom encompasses everything.

Key Lessons
  • 01
    Nothing in this world is permanent. The sun, stars, mountains, and seas will all be destroyed. If these colossal creations will end, how can we attach our hearts to anything in this temporary world? The only thing that lasts is your relationship with Allah and your deeds.
  • 02
    Allah will bring justice for every oppressed soul. The buried infant girl will be asked for what sin she was killed. No act of oppression will go unaccounted for. If you have been wronged, know that your Day of justice is coming. And if you have wronged others, repent before that Day arrives.
  • 03
    Your deeds are your only luggage. "A soul will know what it has brought." On the Day of Judgment, you will not be asked about your wealth, status, or appearance. You will only be shown what you sent forward. Invest in deeds that will benefit you when everything else is gone.
  • 04
    The Quran is a divine revelation, not human speech. It was delivered by the most powerful and trustworthy angel (Jibreel) to the most honest and noble human being (Muhammad, peace be upon him). The chain of transmission is impeccable and beyond any doubt.
  • 05
    "Where are you going?" is a question for every generation. When you chase distractions, pleasures, and worldly ambitions while ignoring the Quran, ask yourself: where am I going? What is the destination of this path? The Quran offers the only path to a good ending.
  • 06
    Free will and divine will are not contradictions. You have the ability to choose, and Allah holds ultimate authority over all things. This balance means you are responsible for your choices, but you should always ask Allah for guidance, because even the ability to choose good comes from Him.
